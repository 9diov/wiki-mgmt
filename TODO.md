## Books to Add

- [x] **The Phoenix Project** — Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, George Spender (2013). IT operations novel modelled after The Goal; introduces the Three Ways (flow, feedback, continuous learning).
- [ ] **Accelerate** — Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, Gene Kim (2018). Research-based; defines the four DORA metrics for software delivery performance and the organizational capabilities that predict them.
- [ ] **Team Topologies** — Matthew Skelton, Manuel Pais (2019). Four team types and three interaction modes; uses cognitive load as the primary org design constraint.
- [ ] **The Toyota Way** — Jeffrey Liker (2004, revised 2021). The most comprehensive English-language treatment of TPS as a management system. 14 principles across four categories (philosophy, process, people, problem-solving). Fills the gap between Ohno's practitioner memoir and modern application.
- [ ] **Toyota Kata** — Mike Rother (2009). The source for the Improvement Kata and Coaching Kata that Erik cites in TPP. How Toyota builds continuous improvement as a daily practice habit, not a project. Directly extends the Third Way and the vault's synthesis on training and agentic AI.
- [ ] **Out of the Crisis** — W. Edwards Deming (1982). The upstream intellectual source for most of what Goldratt, Ohno, and the DevOps movement built on. Deming's 14 Points and System of Profound Knowledge — systems thinking, variation, theory of knowledge, psychology — applied to management. Dense but foundational.
- [ ] **The DevOps Handbook (2nd ed.)** — Gene Kim, Jez Humble, Patrick Debois, John Willis, Nicole Forsgren (2021). Operational playbook for implementing flow, feedback, and continual learning at scale; strongest practical bridge from TPP + ACC to day-to-day engineering process design.
- [ ] **Site Reliability Engineering** — Betsy Beyer, Chris Jones, Jennifer Petoff, Niall Richard Murphy (eds.) (2016). Reliability operating model (SLOs/error budgets, incident response, toil reduction, postmortems) that directly supports the vault's work on measurement systems and stopping work to fix problems.
- [ ] **The Principles of Product Development Flow** — Donald G. Reinertsen (2009). The queueing/economic foundation for WIP limits, batch-size reduction, and flow efficiency in product/software development; deepens synthesis around limiting steps and local optimization traps.
