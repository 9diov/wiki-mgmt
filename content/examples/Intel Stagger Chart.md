# Intel Incoming Orders Stagger Chart

Intel tracks forecasted incoming order rates for a division using a stagger chart: each month's forecast is recorded and displayed alongside all prior forecasts for the same future periods.

## How It Works
Each month, a new forecast is added for the coming months. The chart shows not just the current outlook but how that outlook has changed from month to month. When the actual number arrives, it's marked with an asterisk.

## What It Reveals
- A forecast that keeps getting revised downward signals deteriorating business conditions before they show up in actuals.
- A forecast that keeps improving signals an accelerating business.
- The *rate of change* between successive forecasts is often more informative than any single forecast.

## Why It Creates Accountability
Forecasters know their predictions will be displayed alongside future updates and eventual actuals. Overconfident or lazy forecasting becomes visible. The discipline of knowing you'll be compared to yourself next month improves the quality of the forecast.

Grove writes: "I would go as far as to say that it's too bad that all economists and investment advisers aren't obliged to display their forecasts in a stagger chart form."

## What It Illustrates
The stagger chart is the most powerful form of **trend indicator** because it makes the *trend of the forecast itself* visible—not just the trend of the underlying data. Applies to any domain where rolling forecasts are made: demand, hiring, project milestones, revenue.
