# Superficial Review Rewrite

One of Grove's subordinates wrote a performance review that Grove considered superficial—lacking analysis and depth. Grove asked him to rewrite it.

## What Happened

After some discussion, the subordinate agreed the review was superficial, but considered the issue too minor to be worth the time to redo. After more spirited back-and-forth, they reached a deadlock.

Grove finally said: "Look, I understand you don't think it's worth your time. But I *want* you to do it. The integrity of the performance review system is just more important to me than it is to you. That is why I have to insist."

The subordinate was unconvinced and resented being made to spend time on something he considered unimportant. But he committed to rewrite it—and did it well. His own subordinate received a thorough, thoughtful review.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Conflict Resolution Stages]]**: The subordinate never moved past disagreement but did reach *commitment*—an acceptable resolution. Grove's realization: insisting on agreement (when the subordinate had already committed) was about Grove's own psychological comfort, not the operational need.

**[[Performance Appraisal ⭐]]**: The goal of a performance confrontation is a commitment to action, not emotional agreement. A subordinate can disagree with an assessment and still follow through on a corrective course. When commitment is secured, the supervisor should stop pressing for agreement and monitor performance against the commitment instead.
