# Intel Plant Security Dual Reporting

At a staff meeting early in Intel's history, the team wrestled with a structural question: who should security personnel at the company's new outlying plants report to?

## The Dilemma

**Option A — Report to the plant manager**: The plant manager can observe daily performance—whether officers show up, arrive on time, perform their duties. But plant managers are typically engineers or manufacturing people with little knowledge of or interest in security practices.

**Option B — Report to the corporate security manager**: He has the expertise, set the standards, and hired the people. But he works at headquarters and has no visibility into what's happening day-to-day at remote locations.

Neither option alone covered both what was needed.

## The Solution

After wrestling with the question, the team concluded that security personnel should report *jointly* to both: the corporate security manager specifies how the job should be done; the local plant manager monitors how it is actually being performed each day. The initial reaction—"a person has to have a boss, so who's in charge?"—was overcome with a tentative "yes, an employee can have two bosses."

## What It Illustrates

The founding moment of Intel's **[[Dual Reporting]]** culture. The case makes the principle concrete: two supervisors covering two distinct dimensions—standards (functional) and daily performance (operational)—that neither could cover alone. The discomfort with ambiguity ("who's really in charge?") is acknowledged and overridden by the practical need.
