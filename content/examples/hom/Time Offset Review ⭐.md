# Time Offset Review

One of Grove's direct reports had an outstanding year on every output measure: sales increased, margins were good, products worked. Despite some internal warning signs—higher-than-normal turnover, grumbling subordinates—Grove gave the manager a very positive review.

## What Happened

The following year, the organization collapsed. Sales growth disappeared, profitability declined, product development slipped, and subordinate turmoil deepened.

On reflection, Grove concluded that the excellent output had been produced by work done *years earlier*—like light from a distant star. The output indicators at review time were lagging far behind the work that had actually generated them. The manager's current performance had in fact been poor; the internal warning signs were the accurate signal.

Grove gave the superior rating when he should have trusted the internal measures and had the courage to rate the manager lower—despite the excellent lagging indicators.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Performance Appraisal ⭐]]**: The time offset between a manager's work and its output can be as long as a year or more. Supervisors must judge the work *performed during the review period*, not just read the output indicators, which may reflect activity from much earlier. When internal measures and output measures conflict, internal measures are often the more current signal and should be trusted even when they are harder to defend.
