# Grove Learning from Subordinates

When Intel was young, Grove was expected to supervise both engineering and manufacturing—but his background was in semiconductor device research. He knew little about memory device design or manufacturing techniques.

## What Happened

Two of his subordinates agreed to give him private lessons on memory design and manufacturing. These were scheduled appointments. The subordinates prepared for each session; Grove attended as the student, taking notes throughout.

## What It Illustrates

The one-on-one doesn't only run in one direction. Here the formal supervisor was the learner and the subordinates were the teachers. This became the origin of Intel's one-on-one culture: the tone of mutual teaching established in these early sessions persisted and scaled as the company grew.

It also illustrates that a manager's information-gathering obligation is real—Grove didn't pretend to know things he didn't. He structured regular sessions to close the gap, which made him a more effective supervisor than if he had relied on authority alone.
