# The Depressed Manager

After completing Intel's annual planning process, a division manager realizes that despite successful cost reductions in the prior year, his division will still not turn a profit in the coming year.

## What Happened
The manager became depressed. He didn't realize he was broadcasting it, but his mood began to affect the people around him immediately. Depression spread through his organization. It took someone on his own staff pulling him aside—telling him directly what he was doing to the team—before he snapped out of it.

## Why It's a Leverage Problem
The manager's emotional state became a signal that propagated across his entire organization. No single action caused it; no memo was sent. Yet output suffered across the whole group.

Unlike a poor training session (which can be corrected by retraining), the damage from depression and low morale is:
- **Pervasive**: it spreads laterally and downward through informal channels
- **Elusive**: there's no clear event to diagnose or reverse
- **Cumulative**: the longer it persists, the harder it is to undo

This is **negative leverage with virtually unlimited reach**—a small persistent signal that multiplies into large organizational effects.

## What It Illustrates
Managers are role models whether they intend to be or not. Visible behavior—including emotional state—carries managerial weight. How a manager handles bad news is itself a high-leverage act.
