# Grove's Day Schedule

Grove walks through an actual full day of his work as Intel's president, cataloguing each activity and labeling its type.

## The Day (Summary)

| Time | Activity | Type |
|---|---|---|
| 8:00 | Met with manager about to resign | Information-gathering, nudge, decision |
| 8:00 | Call from competitor (feeling out business conditions) | Information-gathering |
| 8:30 | Read afternoon mail, scribbled responses | Information-gathering, nudging, decision |
| 9:00–12:00 | Executive Staff Meeting: orders review, planning priorities, marketing program review, cycle-time program review | Information-gathering, decision-making, nudging |
| 12:00 | Lunch—training org complained about senior manager participation at foreign sites | Information-gathering |
| 1:00 | Product quality problem meeting | Information-gathering, decision |
| 2:00–4:00 | Lecture at employee orientation | Information-giving, role model |
| 4:00 | Returning phone calls | Decisions |
| 4:45 | Met with assistant on schedule requests | Decisions |
| 5:00–6:15 | Read day's mail and progress reports | Information-gathering, nudging, decisions |

~25 separate activities. About two-thirds of the time spent in meetings.

## Key Observations

**Meetings are a medium, not an activity.** You perform managerial work (information-gathering, giving, deciding, nudging) *inside* meetings. Meetings aren't the problem; misusing them is.

**Most valuable information is verbal and informal.** The lunch conversation with the training org—unplanned, casual—surfaced a problem Grove didn't know existed and wouldn't have found through any report.

**A manager is never done.** The day ends when Grove is tired, not when the work is finished. "Like a housewife's, a manager's work is never done."

**Nudging is ubiquitous.** For every formal decision, there are many more nudges: a note on a memo, a comment during a presentation, a suggestion after lunch.
