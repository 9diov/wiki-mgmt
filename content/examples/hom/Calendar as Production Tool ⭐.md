# Calendar as a Production Planning Tool

Grove applies factory scheduling principles to time management.

## The Problem with Passive Calendar Use
Most managers treat their calendar as an order book: someone requests a meeting, it gets added. The manager reacts. This is "mindless passivity"—the calendar fills with other people's priorities, leaving no structured time for the manager's own high-leverage work.

## The Production Approach

**Identify your limiting step**: What in your day is truly immovable? For Grove, it's the class he teaches—over 200 students are expecting him at a fixed time. Everything else gets scheduled around that constraint, just as breakfast production is planned around the egg.

**Batch similar tasks**: Set-up time is real in managerial work. Reading a stack of performance reviews together requires one mental context switch, not many. Preparing one set of training materials and reusing it across groups multiplies output per hour of preparation.

**Build in slack**: A freeway runs optimally below capacity—add a few too many cars and everything stops. A manager's schedule works the same way. Leave gaps so that one unexpected phone call doesn't cascade into a ruined afternoon.

**Carry a project inventory**: Keep a supply of discretionary, non-urgent projects. When unexpected free time opens up, work on these rather than wandering into subordinates' work. Without this inventory, free time turns into meddling.

**Say no at the outset**: Accepting work beyond capacity means failing to deliver later—at a higher-value stage. Better to decline early, explicitly, than to implicitly say no by not delivering.
