# Columbus MBO

Grove uses the story of Columbus's 1492 voyage—with admitted liberties taken—as a teaching example for how the MBO nesting hierarchy works, and where mechanical application of MBO breaks down.

## The Nesting Hierarchy

**Queen Isabella's objective**: Increase Spain's wealth (to fund the war against the Moors)
**Columbus's objective**: Find a new trade route to the Orient
**Columbus's key results**: Obtain ships, train crews, conduct a shakedown cruise, set sail—each with specific deadlines

Isabella's objective was served by Columbus's objective: if he found the trade route, Spain would gain new wealth. Columbus's objective was supported by his key results: completing each step on schedule would get him to the goal.

## The Twist

Columbus completed his key results. He did not find a trade route to China. He found the New World instead—a source of incalculable wealth for Spain.

By strict MBO terms, Columbus failed his objective. Isabella's objective was exceeded beyond all expectation. By any reasonable judgment, Columbus performed well.

## What It Illustrates

Two lessons about **[[Management by Objectives ⭐]]**:
1. **The nesting hierarchy works**: subordinate objectives, when met, should advance supervisor objectives—and this structure lets each level focus on their own piece without needing to manage the whole picture.
2. **MBO is a pacing tool, not a contract**: a supervisor who mechanically rates Columbus a failure for not finding China has misused the system. Key results are progress checkpoints; objectives are directional, not absolute. Judgment and common sense must govern how results are interpreted.
