# The Pencil Delegation

Grove describes a scenario: "I am your supervisor, and I walk over to you with pencil in hand and tell you to take it. You reach for the pencil, but I won't let go. So I say, 'What is wrong with you? Why can't I delegate the pencil to you?'"

## The Point
We all hold on to certain tasks because we enjoy them or feel comfortable with them—not because delegation would be wrong. This is human. It's acceptable *if it's a conscious choice*: "I'm keeping this task because I want to, and I know I could delegate it."

What's not acceptable is the **charade of insincere delegation**: going through the motions of handing something off while actually retaining control. This produces large negative leverage—subordinates learn they don't really own the work, and they stop investing in it.

## The Counterintuitive Rule
When you have a choice, delegate tasks you know **well**, not tasks you know poorly. Familiarity makes it easier to monitor the work intelligently—to know when it's on track, to ask the right questions, to catch problems at a [[Lowest Value Stage]].

This will feel wrong emotionally (you naturally want to keep doing things you're good at), but it's the right production-planning choice.
