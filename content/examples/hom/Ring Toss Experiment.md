# Ring Toss Experiment

A psychology experiment in which volunteers were placed in a room with pegs set at various distances on the floor and given rings—without any instructions. Each person eventually began tossing rings at the pegs.

## Three Behavioral Types

**Gamblers**: Stood far from the pegs, made long difficult throws. High risk, but no influence over the outcome—success was mostly chance.

**Conservatives**: Stood directly over the pegs and dropped rings onto them. Zero risk, guaranteed success.

**Achievers**: Walked to exactly the distance at which tossing the ring was a genuine challenge—difficult enough to miss several times, but not so difficult that success was impossible. They spontaneously positioned themselves at the boundary of their capability.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Maslow's Hierarchy|Achievement-driven self-actualization]]**: some people spontaneously and without prompting seek out the edge of their capability. They are not satisfied by guaranteed success (conservatives) or random outcomes (gamblers)—they need to test themselves in a way where skill is the determinant. Missing occasionally is acceptable; that's what boundary-testing looks like.

The experiment reveals a key property of achievement orientation: it is *self-initiated*. No instruction was given; no reward was offered. The achievers chose the hardest productive challenge on their own. When managers create **[[Competitive Environment|competitive environments]]** that put people at this boundary, they are creating the conditions for this behavior to emerge at work.
