# Cindy Process Engineering Plan

Cindy, an Intel manufacturing process engineer, is responsible for qualifying new processes and production tools before they enter production. She works as an internal consultant coordinating between development engineers (who resist documentation requirements) and production engineers (who demand them).

## Environment Mapping

Cindy maps her environment explicitly before planning:
- **Customers**: the production area—needs new processes and tools to work reliably on time
- **Vendors**: development engineers, product engineers, production engineering
- **Key tension**: development engineers prioritize speed; production engineers prioritize completeness; both groups affect Cindy's output

**Problem identified**: development engineers routinely delivered incomplete data, which wasn't a priority for them but blocked Cindy's qualification work.

## The Plan

Cindy's strategy—what she needed to do to close the gap between current state and required future state:
1. Specified exactly what steps had to be completed before any new process or tool could be accepted into production
2. Used **time offsets** (working backward from required deployment dates) to determine when each step had to be completed
3. Negotiated with the development engineering manager to get explicit, mutual agreement on her detailed schedule—creating shared accountability for dates
4. Set up weekly monitoring of each "vendor's" performance against the agreed schedule
5. Published performance results to create motivation to meet key dates (indicator function) and to surface problems early (window into the production process)

## What It Illustrates

Applying the **[[Planning Process]]** to a coordination role: defining your environment in terms of customers, vendors, and competing influences; working backward from future requirements using time offsets; and using published [[Indicators ⭐|indicators]] to hold upstream contributors accountable.
