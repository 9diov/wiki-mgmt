# Criminal Justice System as a Production Process

Grove applies production analysis to the criminal justice system, treating it as a flow aimed at putting convicted criminals in jail.

## The Flow
Crime reported → police investigation → arrest → indictment → trial → conviction → sentencing/appeals → jail

At each stage, a large fraction of cases drop out—lack of evidence, acquittals, suspended sentences, overturned convictions. Only a tiny percentage of accused individuals end up incarcerated.

## The Cost Calculation
When the total cost of the entire system is assigned only to those who actually reach the final stage (jail), the cost per conviction exceeds **$1 million**. A jail cell costs ~$80,000 to build and $10–20,000/year to operate.

## The Production Failure
Prisons are overcrowded. Many convicted criminals serve shortened sentences or none at all because cells are unavailable. Society has invested over $1 million per conviction—then fails to use it because the output stage (a jail cell) is too scarce.

**The wrong step is limiting the process.** The limiting step should be obtaining a conviction (the most expensive, hardest-to-achieve step). Instead, the system is constrained by jail-cell availability—a far cheaper resource. This violates the core production principle: design the system around the right limiting step.

## What It Illustrates
Misidentifying the limiting step wastes the entire upstream investment. The same logic applies wherever a cheap downstream resource prevents utilization of an expensive upstream one.
