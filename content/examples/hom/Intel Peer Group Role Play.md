# Intel Peer Group Role Play

At Intel's first management training session, a group of organizational equals was seated around a table to tackle a live business problem. The chairman—one level above the group—was sent out of the room so he couldn't hear what would unfold.

## What Happened

For fifteen minutes, the group went in circles. Observers watching from the audience couldn't believe what they were seeing—or not seeing. The managers produced motion without progress, and none of them noticed. When the chairman was brought back in, he sat down, listened briefly, leaned forward trying to make sense of it, then slapped the table: "What's going on here? You people are talking in circles and getting nowhere." After his intervention, the issue was resolved in short order.

## What It Illustrates

The founding demonstration of the **[[Peer Group Syndrome]]**: capable people, without a clear authority figure, reverted to waiting for consensus rather than advocating positions. Grove named this the *peer-plus-one* approach—adding a senior person not because they are the most expert, but because their presence gives the group the confidence to commit and, if needed, to make the call.
