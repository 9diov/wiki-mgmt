# Cindy Two-Plane Organization

Cindy, the Intel manufacturing process engineer introduced in earlier chapters, operates simultaneously on two distinct organizational planes—each with its own hierarchy.

## The Two Planes

**Operating plane (80% of her time)**
Cindy works in her production plant, manipulating manufacturing equipment, watching process monitors, making adjustments. Her reporting chain: Cindy → supervising engineer → plant engineering manager. Clear, crisp, single-supervisor.

**Coordinating plane (20% of her time)**
Cindy meets monthly with her counterparts from other Intel production plants to identify, discuss, and solve problems common to the process they each run. This coordinating group standardizes procedures across all plants. Her supervisor here is the group's chairman. The group itself is overseen by an Engineering Managers Council composed of engineering managers from all plants.

## No Conflict, Doubled Leverage

Cindy's name appears on two separate org charts. This is no different from belonging to both a company and a church—separate planes, separate hierarchies, no inherent conflict. Each role has distinct purposes and distinct supervisors.

The leverage multiplication is significant: on the operating plane, Cindy's expertise affects one plant. On the coordinating plane, the standards she helps set affect *all* plants. The two-plane structure gives her know-how organization-wide reach without changing her primary job.

## What It Illustrates

The **[[Two-Plane Organization]]** in its clearest form: an operating plane with a direct reporting chain, and a coordinating plane with a separate hierarchy and purpose. Also demonstrates how coordinating groups multiply the leverage of know-how managers like Cindy beyond their single unit.
