# Mr. Clean Inspections

Intel managers are asked to conduct "Mr. Clean" inspections: visits to parts of the company they wouldn't normally go to, where they examine housekeeping, lab arrangement, safety equipment, and general organization.

## The Problem It Solves
Managers underutilize walk-arounds because it feels awkward to wander through an area without a specific task in mind. The social dynamics of an in-office meeting impose a 30-minute overhead on what might be a 2-minute conversation. Walking through an area eliminates that overhead—a quick exchange covers the issue and you move on.

## How Mr. Clean Works
The inspection gives managers a formal task (evaluate housekeeping and safety), which legitimizes the visit. But the real value is the hour spent browsing firsthand—the ad hoc conversations, the things spotted that wouldn't appear in any report, the two-minute concerns handled on the spot.

## What It Illustrates
**Information-gathering through visits is highly efficient**—both for the manager initiating conversation and for subordinates who can raise things informally. The formal pretext (cleanliness inspection) solves the social awkwardness that prevents managers from walking around without an agenda.

The underlying principle: direct observation produces information that filtered, written channels cannot. A thermometer in the egg-boiler beats learning about temperature problems from a customer complaint.
