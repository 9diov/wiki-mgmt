# Army Encampment TRM

A sergeant commands an army encampment where nothing much happens. He knows each of his soldiers well. Routines are established. He rarely has to tell anyone what to do—appropriate to the high TRM of the group for peacetime camp operations, he contents himself with monitoring.

## Phase 1: Sudden Change

One day, a jeepload of enemy soldiers appears over the hill, shooting. Instantly, the sergeant shifts to a fully structured, task-oriented leadership style—barking orders at everyone: what to do, when, how. The high-TRM monitoring style that suited peacetime operations is abandoned without hesitation.

## Phase 2: Recovery

After weeks and months of similar skirmishes, combat becomes the new routine. The group's TRM for *fighting*—not just camp operations—has grown. The sergeant can gradually ease off the what-when-how instructions. The monitoring style becomes appropriate again, now for a different, harder task.

## What It Illustrates

Two properties of **[[Task Relevant Maturity ⭐]]** in one story:

1. **TRM drops when the situation changes**: High TRM for peacetime routine does not transfer to a new, unfamiliar task (combat). The appropriate response is an immediate reversion to a structured style—not a gradual one.

2. **TRM can be built in a new domain**: After enough shared experience with the new task, TRM grows and the management style can relax again. The progression is not permanent—it resets for each new task.

Also illustrates the practical necessity of the structured management style: a manager who has internalized the idea that telling people what to do is "not enlightened" will hesitate at exactly the wrong moment. Matching style to TRM means being willing to shift decisively when circumstances demand it.
