# Intel Ion Implanter Failure

An ion implanter in an Intel silicon fabrication plant drifted slightly out of tune. The machine operator was relatively new—trained in basic operation but not in recognizing the signs of an out-of-tune condition.

## What Happened

Unaware that anything was wrong, the operator continued running wafers through the machine under incorrect conditions. By the time the problem was discovered, nearly a full day's worth of almost-completely processed silicon wafers had passed through. Material worth more than $1 million had to be scrapped.

Because it takes over two weeks to manufacture fresh wafers to replace the loss, customer deliveries slipped—compounding the damage beyond the direct material cost.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Training ⭐]]**: The operator's failure was not a failure of basic competence—she was doing exactly what she had been trained to do. The failure was a gap in her training: nobody had taught her what a malfunctioning machine looked like. In high-stakes, high-complexity environments, the cost of incomplete training is not proportional to the gap—it can be catastrophic. One missing skill in one employee cost over $1M in materials and weeks of delivery delays.
