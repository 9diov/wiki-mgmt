# Inventory as Asset

## The Situation

After the plant's turnaround, the official audit reported a 12.8% profit improvement — below the internally-estimated 17%, and below the 15% Rogo had promised. The gap was attributed to Lou's cost-base change. It was embarrassing but manageable.

Then Lou discovers the real number. The plant's actual net profit improvement was **over 20%** in each of the last three months.

## The Accounting Distortion

Here is why the books showed less:

Inventory is reported on the balance sheet as an **asset**, valued at production cost (raw materials + value-added labor).

Over the previous months, the plant drained approximately 50% of WIP and 25% of finished goods — a deliberate, correct action enabled by DBR and batch-size reductions. Less inventory = less cash tied up = faster response to orders.

But on the books: *reducing inventory reduces assets*. And reducing assets without a corresponding reduction in liabilities shows up as a **loss**. The cash saved by not purchasing replacement materials partially offset this, but not enough. The books recorded the inventory drain as a net decrease in value.

Lou's formulation: *"We were penalized for doing the right thing. Reducing the excess inventory was interpreted by our books as a loss."*

Once inventory stabilized at its new lower level, this artificial drag disappeared — which is why Lou projected 20%+ profits going forward even with slightly declining incoming orders.

## The Perverse Incentive

This accounting treatment creates a systematic incentive to **hold excess inventory**:

- Building finished goods increases assets → improves reported profits
- Reducing finished goods decreases assets → worsens reported profits

Companies in slow periods frequently build finished goods inventory to "generate profits" — which are entirely fictional, since no product has actually been sold. The cash is consumed, not created. But the income statement looks better.

At the same time, the right action — draining excess inventory and freeing cash — looks destructive. Every period during the drain, the company reports lower profits than it actually earned in economic terms.

## Why It Matters Beyond Accounting

This wasn't just a numbers problem. In other divisions of UniCo, the same distortion was actively preventing decisions:

- Engineering had developed new products, but management refused to launch them — because launching new models would force write-offs of the old finished goods inventory sitting on shelves. The write-offs would hit the bottom line. So they kept selling outdated products, losing market share, to avoid "destroying assets" that were already economically worthless.

The measurement wasn't neutral. It was making the wrong decision feel like the right one.

## The Principle

Inventory is a **liability** — it represents cash that has been consumed, tied up, and is at risk of becoming worthless. Reporting it as an asset inverts the incentive structure and causes exactly the behavior that TOC says to avoid: building excess WIP and finished goods to "look efficient" rather than reducing them to improve flow.

The correct measurement system would treat inventory reduction as an improvement, not a loss.
