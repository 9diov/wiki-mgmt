# The Djangler Order

## The Situation

Johnny Jons, the division's marketing director, has been negotiating with a French distributor (Djangler) for months. They agreed on every technical detail across almost the entire product range. Then, right at the end, Djangler sent a counteroffer — prices far below what Jons expected.

For Model 12:
- Standard list price: $992
- Burnside price (large domestic client): $827
- Djangler's offer: **$701**

Jons killed the deal. The product costs more than $701 to make. You don't sell below cost.

## What Rogo Does

Rogo asks one question: "What's the material cost for Model 12?"

Lou: $334.

Rogo accepts the order.

His logic: the plant has 20% spare capacity. That capacity is sitting idle — the overhead, the labor, the machines are all already paid. The only incremental cost of producing another unit is the materials it consumes. At $701 revenue and $334 material cost, every unit delivers **$367 to the bottom line**.

The "product cost" that made the order look unprofitable assumed overhead was divided over units and that all units contribute equally to covering it. That assumption is only valid at full utilization. With idle capacity, the overhead is already covered — or rather, not covered, but it will be paid regardless. The new order doesn't add to overhead; it contributes to offsetting it.

Rogo adds a competitive offer: three-week delivery for any quantity, on an ongoing basis. European lead times are 8–12 weeks. Djangler accepts, and Jons negotiates slightly better prices than the original offer in exchange for the flexibility.

The financial result: seven-digit contribution to net profit.

## The Principle

When a plant has spare capacity, the correct cost of a new order is **marginal cost** — the materials it requires. All other costs are fixed and will be paid whether the order runs or not.

The "product cost" calculation divides fixed costs across units. This is useful for deciding whether a product line should exist at all. It is **wrong** for deciding whether to accept one more order when the plant is not full.

Jons's instinct — never sell below cost — is correct in a world where every unit you accept displaces another unit. It is wrong when the alternative to accepting the order is running nothing at all.

## What Makes This Counterintuitive

The order price ($701) is below the product cost (~$800+ fully loaded) and below the price charged to Bearington's best domestic client ($827). By any conventional analysis, this order loses money.

But the company made more money that quarter because of it than any other single action they took.

The reason the intuition fails: "product cost" is not the marginal cost of production. It is an accounting allocation. Treating allocations as variable costs is one of the most common errors in business decision-making.

## The Japanese Camera Analogy

Rogo explains it to the skeptical Jons this way: Why can you buy a Japanese camera in Manhattan for half what it costs in Tokyo? Transportation costs aren't negative. The answer: Japanese manufacturers sell domestically at full margin and export at near-marginal cost to fill capacity. The "loss" on the export order more than compensates by absorbing fixed costs that would otherwise go uncovered. The same logic applies to the Djangler order.
