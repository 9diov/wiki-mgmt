# Robot Productivity Audit

## The Setup

UniCo installed a line of robots in Rogo's plant six months ago — a $3M investment celebrated as a model of modern automation. The division VP called it a productivity triumph. Jonah, on a phone call from his hotel room, asks three diagnostic questions:

1. Did your company sell more products as a result?
2. Did you reduce the number of employees?
3. Did inventory go down?

Rogo initially says the robots increased productivity in their department by 36%. Jonah's questions cut through this: did the plant ship more? No. Did headcount fall? No — workers were just reassigned. Did inventory decrease? The honest answer, once Rogo checks with Lou and Stacey, is that inventory actually went *up*.

The robots are faster than surrounding operations. They process parts quickly and pile them up waiting for the next step. They run at high utilization because stopping them "wastes the investment." The result: high local efficiency, higher inventory, same throughput, same cost base.

Jonah's verdict: the robots did not improve productivity. By the only definition that matters — moving the plant toward its goal — they made things worse.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Local Optimization Trap (synth)]]** — The robots are the clearest example in the book of a local metric (departmental efficiency, utilization rate) being treated as a proxy for system performance. The department looks excellent — 36% productivity gain. The plant is no better off. This is the trap: optimizing a part of a system without measuring the effect on the whole. High local efficiency can actively harm system performance by creating inventory without increasing throughput.

**[[Productivity]]** — Jonah's definition: productive = moves toward the goal. The robot audit is the first time this definition is applied as a diagnostic tool. Rogo assumed "more efficient" meant "more productive." The three questions expose that assumption: if shipping didn't increase, headcount didn't decrease, and inventory didn't fall, the robots didn't move the plant toward the goal. They were an expensive way to move work in progress from one pile to another.

**[[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐]]** — The audit maps directly to the T/I/OE framework. Throughput (T): unchanged — same number of orders shipped. Inventory (I): increased — parts piling up behind and ahead of the robots. Operating expense (OE): unchanged — workers reassigned, not eliminated. All three measurements either flat or worse. The robots consumed capital and produced no improvement on any dimension that the goal framework recognizes.

**[[The Goal]]** — The robot audit is the first in-plant test of the question "does this activity move us toward the goal?" It is the moment where Goldratt's abstract definition of productivity meets a real capital investment decision — and the investment fails the test.
