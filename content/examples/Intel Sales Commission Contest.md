# Intel Sales Commission Contest

Intel's factory marketing managers complained that the field sales force was governed purely by self-interest—spending most of their effort on products with the highest commissions and bonuses, while neglecting other products.

## What Actually Happened

The marketing departments had created the incentive structure themselves. To get salespeople to favor particular products, divisions had been running contests with prizes ranging from cash bonuses to trips to exotic places. Each division was competing against the others for a finite, valuable resource: salespeople's time and attention.

The salespeople responded exactly as economic theory would predict: they directed their effort toward whatever produced the most reward. The marketing managers who complained were the same people who had designed the market-mode incentive system that produced this behavior.

## What It Illustrates

The unintended consequences of applying **[[Modes of Control ⭐|free-market forces]]** in a domain where group-interest coordination is required. Once divisions began competing for sales attention using market incentives, they activated self-interest in the salespeople—making it nearly impossible to align them toward any broader company goal. The managers were irritated and self-righteous, but they had built the machine that produced the outcome.

A corollary: the mode of control a manager installs tends to establish the motivation it assumes. Use market incentives, and you get market behavior—even where you didn't want it.
