# Chuck at Harvard Business School

Chuck, a young Intel manager, reflects on his experience as a first-year student at Harvard Business School. His story traces a progression through three levels of Maslow's hierarchy within a single academic year.

## The Progression

**Phase 1 — Physiological/fear**: Chuck's first weeks were dominated by fear of the class material, his professors, and failure. The primary drive was survival—not being expelled.

**Phase 2 — Safety/security**: He realized that everyone else was equally afraid. Students formed study groups—ostensibly to work through course material together, but really to reduce individual fear through shared experience. The group provided safety in numbers.

**Phase 3 — Social/affiliation**: The study groups eventually dissolved as students began associating more broadly. The entire class section developed a recognizable identity and shared characteristics. Members enjoyed belonging to it, identifying with it, and sustaining its reputation among professors and other sections. Chuck was now satisfying affiliation needs.

## The Interruption

The progression can reverse. A manufacturing team at an Intel plant was suddenly jolted backward to the most fundamental physiological need—survival—when an earthquake struck. People dropped everything, abandoned expensive equipment, even left colleagues, and ran for exits. Maslow levels collapsed instantly under the threat of physical harm.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Maslow's Hierarchy]]** as a dynamic, observable progression within a single person's experience: needs are sequential, movement is possible in both directions, and the dominant need at any moment determines behavior more than any external incentive does.
