# Dekansho Production

## The Situation

In the early 1950s, during the Korean War production surge, Toyota faced a parts supply problem. Chassis assembly required hundreds of components from suppliers. Those parts did not arrive at consistent rates — they trickled in throughout the month, intermittently and irregularly.

The consequence: Toyota could not assemble during the first half of the month because not enough parts had arrived. Work accumulated. Then, in the final days of the month, everything happened at once — all the parts that had been gathering were assembled in a frantic rush to meet the monthly target.

Ohno named this pattern after *dekansho*, a Japanese folk song about a man who sleeps for half the year and is active for the other half. **Dekansho production**: idle half the month, frantic the other half.

## Why It Nearly Did Them In

The pattern created compounding problems:

- **Workers** cycled between enforced idleness and overwork, neither sustainable
- **Quality** suffered under end-of-month pressure — shortcuts were taken, defects went unchecked
- **Suppliers** received irregular orders and responded with their own batching, amplifying the problem upstream
- **Just-in-time was impossible** — pull systems require a steady withdrawal rate; random demand spikes cannot be responded to without either stockpiling in advance or failing to deliver

The fundamental issue: the irregularity of demand was being passed through the system rather than absorbed. Each process inherited the lumpiness of the one before it and passed it on amplified.

## The Fix

The arithmetic of leveling: if 1,000 parts are needed per month across 25 working days, make 40 per day. If the workday is 480 minutes, that is one part every 12 minutes. This rate — *takt time* — becomes the metronome for every process in the chain.

Achieving this required Toyota to first level its own internal production, then to coordinate with suppliers: sharing leveled order schedules, discussing manpower and materials together, making leveled demand a joint discipline rather than a one-sided demand.

## The Principle

Dekansho production is not an unusual failure — it is the natural outcome of any system where each stage simply responds to what the previous stage provides. Irregularity propagates and amplifies. The cure is not to respond faster to lumpy demand but to eliminate the lumpiness — to produce at a smooth, steady rate that makes coordination possible throughout the supply chain.

This is what production leveling means in practice: not a scheduling optimization, but a commitment to a rate that the entire system — internal and external — can sustain.
