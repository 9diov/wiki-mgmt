# Tsutsumi Plant Mixed-Model Leveling

## The Situation

Toyota's Tsutsumi plant assembles passenger cars on two lines: one running Corona, Carina, and Celica. The obvious approach — all Coronas in the morning, all Carinas in the afternoon — would produce large batches of each model, simplifying assembly but creating violent demand spikes for every upstream process each time the model changed.

Instead, Toyota interleaves the models: Corona and Carina alternate continuously on the same line. They do not run in batches by model.

For a specific example: 10,000 Coronas per month across 20 working days = 500 Coronas per day, broken down as 250 sedans, 125 hardtops, and 125 wagons. On the production line, these are sequenced as: sedan → hardtop → sedan → wagon → sedan → hardtop → ..., repeating throughout the day.

## What This Required Upstream

Every upstream process — body stamping, engine assembly, painting, die press — must be capable of switching between models in the same sequence, at the same mixed rate.

The die press section faced the sharpest challenge. Conventional wisdom held that continuous pressing with one die minimized cost — large batches, no changeovers, maximum utilization. Mixed-model leveling made this impossible: dies needed to change as frequently as the model sequence on the assembly line demanded.

In the 1940s, a die change at Toyota took **2–3 hours**.

By the time production leveling spread company-wide in the 1950s, setup times fell to **under one hour, sometimes 15 minutes**.

By the late 1960s: **3 minutes**.

## What Drove the Reduction

The die changes weren't optimized because someone set an efficiency target — they were forced by the logic of leveling. Once the assembly line committed to mixed-model sequencing, the die press had no choice but to adapt. The sequence demanded small lots; small lots demanded fast changeovers; fast changeovers required everyone in the die press section to generate ideas, train intensively, and redesign the setup process from scratch.

The improvement came from necessity applied to a specific, painful constraint.

## The Principle

Production leveling appears, from the die press's perspective, to be an unreasonable demand: *"You want us to change dies every few minutes instead of running one die all day?"* The conventional response is to resist and protect batch production.

The TPS response: if the assembly line needs mixed models, then the die press section's job is to make die changes fast enough to deliver them. The "unreasonable" demand is the improvement driver. Leveling doesn't just enable JIT — it creates the pressure that generates capability the organization didn't previously believe was possible.
