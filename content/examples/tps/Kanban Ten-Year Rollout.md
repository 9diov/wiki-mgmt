# Kanban Ten-Year Rollout

## The Timeline

Ohno began developing kanban around 1949. Company-wide adoption was completed in 1962. External supplier adoption began in 1963. From first experiment to full external deployment: nearly 20 years.

The rollout proceeded in strict sequence, each expansion gated by Ohno's growing authority:

| Period | Scope | Ohno's role |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 1949–1950 | Machine shop + assembly, main plant No. 2 | Manager, machine shop |
| 1953 | Machine shop formally adopted kanban | Manager, machine shop |
| 1959 | Motomachi plant (machining, press, assembly) | Manager, Motomachi plant |
| 1962 | Company-wide including forging and casting | Manager, main plant |
| 1963 | First external suppliers begin delivery via kanban | — |

## Why It Took So Long

**1. Kanban cannot precede flow.**
The most fundamental reason: kanban manages flow — it does not create it. Ohno's rule: *"Unless one completely grasps this method of doing work so that things will flow, it is impossible to go right into the kanban system."* Before kanban could be introduced in any process, that process had to be reorganized into a production flow. This itself took years per area.

**2. It could only expand within Ohno's authority.**
Kanban required every process in a chain to adopt it simultaneously. Ohno could experiment in No. 2 plant because he managed it. He could not touch No. 1 plant's forging and casting until he managed the main plant in 1962. He did not attempt to cross organizational lines he didn't control.

**3. Resistance was severe.**
Workers and foremen had learned that producing as much as possible was correct behavior. Kanban contradicted this. Foremen in areas Ohno didn't directly control couldn't be reached — *"I could yell at a foreman under my jurisdiction, but not at a foreman from the neighboring department."*

Top management received complaints that Ohno was doing something *"utterly ridiculous."* His superior absorbed the complaints without ordering a stop. This protected the experiment.

**4. External rollout required internal completion first.**
A supplier asked to adopt JIT delivery without having established internal flow will build inventory to buffer against the uncertainty — transferring the waste upstream rather than eliminating it. Ohno waited until the internal system was complete before extending it.

## What This Demonstrates

The conventional model of organizational change — announce the new system, train everyone, implement simultaneously — would have failed here. Kanban adoption required:
- Physical reorganization of processes (flow before kanban)
- Gradual expansion within existing authority
- Demonstrated results to carry skeptics
- Top management protection of the experiment during resistance

The 10 years were not delay. They were the minimum time required to build the capability that made the system viable.

Ohno: *"It took 10 years to establish kanban at the Toyota Motor Company. Although it sounds like a long time, I think it was natural because we were breaking in totally new concepts."*
