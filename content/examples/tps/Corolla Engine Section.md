# Corolla Engine Section

## The Situation

In late 1966, the Corolla launched and was selling well. Ohno set the engine section a target: produce 5,000 units per month using under 100 workers. After two or three months, the section head reported back: they could make 5,000 units with 80 workers.

Ohno's response: good. Then he asked — if demand doubled to 10,000 units, how many workers would be needed?

The section head immediately replied: **160 workers**.

## What Ohno Did

He yelled at him.

*"In grade school I was taught that two times eight equals sixteen. After all these years, do you think I should learn that from you? Do you think I'm a fool?"*

The section head's answer was arithmetic: if 80 workers make 5,000, then 160 make 10,000. But this assumes that scaling up means adding workers proportionally — that the line efficiency stays constant as volume doubles.

Before long, **100 workers were making over 10,000 units**.

## The Principle

When volume doubles, takt time halves. A faster takt time means the same work content must be completed in less time per unit. This forces more waste elimination, better work sequencing, and tighter standard work — not proportional headcount addition.

The 80-worker line for 5,000 units had not been fully optimized — it contained waste that wasn't worth eliminating at the lower volume. Doubling the required output made that waste painful enough to act on. By the time the line was producing 10,000 units with 100 workers (not 160), it had been fundamentally redesigned.

Proportional arithmetic describes a line with zero room for improvement. Toyota's production system is built on the assumption that room for improvement always exists. The correct question is not "how many workers do I need?" but "how much waste can I eliminate at this takt time?"

Ohno: *"It was due largely to the Toyota production system in which waste, inconsistencies, and excesses were thoroughly eliminated."* Not magic — structured elimination of non-value-added work under the pressure of a tighter takt time.
