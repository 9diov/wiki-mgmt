# Toyoda Sakichi's Loom

## The Situation

In a conventional power loom, the machine runs continuously. If a thread breaks — warp or weft — the loom keeps running, weaving defective fabric until an operator notices. By then, meters of ruined cloth may have been produced.

Toyoda Sakichi (1867–1930), the founder of the Toyota group, invented an auto-activated loom with a specific capability: it stopped instantly when any thread broke. A device built into the machine could detect the difference between normal and abnormal, and halt automatically.

## What It Changed

Before: defects accumulated silently. The operator's job was to watch the loom and catch problems — which meant one operator per loom.

After: the loom monitors itself. Under normal conditions, no human attention is needed. When the loom stops, the operator knows exactly where to look. One operator can now supervise many looms simultaneously.

Two things happened at once:
- **Defect containment** — the loom produces one defective piece, then stops. Not a hundred.
- **Labor multiplication** — the operator is freed from watching; their attention is reserved for abnormalities.

## Why Ohno Transplanted It

When Ohno moved from Toyoda's textile operation to automobile manufacturing in the 1940s, he brought this principle with him. The loom became the model for every Toyota machine: attach a stopping device, build in the ability to detect abnormal conditions, give the machine the human intelligence to know when something is wrong.

Ohno called this **autonomation** — automation with a human touch — to distinguish it from simple automation, which runs blindly regardless of output quality.

## The Principle

A machine that runs through defects hides problems. A machine that stops on defects reveals them — immediately, at the source, with minimal damage. The act of stopping is not a failure; it is the system working correctly.

This is why Ohno made autonomation the second pillar of TPS: not for efficiency, but for the discipline of surfacing problems rather than burying them.
