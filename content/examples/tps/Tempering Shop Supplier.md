# Tempering Shop Supplier

## The Situation

Ohno visits a supplier's tempering shop. Toyota's monthly production at the time is approximately 70,000 cars. The supplier's manager greets him with confidence: *"We have enough manpower and equipment to cope with your order even if you make 100,000 cars."*

Ohno asks: "Then, is your plant closed for 10 days out of the month?"

The manager is offended: *"We would never do a silly thing like that."*

Ohno walks to the earlier process — the machine processing section. He finds women workers running at maximum speed, because they cannot allow the tempering furnace to sit idle. The furnace is packed with parts to minimize fuel cost per unit. The supplier is producing 100,000 units per month because they have the capacity to — regardless of the fact that Toyota only orders 70,000.

## The Cascade

The 30,000 surplus parts per month must go somewhere:
- A warehouse is built to store them
- Workers are hired to carry goods to the warehouse
- Rust prevention is needed for stored metal parts
- Inventory management staff are added
- An inventory control system is eventually purchased
- Despite all this, shortages are still discovered — because the surplus is the wrong mix

The supplier has interpreted capacity as an obligation to produce. The unit price looks low because the furnace runs full. But the total cost — warehouse, handling, inventory, management — is enormous and invisible in the per-unit calculation.

## The Accounting Illusion

The supplier's logic is internally coherent: full furnace utilization → low fuel cost per part → competitive unit price → good business. This is the cost-world calculation that makes overproduction feel efficient.

What the unit price calculation omits: all the secondary costs triggered by the surplus 30,000 parts. These costs appear in overhead, not in the cost of the part — so they are invisible to the person making the production decision.

Ohno: *"The oil crisis made people begin to understand the waste of overproduction. And only then did they begin to recognize the Toyota production system's real value."*

## The Principle

A supplier that produces to capacity regardless of orders is not serving Toyota — it is serving its own furnace utilization metric. Every extra unit produced triggers secondary costs that eventually appear somewhere in the supply chain.

The correct production quantity is the required quantity — not the capacity quantity, not the economical-batch quantity, not the furnace-full quantity. Ohno's visit to the tempering shop is a direct illustration of why the kanban rule "produce only what was withdrawn" must extend to suppliers, not just to Toyota's internal processes.
