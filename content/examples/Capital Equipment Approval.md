# Capital Equipment Purchase Approval

Intel asks a subordinate to prepare and present a capital equipment purchase request. The manager doesn't review the purchase itself—they review the quality of the *thinking* behind it.

## The Process
1. Subordinate thinks through the entire request thoroughly before presenting it.
2. During a review meeting, the manager asks specific, probing questions about the request.
3. If the subordinate answers convincingly—demonstrating sound reasoning—the request is approved.

## What This Achieves
The manager gains confidence that the decision is well-considered without having to perform the analysis themselves. They're monitoring the decision-making *process*, not auditing the decision.

This is delegation done correctly: the subordinate owns the work and the thinking; the manager verifies the process was sound.

## What It Illustrates
**Monitoring delegated decision-making.** Rather than either rubber-stamping everything or doing the work yourself, probe the process. Good answers mean the thinking was done; bad answers reveal where the subordinate needs to go back and work harder.

This is also an example of reviewing at the **lowest-value stage**: catching a flawed rationale during a review meeting is far cheaper than approving a bad purchase and discovering the problem after equipment has been installed.
