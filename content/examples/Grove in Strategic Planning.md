# Grove in Strategic Planning

Andy Grove, as president of Intel, is also a member of a corporate strategic planning group. The group is chaired by one of Intel's division controllers—a person who, in the operating plane, reports several layers below Grove.

## The Plane Reversal

Operating plane: Grove is president; the division controller is Grove's subordinate (or his subordinate's subordinate).

Strategic planning plane: Grove is a group member; the division controller is the group's chairman—Grove's supervisor in that context.

Grove uses the analogy of Army Reserve weekends: back at Intel he may be the commanding officer, but on Reserve exercises he might find himself under the command of a regimental leader who is his corporate subordinate. The roles are fully separate and don't conflict.

## Why This Is a Feature, Not a Bug

The multi-plane structure enables the best-qualified person to lead each group regardless of organizational rank. Grove doesn't need to chair every group he participates in. In groups where others are more qualified to lead, he can contribute as a peer or foot soldier—which is often more useful than taking charge. This gives the organization flexibility it couldn't have if seniority always determined who ran what.

## What It Illustrates

The most striking implication of the **[[Two-Plane Organization]]**: supervisory relationships can be *reversed* across planes. Rank in one plane doesn't determine rank in another. This allows senior people to participate usefully in coordinating groups without needing to be in charge—and allows the most appropriate person to lead each group regardless of their position in the operating hierarchy.
