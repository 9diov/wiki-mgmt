# Intel Potential Trap

Grove was asked to approve a performance review for a general manager whose supervisor had rated him highly for the year. The manager's business unit had lost money, missed its revenue forecast month after month, slipped engineering schedules, and showed poor output and internal measures across the board.

## What Happened

Grove declined to approve the review. The manager's supervisor argued: "He is an outstanding general manager. He is knowledgeable and handles himself well. It's his *organization* that did not do well, not the manager himself."

Grove rejected this argument. A manager's performance rating cannot be higher than the rating accorded to his organization's output. The manager's personal polish and knowledge are irrelevant if his organization is failing.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Performance Appraisal ⭐]]**: Assessing *potential* (form, style, how someone carries themselves) instead of actual output is the most common rating error—the "potential trap." If Intel approved a high rating for this manager, it would signal to the entire company that to do well, you need only *act* like a good manager—not perform like one. Promotion and rating decisions communicate organizational values more clearly than any policy statement.
