# Bruce Marketing Staffing Plan

Bruce, an Intel marketing manager, faced a genuine gap: three people in his department, a large inventory of projects that all had to be completed (leaving any unfinished would cost more later), and a budget that prevented him from hiring additional staff.

## The Three Steps Applied

**Environmental demand**: Every project in the backlog needed to be completed—no acceptable reduction in output.

**Present status**: Three people, unable to complete everything on their current trajectory.

**Closing the gap**: Full convergence was impossible. Bruce's plan:
- Moved noncritical tasks to other (less qualified but less busy) groups in the company
- Arranged to bring a summer student on board for well-defined, easily scoped tasks
- Set up tight monitoring of his group's performance
- Explored splitting work with similar marketing groups to eliminate duplication of effort
- Filed a formal headcount request, using the planning documentation as evidence that the gap was real and irreducible—even after going the extra mile

## What It Illustrates

The **[[Planning Process]]** in practice: when full gap closure is impossible, planning still produces concrete near-term actions. The output is not a document—it is the redistribution decisions, the intern arrangement, and the headcount request, all taken as a direct result of the planning analysis.
