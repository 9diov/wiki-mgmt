# Hungary Central Planning

Grove's personal experience as an amateur photographer growing up in Hungary, where a central planning organization controlled the production and distribution of all goods.

## What Happened

Every winter, when Grove needed high-contrast film for photography, none could be found anywhere in the country. Every summer, the market was flooded with high-contrast film while regular film was in short supply. This pattern repeated year after year—the central planning organization was so slow and clumsy that it could not respond even to *predictable*, seasonal shifts in demand.

## What It Illustrates

The failure mode of fully centralized allocation: even with sound rationale and good intentions, a central body cannot process the transaction volume and information variety required to match supply with real demand across a complex economy—or a complex organization.

Grove uses this to make the case that within a **[[Hybrid Organization ⭐]]**, the allocation of shared resources cannot be handled centrally. Middle managers—numerous, close to actual resource flows—must resolve allocation conflicts in real time. Attempting to do it from the top produces the Hungarian film problem at corporate scale.
