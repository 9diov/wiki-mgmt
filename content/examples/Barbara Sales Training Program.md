# Barbara Sales Training Program

Barbara's Intel department is responsible for training the division's sales force on its products. A single work project—field sales training—involves all three modes of control operating simultaneously.

## Three Modes in One Project

**Buying training materials** → *Free-market forces*
Binders of the required quality are purchased at the lowest available price. Value is easily defined; price comparison works; no oversight needed.

**The existence of the training program itself** → *Contractual obligations*
Salespeople *expect* each division to provide product training on a regular basis. This expectation is not written in a formal policy document, but it binds as tightly as one. If the training stopped, it would be a breach—even without a legal clause to cite. Expectations can be as binding as legal documents.

**Coordinating the message across divisions** → *Cultural values*
Multiple divisions share a common sales force. Each division has an incentive to maximize airtime for its own products. Without cultural values—willingness to sacrifice divisional self-interest for the common interest—the training sessions become disjointed free-for-alls that confuse salespeople and reduce the value of all the training. Coordination requires group interest to override individual division interest.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Modes of Control ⭐]]** within a single work project: purchasing is governed by market forces, program expectations by contractual norms, and cross-division coordination by cultural values. A manager of this program must operate in all three modes simultaneously.
