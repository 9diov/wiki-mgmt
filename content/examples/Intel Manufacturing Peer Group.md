# Intel Manufacturing Peer Group

An Intel manufacturing manager, sitting in the cafeteria, starts chatting with the manufacturing manager from another division—whose general manager has a finance background. They realize they share several technical problems.

## How It Evolved

The two managers begin meeting more regularly. Other manufacturing managers from other divisions join. The meetings become scheduled. Over time, a formal committee of manufacturing manager peers comes into existence to tackle issues they all share.

Their respective general managers—promoted from sales, finance, engineering—lack the manufacturing background to supervise the technical aspects of their manufacturing managers' work. The peer group fills this gap: it provides the functional/technical supervision that no individual boss in the hierarchy is qualified to give.

## The Requirement

For the group to work, members must *voluntarily surrender individual decision-making to the group*: they accept that on most matters within the group's scope, they will go along with collective decisions even when they might have chosen differently alone. This requires trust—both in the soundness of peers' technical judgment and in the values of the organization.

## What It Illustrates

A variation of **[[Dual Reporting]]** where the functional supervisor is not a single individual but a peer group. The manufacturing managers now report to two supervisors: the peer coordinating group (for technical standards) and their respective general managers (for operational priorities). Demonstrates how peer supervision emerges organically when the formal hierarchy lacks the expertise to fill the technical supervisory role.
