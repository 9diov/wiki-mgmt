# The Free Puppy

## The Setup

During lunch, Bill tells Chris about a tool that landed in his team's lap. A Marketing summer intern spent a couple of months building a database reporting tool in Microsoft Access. It was genuinely well-built for what it was. Marketing found it useful and started using it in daily operations.

Then they told IT to support it.

IT inherited a tool with no documentation, no security model, and no audit controls — built by someone who had left the company. When auditors found that access to the underlying data couldn't be properly controlled, the team spent weeks building compliance scaffolding around a tool they hadn't designed and hadn't been consulted about.

Bill's summary: *"It's like the free puppy. It's not the upfront capital that kills you, it's the operations and maintenance on the back end."*

Chris laughs: *"They'll say, 'The puppy can't quite do everything we need. Can you train it to fly airplanes? It's just a simple matter of coding, right?'"*

## What It Illustrates

**[[The Four Types of Work ⭐]]** — The free puppy is a generator of Type 2 and Type 4 work that arrives without ever being planned or budgeted. IT didn't decide to adopt the Access tool; the business decided, and IT absorbed the consequences. The maintenance overhead, the compliance remediation, and the eventual support burden all land on IT's capacity without appearing on anyone's project list as a cost of the original business decision.

**[[Throwing the Pig Over the Wall]]** — The pattern is the same as Dev throwing Phoenix over the wall, just at smaller scale and with a business team in the throwing role. The intern builds a tool. Marketing adopts it. The tool is declared "done" at the moment it works for its creators. IT receives it as a fait accompli, responsible for running, securing, and auditing something built without their involvement or input. The operational cost of the handoff is borne entirely by the receiving team.

The free puppy is also a useful frame for any unsanctioned tool, cloud service, or system that a business team adopts independently and then asks IT to maintain, integrate, or secure — the upfront cost is invisible because someone else paid it; the ongoing cost is indefinite because IT now owns the animal.
