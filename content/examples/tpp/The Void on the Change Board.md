# The Void on the Change Board

## The Setup

Patty summons Bill to the Change Coordination Room. She points at the board. Through the previous Thursday, it looks normal: forty to fifty changes per day, each marked completed. Then — nothing. A blank stretch through the end of the week, with hundreds of index cards piled in a basket labeled "Changes To Be Rescheduled."

Phoenix had happened. Every available engineer had been mobilized for the recovery. Every scheduled change was abandoned or deferred.

Patty brought Bill in expecting concern about 100% non-completion. Instead, he stares at the void and starts working through something. Phoenix wasn't the fourth type of work — it was a business project. But something about the blank stretch tells him what he's looking for.

What displaced the planned work?

Unplanned work.

He laughs, pumps his arms, and immediately goes to find the slip of paper Erik gave him with a phone number on it.

## What It Illustrates

**[[The Four Types of Work ⭐]]** — The void is the most precise illustration of why unplanned work is "the most destructive type." Types 1, 2, and 3 can be seen directly — they appear on project lists, change boards, and work queues. Type 4 can only be seen by what it erases. The blank section of the board is a week's worth of Type 4 work expressed as the absence of everything else.

Bill's formulation: *"Like dark matter. You can only see it by what it displaces or how it interacts with other matter that we can see."*

**[[Work in Process (WIP) ⭐]]** — The void also makes a WIP point that a pile of cards cannot. WIP accumulation shows work not moving forward. The void shows the other failure mode: the entire flow system being shut down while all capacity routes to a crisis. Both are disruptions to the First Way; the void is harder to see precisely because it looks like nothing is happening — when in fact everything is happening, just nowhere near where it was planned.

**[[Change Advisory Board (CAB)]]** — Ironically, it is the change board that makes the void visible. Before the board existed, Phoenix recovery would have consumed the same planned work — but there would have been no record of what was displaced, no basket of rescheduled cards, no clean visual of the before and after. The process didn't create the problem. It revealed the scale and shape of a problem that was always there.
