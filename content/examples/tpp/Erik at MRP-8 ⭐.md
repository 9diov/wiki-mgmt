# Erik at MRP-8

## The Setup

Erik Reid — the board member and mysterious mentor — takes Bill on a tour of the MRP-8 manufacturing plant on a Friday afternoon. Bill assumes the trip is a waste of time; IT is not a factory.

Erik walks Bill through the floor. He points to the heat treat oven: a massive furnace that every part in the plant must pass through at some point. In front of the oven is a mountain of inventory — pallets stacked high, work piled up, expediters camped permanently nearby. The entire plant's throughput is constrained by this single piece of equipment.

Erik points to Mark at the job release desk — the person who decides when to release new work into the plant. Mark releases jobs based on whether his own station is free. He has no visibility into whether the heat treat oven downstream can absorb more work. The result: Mark's station looks productive while inventory piles at the constraint.

Erik describes the plant in its earlier, failing era: WIP so high you couldn't see across the building. Every day was an emergency. The plant held a standing record as the best customer of an air freight company — regularly shipping thousands of pounds of finished goods overnight to angry customers who had waited weeks.

Erik then asks Bill a series of questions about his own operation. He identifies that Bill knows only one of four types of work IT Operations is responsible for — business projects — and that without understanding all four, Bill cannot protect the constraint, manage demand, or have an honest conversation about capacity. He gives Bill a phone number and one task: identify the other three.

## What It Illustrates

**[[The Four Types of Work ⭐]]** — The plant floor visit is the setup for Erik's central diagnostic question. Business projects are the work visible to executives. But Erik is pointing at the inventory pile — at the unplanned work, the internal IT work, the changes — that consumes most of IT's actual capacity while remaining invisible to leadership. The four-type framework is introduced as a prerequisite to management: you cannot manage what you cannot categorize.

**[[Work in Process (WIP) ⭐]]** — The inventory piled in front of the heat treat oven is the physical version of the 942 pending change cards Bill will discover four chapters later. Erik's point is not historical — it is a current description of Bill's operation, delivered in the language of a plant that Bill can walk through and see with his own eyes. The WIP argument is made tangible before it is made abstract.

**[[The Brent Problem ⭐]]** — The heat treat oven is Brent. The job release desk is the CAB. Bill will connect these explicitly in Chapter 11 when Patty shows him the change incompletion data. Erik plants the analogy here, on the plant floor, weeks before Bill needs to apply it.

**[[The Three Ways ⭐]]** — The Three Ways are introduced during this visit as Erik's framework for how the best technology organizations operate: fast flow from Development through Operations to the customer; fast feedback loops from right to left so problems are fixed at the source; and continuous experimentation and learning as a way of life. Erik presents them without detail, as a direction rather than a prescription.
