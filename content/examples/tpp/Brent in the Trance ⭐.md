# Brent in the Trance

## The Setup

Wes tells Bill a story about a Sev 1 outage from months earlier. The incident had been going for three hours. Every engineer available was working it. Nothing was resolving.

Brent wasn't in the room — he'd been on something else. Someone pulled him in. He sat down at a terminal, went quiet, and began working. Wes describes it as a trance: Brent's eyes half-closed, working faster than anyone around him could follow. Ten minutes later, the outage was resolved.

When someone asked Brent how he'd done it, his answer was: *"I have no idea. I just did it."*

No one documented what he'd done. No one could replicate it. The outage was over and the room moved on.

## What It Illustrates

**[[The Brent Problem ⭐]]** — Wes tells this story as evidence that Brent is exceptional — and he is. But Bill hears it differently. Brent fixed a three-hour outage in ten minutes. That means three hours of downtime and every engineer's attention was the cost of not having Brent available sooner. More importantly: after the fix, the organization was no smarter than before it. Brent knows how to fix this class of failure. No one else does. The next time it happens, the same three hours will be spent before Brent arrives.

The "trance" is the visible shape of tacit knowledge — expertise that lives in muscle memory and pattern recognition, not in language. Tacit knowledge cannot be transferred by asking someone to explain it; the person holding it often cannot explain it. The only mechanism for transferring it is observation and practice: watching what Brent does and having the next engineer try it, guided by Brent's narration. This is precisely the logic behind the no-keyboard rule — it forces the knowledge out of the trance and into a form that someone else can execute and document.

Bill's formulation: *"Every time that we let Brent fix something that none of us can replicate, Brent gets a little smarter, and the entire system gets dumber."*
