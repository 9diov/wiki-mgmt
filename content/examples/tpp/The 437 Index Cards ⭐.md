# The 437 Index Cards

## The Setup

After Bill mandates change management, Patty McKee's team begins collecting every planned production change across all technology groups for the upcoming week. She brings the results to the second CAB meeting as a physical stack of index cards — one card per change.

There are 437 of them.

Bill stares at the pile. This is one week's worth of Type 3 work — changes to production systems — that had previously been invisible. Not 437 changes over a month, or a quarter. One week.

The first instinct in the room is paralysis: there's no way to review 437 changes in a single meeting. Bill and Patty work through the problem in real time. They design a triage system on the spot:

- **Fragile changes** (high risk, historical failure, critical infrastructure): full CAB review, Patty's explicit authorization required. Estimated count: the smallest group.
- **Standard changes** (pre-approved templates, known procedures): reviewed by sampling, not individually. Pre-authorization becomes the norm.
- **Messy Middle** (submitter-owned, self-declared risk): the submitter signs off that they've assessed and accepted the risk. No individual review, but the change is logged and visible.

By the end of the meeting, the 437 cards have been triaged. High-risk changes that previously consumed ninety minutes to approve five are now reviewed in under thirty seconds each. The full week's inventory is processed in a single session.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Change Advisory Board (CAB)]]** — The 437 cards are the central demonstration that the previous CAB wasn't failing because there was too much process — it was failing because it had the wrong process design. Reviewing 437 changes one at a time is legitimately impossible; designing a triage system that handles 437 changes in under two hours is not. The constraint wasn't the volume of changes; it was the absence of a tiered design that matched review effort to actual risk.

**[[Change Risk Tiers]]** — The triage framework emerges directly from the size of the pile. The insight is that most changes don't require the same level of review — but without a shared categorization, every change gets either full review (impossible at scale) or no review (which is what was happening before). The three-tier design resolves the false binary.

**[[Work in Process (WIP) ⭐]]** — The 437 cards make the book's WIP argument concrete before Erik makes it theoretically in Chapter 7. These changes existed before the index cards; they just weren't visible. The pile doesn't represent new work — it represents existing work that had been invisible and unmanaged. Making it visible is the prerequisite to managing it. Bill's later formulation: *"Getting rid of the process would just kill our situational awareness."*
