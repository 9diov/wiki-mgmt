# Bill at Brent's Desk

## The Setup

Bill spends part of a morning sitting near Brent's workstation, quietly observing how the day actually runs.

The pattern is consistent: colleagues walk up or call Brent directly. He answers. He listens briefly to the problem, fixes it — often in minutes — and returns to his queue of Post-it notes stuck to his monitor. No ticket is opened before the fix. No ticket is opened after the fix. No log entry is made. The caller leaves satisfied; the next one arrives.

Bill asks why no tickets are being opened. The answer is simple: opening a ticket in the current system takes five to ten minutes. The fix itself takes two. The overhead of the process exceeds the cost of the work it's supposed to track. Tickets are for problems that require escalation or handoff; these problems don't — Brent resolves them in place. Skipping the ticket is the rational choice.

The Post-it notes on Brent's monitor represent his actual work queue: a rotating collection of one-line reminders for problems he's mid-solving or needs to get to. There's no system of record. When a Post-it gets resolved, it gets thrown away.

## What It Illustrates

**[[The Brent Problem ⭐]]** — The direct observation makes visible the mechanism that the organizational view obscures. Brent is not overloaded because people are being inconsiderate or because the system is broken. He's overloaded because the system is working exactly as designed: he's the fastest path to resolution, the ticket process has more friction than the fix, and nobody has a reason to change this until someone measures what it costs. The cost — Brent's time, the knowledge that evaporates with each Post-it, the growing fragility of systems only Brent understands — is invisible in real time and compounding over time.

The desk observation leads directly to the containment design: gated access through a level-3 engineer pool, the no-keyboard rule to force knowledge transfer, and daily timesheets to make Brent's time consumption visible and accountable. All three interventions are responses to what Bill sees at the desk: a frictionless, invisible pattern that concentrates knowledge and removes it from the organization with every resolved call.
