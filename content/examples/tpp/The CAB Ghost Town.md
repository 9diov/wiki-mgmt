# The CAB Ghost Town

## The Setup

After the payroll failure and SAN outage, Bill mandates that all technology team managers attend a Change Advisory Board meeting on Friday. Attendance is described as a condition of employment.

When Bill arrives at the scheduled time, only Patty McKee is in the room.

Wes calls Bill before the meeting with his objections — accurate ones: the previous CAB required engineers to spend twenty minutes filling out forms for a five-minute change. The tool was ITIL-compliant, the process was formally rolled out, and within two years it had been quietly abandoned because nobody used it. Wes's prediction: this one will fail the same way.

John, the CISO whose unauthorized change triggered the payroll failure, doesn't attend and hasn't been seen enforcing the process. The managers whose teams would most benefit from change visibility are the ones most confident the process isn't worth their time.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Change Advisory Board (CAB)]]** — The empty room is a precise illustration of the CAB's second failure mode: no top-level enforcement. When managers don't attend, their teams don't attend. A process that requires voluntary buy-in from every team to function will fail the moment one team concludes the friction isn't worth it — because that team's exemption then sets the precedent for everyone else. Bill's own observation: *"Efforts like this must start and be continually maintained from the top."*

The ghost town also reveals the third failure mode: no feedback loop to the person who bypassed the process. John's unauthorized change cost the company over a million dollars and a payroll disruption. John experienced no consequence that connected to his bypass of change management. Without that feedback loop, there is no signal that makes the cost of bypassing visible to the next person considering whether to file a ticket.
