# Sales Manager to Factory

An extremely productive sales manager—promoted precisely because of his exceptional performance—is moved from field sales into a plant, where he is placed in charge of a factory unit. The scope and seniority of the two jobs are comparable.

## What Happened

His performance in the new role deteriorated markedly. He began showing signs of someone overwhelmed by his work: missed timelines, poor decisions, visible stress. The people who had promoted him were surprised.

The explanation: his personal maturity and general competence had not changed. But his **task-relevant maturity** in the factory environment was near zero. The environment, content, and specific tasks were all new. He had no accumulated experience, pattern recognition, or internal structure to draw on.

Over time, as he learned the environment and accumulated experience, his TRM gradually increased. With it, his performance began to approach the levels that had earned his promotion in the first place.

## What It Illustrates

**[[Task Relevant Maturity ⭐]]**: TRM is task-specific, not person-specific. A person with high TRM in one domain starts at near-zero in a genuinely new one. The mistake Intel made was confusing the manager's general maturity with his task-relevant maturity—an error that is both entirely predictable and entirely avoidable if the concept is applied in advance.

The appropriate management style for this manager, upon entering the factory role, was structured and directive—despite his experience level. Applying a high-TRM "monitoring" style from day one would have set him up to fail.
