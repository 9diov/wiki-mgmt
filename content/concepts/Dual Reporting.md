# Dual Reporting

The practice of having an employee report to two supervisors: one for operational priorities and daily oversight, one for functional/technical standards and professional development. This is the mechanism that enables hybrid organizations to function.

## Why a Single Supervisor Isn't Enough
In a hybrid organization, a single supervisor faces an impossible tradeoff:
- The *mission-oriented manager* (division GM, plant manager) can observe day-to-day performance and set operational priorities—but often lacks technical expertise in the specialist function they're overseeing
- The *functional manager* (corporate security, finance, manufacturing VP) has the technical expertise and sets standards—but can't observe daily performance at remote or separate units

Neither alone can do both jobs. Splitting the supervisory role solves both problems simultaneously.

## The Two Supervisory Roles

**Mission/operational supervisor**: Assigns work, sets priorities, monitors day-to-day performance, provides operational context.

**Functional/technical supervisor**: Sets professional standards, provides technical guidance, evaluates technical performance, manages career development within the function.

## Peer Group as Functional Supervisor
The functional supervisor doesn't have to be a single individual. When a general manager lacks technical expertise in one of their functional areas, the specialists across different divisions can form a *peer coordinating group* that collectively exercises the technical supervision their individual bosses cannot provide. The group makes standards decisions; membership requires voluntary surrender of some individual autonomy to the group's collective judgment.

## The Role of Culture
Dual reporting introduces ambiguity: "Who is really in charge?" This ambiguity cannot be resolved by organizational clarity—it is inherent to the hybrid form. The only thing that makes it work is a strong, positive corporate culture: shared values, trust in peers' intentions, and familiarity with how decisions are made and should be made in the company.

## Examples
- **[[Intel Plant Security Dual Reporting]]** — security personnel at outlying plants report jointly to the plant manager (monitors performance) and the corporate security manager (sets standards); Intel's founding experience with dual reporting
- **[[Intel Manufacturing Peer Group]]** — manufacturing managers from different divisions form a peer coordinating group to fill the technical supervisory gap their general managers (with non-manufacturing backgrounds) cannot fill
