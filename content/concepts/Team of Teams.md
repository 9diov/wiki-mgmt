# Team of Teams

At sufficient scale, management shifts from running a single team to running a network of teams—each a coherent unit, all needing to operate in a suitable, mutually supportive relationship.

## The Shift

Managing a single unit: direct visibility, everyone known by name, all decisions reachable by one person. Managing a network: each unit has its own team, its own local conditions, its own incentives. The challenges are not bigger versions of the same problems—they are structurally different.

## What Changes
- Overhead functions that didn't exist at unit scale (traffic coordination, personnel, real estate acquisition) become necessary coordination mechanisms
- The central question shifts from "what should we do?" to "what should each team decide for itself vs. what should be decided once for all?"
- Economies of scale become available—but only if the teams are coordinated to capture them

## The Structural Response
Chapter 7 raises this challenge; the subsequent chapters develop the structural responses: hybrid organizations (Chapter 8) and dual reporting (Chapter 9) are mechanisms for managing the team-of-teams problem in practice.

## Examples
- **[[Breakfast Factory National Franchise]]** — a hundred-plus franchise locations require coordination structures that didn't exist when it was one restaurant: central purchasing, regional egg depots, national quality standards, corporate overhead staff
