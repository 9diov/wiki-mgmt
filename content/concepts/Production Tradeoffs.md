# Production Trade-offs: Capacity, Manpower, Inventory

When a bottleneck appears, three levers can resolve it. Each costs money; the task is finding the most cost-effective combination.

## The Three Levers

**Equipment / Capacity**
Add machines or increase throughput of existing ones. High upfront cost; increases fixed overhead. Example: adding a second toaster.

**Manpower**
Add people or reorganize existing staff (specialists, temporary help). Increases labor cost and coordination complexity.

**Inventory**
Build up a buffer of partially or fully finished product so the downstream step is never starved. Costs money to build and hold; creates waste if unused.

## The Optimization Goal
There is a right answer for any given production situation: the combination that delivers the required output at the required time and quality at the lowest total cost. Finding it requires understanding the *quantitative* relationships between the three levers—not necessarily with a stopwatch, but with disciplined thinking.

## Inventory as a Shock Absorber
Inventory is most valuable when it buffers against unpredictability (demand swings, supplier variability). It should be held at the **lowest-value stage** to minimize cost while maximizing production flexibility.

Rule: hold enough inventory to cover consumption during the replenishment lead time. Balance the cost of carrying it against the *opportunity at risk*—what it would cost if you had to shut down.

## Continuous Operations Trade-off
Scaling to continuous operations (e.g., an automated egg-boiler) reduces cost and improves consistency but sacrifices flexibility. You can no longer make each customer's order exactly as requested. Customers must adjust their expectations in exchange for the benefits.

## Examples
- **[[Three Minute Breakfast ⭐]]** — adding a second toaster (capacity), extra staff (manpower), or pre-brewed coffee (inventory) are the three levers available when the breakfast flow hits a bottleneck
