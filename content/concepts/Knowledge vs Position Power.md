# Knowledge vs Position Power

In traditional industries, decision-making authority tracks rank. In know-how businesses, technical expertise ages while position power grows—creating a structural gap between who knows and who decides.

## The Divergence
A new graduate entering a technical company arrives with current knowledge: they are among the most technically expert people in the building. As they advance, position power grows but domain expertise fades. The faster the technology changes, the wider the gap between those with authority and those with knowledge.

## Why It Matters
If position power alone governs decisions, the company makes decisions based on outdated knowledge. Intel's solution: use a decision-making process that explicitly surfaces knowledge-based input regardless of rank, enforcing egalitarianism (open floor plans, no reserved parking, informal dress) not as style but as structural necessity—status symbols suppress the flow of ideas from the people who actually know.

## The Middle Manager's Role
Middle managers are the link. They understand enough of the technology to evaluate technical input, and enough of the business to frame it for position-power holders. Getting these two types of power to mesh smoothly is one of their central functions.

## Examples
- **[[John Wayne Manager]]** — a manager who treated decision authority as a matter of individual rank, discovered the system didn't work that way, and returned to Intel after four months
