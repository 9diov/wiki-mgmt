# Interviewing

Assessing a candidate's likely future performance based on an hour or two of conversation. Harder than [[Performance Appraisal ⭐]] because there is no history with this person and the environment they are being hired into is new to them. Careful interviewing increases the odds of a good hire but guarantees nothing.

## Purpose

The interview serves four goals simultaneously:
1. Select a good performer
2. Educate the candidate about who you and the company are
3. Determine whether a mutual match exists
4. Sell the candidate on the job

## Control Through Active Listening

The candidate should do **80% of the talking**. The manager's job is to be an active listener who controls the direction of conversation. When a candidate drifts off-track, redirect immediately rather than waiting out of courtesy—the interview hour is the only asset, and wasting it is the manager's failure.

Steer conversation toward subjects familiar to *both* parties so that the manager can accurately evaluate what the candidate is saying. Ensure words mean the same thing to both sides.

## Four Categories of Information

| Category | What You're Learning |
|---|---|
| **Technical skill** | What the candidate knows about performing the job |
| **What he did with knowledge** | Past performance: what he actually accomplished |
| **Discrepancies** | Why capability and performance may have diverged |
| **Operational values** | What principles would guide his behavior on the job |

Sample questions map to each category:
- *Technical*: "Describe projects highly regarded by management" / "What are your weaknesses?"
- *Did with knowledge*: "Most significant achievements / failures?"
- *Discrepancies*: "What did you learn from your failures?" / "Problems in current position?"
- *Values*: "Why are you ready for this new job?" / "Why should we hire you?"

## Useful Techniques

**Hypothetical situations**: Walk the candidate through an unfamiliar problem and ask them to reason through it. The quality of their thinking process—not just the answer—reveals problem-solving capacity. See [[Intel Cost Accountant Interview]].

**Candidate questions**: Ask the candidate what he wants to know about you, the company, and the job. The questions he asks reveal preparation, existing knowledge, and what he values.

**Direct self-assessment**: Blunt questions ("How good are you technically?") tend to produce direct answers or revealing hesitation. Don't avoid them out of politeness.

**References**: Aim for the last ten minutes of a thirty-minute call—personal rapport takes time to develop, and candid information tends to come after the bond forms. Follow up with the candidate after checking references if new perspectives emerged.

## Avoid Tricks

Stunts designed to test character under stress (see [[Rickover Chair Trick]]) create false first impressions that take a long time to correct once the person is hired. Show the company and yourself as they really are.

## Limits

Careful interviewing increases the odds but does not eliminate risk. A thorough process can still produce a disastrous hire with no apparent explanation in retrospect.

## Examples
- **[[Intel Cost Accountant Interview]]** — Grove walked an MBA candidate through semiconductor cost accounting from scratch; candidate derived the right answer from first principles, revealing problem-solving capacity that the hire confirmed
- **[[Rickover Chair Trick]]** — Rickover's three-legged chair as cautionary contrast; tricks produce false impressions of the work environment
