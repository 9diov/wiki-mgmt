# Handling Resignation

What to do when a valued, loyal subordinate announces he is leaving—not for better pay elsewhere, but because he feels his work is unappreciated. The employee's decision reflects a failure of management, and the manager's first response in the first moments determines whether recovery is possible.

## Why It Matters

The employee is quitting because he believes he is not important to you. Everything you do from the moment of announcement either confirms or refutes that belief. Other high performers in the organization watch what happens to people like them. Their morale and commitment hinge on the outcome.

## The Sequence

**1. Drop everything immediately.**
Do not defer to a later conversation. Deferring confirms the employee's feeling that he is unimportant and makes the outcome inevitable. Sit him down now.

**2. Let him talk—don't argue.**
He has rehearsed his reasons through many sleepless nights. Let him deliver them without interruption or rebuttal. After the prepared speech is done, ask more questions—the real issues often surface only after the scripted reasons are exhausted. The goal here is not to change his mind; it is to buy time and uncover what is actually wrong.

**3. Escalate to your supervisor.**
This is a major problem. Make it your supervisor's problem too—not because you need permission, but because you need help and resources. Your supervisor will resist getting involved (it affects you more than him), so it is your responsibility to insist.

**4. Pursue every avenue, including transfer.**
The employee is valuable to the *company*, not just to you. If a transfer to another department is the right solution, become the project manager of that solution. You owe it to your employer to save the employee for the firm, even if it means giving him up to a colleague.

**5. Return with a real solution.**
Come back with something that addresses the underlying issue. Acknowledge the situation directly: "You shook us up and made us aware of the error of our ways. We are doing what we should have done without any of this happening." Reframe the employee's action as prompting a correction, not as blackmail.

**6. Handle the "I've already accepted another offer" objection.**
The employee has made two commitments: a weak one to a new employer he barely knows, and a strong one to the people he has worked with every day. Make him weigh them honestly. Ask him to quit again—the new commitment.

## What This Is Not

This process applies to an employee who feels unvalued, not one leaving for purely financial reasons. The manager has failed if a loyal, capable subordinate reaches the point of announcing resignation—but the failure is recoverable if acted on immediately.
