# Just-in-Time

One of the two pillars of the Toyota Production System. Just-in-time means that each process in a production flow receives exactly what it needs, when it needs it, in the amount it needs — no earlier, no more. A company that achieves this throughout can approach zero inventory.

## The Reversal of Production Flow

Conventional production is **push**: each earlier process makes parts according to a schedule and forwards them to the next process. This generates inventory wherever demand doesn't match supply — and it always doesn't, because plans are never perfect.

Just-in-time is **pull**: the later process goes to the earlier process to withdraw only what it needs. The earlier process then makes only what was taken. Information flows backward; materials flow forward only in response.

This reversal has a profound implication: the production schedule is no longer the driver. Actual consumption is. Nothing is produced until something is consumed.

## Why It Is Difficult

Any disruption — late parts, defective output, machine downtime, absenteeism — immediately surfaces in a JIT system, because there is no inventory buffer to absorb it. A problem upstream stops the downstream process. This is not a flaw; it is the mechanism by which problems become visible and urgent.

Ohno on the difficulty: *"An upset in prediction, a mistake in the paperwork, defective products and rework, trouble with the equipment, absenteeism — the problems are countless."* Conventional management responses (build safety stock, expedite) are exactly what JIT refuses. Instead, problems must be fixed at the source.

## Pre-conditions

JIT only works if:
- **[[Production Leveling (Heijunka)]]** is in place — demand spikes make pull systems impossible to sustain
- **[[Autonomation (Jidoka) ⭐]]** prevents defective parts from flowing to the next process
- **[[Pull System and Kanban ⭐]]** provides the physical mechanism for withdrawal and authorization

## Relation to Waste Elimination

Just-in-time is not primarily a scheduling technique. It is a waste-elimination strategy. Every item produced before it is needed is waste — it consumes material, space, and labor, and it risks becoming defective or obsolete. JIT treats inventory not as an asset but as evidence of a failure to synchronize.
