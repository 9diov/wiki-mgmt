# Autonomation (Jidoka)

The second pillar of the Toyota Production System. Autonomation — from the Japanese *jidoka* — is automation equipped with the ability to detect abnormalities and stop. It is specifically distinguished from simple automation, in which a machine runs regardless of whether it is producing good parts or bad ones.

## The Origin: Toyoda Sakichi's Loom

The concept originates with Toyoda Sakichi (1867–1930), the founder of Toyota, who invented an auto-activated loom that stopped instantly if any warp or weft thread broke. Before this invention, a broken thread produced defective fabric until an operator noticed. With the automatic stop, every stoppage was visible and immediate.

Ohno brought this principle from textiles to automobile manufacturing: build into each machine the ability to distinguish normal from abnormal, and stop on the abnormal.

## What It Enables: One Operator, Many Machines

In conventional automation, an operator must watch the machine — because if something goes wrong, the operator must intervene. With autonomation, the machine monitors itself. The operator's attention is only required when the machine stops.

This makes it possible for one operator to supervise many machines simultaneously. Under normal conditions, the operator simply monitors; when a machine stops, they diagnose and fix. Labor cost per part drops without requiring higher machine speeds.

## The Deeper Function: Surface Problems

The more important effect is cultural and managerial. When a machine stops, every manager in the area knows something is wrong. The problem cannot be hidden. It must be understood and fixed before production resumes.

Ohno: *"An old Japanese saying mentions hiding an offensively smelly object by covering it up. If materials or machines are repaired without the managing supervisor's being made aware of it, improvement will never be achieved and costs will never be reduced."*

A line that runs through defects produces a mountain of defective parts. A line that stops on the first defect produces one defective part and then a solved problem. Stopping is progress.

## The Andon Cord Extension

The same logic extends to human operators: workers are given the authority — and the responsibility — to stop the production line if they detect an abnormality. This is the andon system. A worker who spots a defect and doesn't stop the line is hiding a problem. Stopping the line is the correct action.

This requires a management culture that treats line stoppages as diagnostic events, not failures. The instinct to penalize stoppages is the instinct to bury problems.

## Relation to Just-in-Time

Autonomation and just-in-time are mutually reinforcing. JIT creates conditions where defects surface immediately (no buffer to absorb them). Autonomation ensures defects are caught at the source rather than passed downstream. Together, they create a system where abnormalities are impossible to ignore and therefore cannot persist.
