# Seven Wastes (Muda)

*Muda* (無駄) is the Japanese word for waste or futility. Ohno defines waste operationally: anything that consumes resources — time, material, labor, space — without adding value that a customer would pay for.

The seven wastes are the complete classification of how muda appears in a production system. Identifying them is the preliminary step toward elimination.

## The Seven

**1. Overproduction**
Making more than is needed, or making it before it is needed. The worst waste — it generates most of the others. Excess output becomes inventory; inventory requires storage, handling, and eventual write-off. See [[Overproduction]].

**2. Waiting**
Time when workers or machines are idle because an upstream process hasn't delivered, a machine has broken down, or a decision hasn't been made. Waiting is often invisible — an idle worker looks calm, not wasteful.

**3. Transportation**
Moving material between locations that are not adjacently arranged in process sequence. Transportation never adds value; it only moves material from where it is to where it needs to be. Excessive transportation is often the result of a plant layout organized by machine type rather than by process flow.

**4. Processing itself**
Work that is done to the product that is unnecessary — extra machining passes, over-tight tolerances, excessive inspection. The question: does this step add value the customer pays for, or is it a consequence of poor upstream quality or design?

**5. Inventory**
Stock of raw materials, WIP, or finished goods beyond the immediate need. Inventory ties up cash, occupies space, hides defects, and delays the discovery of problems. In TPS, inventory is not an asset — it is evidence of a failure to synchronize. See [[Just-in-Time ⭐]].

**6. Movement**
Unnecessary motion by workers: reaching, bending, walking between machines, searching for tools or parts. Movement is not work. A worker who walks ten steps to retrieve a part before machining it performs two operations where one is needed.

**7. Making defective products**
Producing output that must be scrapped, reworked, or returned. Defects consume all the resources of production (materials, labor, machine time) while contributing nothing to throughput. In a JIT system, defects immediately stop the downstream process — making them impossible to ignore.

## The Key Equation

Ohno's formulation: *present capacity = work + waste*.

True efficiency improvement is not running existing processes faster — it is eliminating the waste portion until all capacity is real work. Reducing manpower on a line that is running below its true capacity doesn't reduce output; it reveals that the waste was absorbing the difference all along.

## Overproduction as the Root Waste

The seven wastes are not equal. Overproduction is the primary waste because it actively generates the others: excess inventory causes transportation and movement; inventory of defectives causes defect waste; the workers and machines producing unnecessarily create their own processing and waiting wastes.

Eliminating overproduction — through just-in-time and kanban — addresses multiple wastes simultaneously. This is why Ohno treats it as the worst waste and why the pull system is the system's most important structural element.

## Muda, Mura, Muri

Ohno also identifies two related concepts alongside muda:
- **Mura** (inconsistency) — uneven production pace, variable quality, unpredictable demand signals. Mura generates muda by forcing buffers.
- **Muri** (unreasonableness) — overburdening people or machines beyond their designed capacity. Muri generates defects and breakdowns, which generate muda.

The three are addressed together: eliminate muri and mura to eliminate muda sustainably.
