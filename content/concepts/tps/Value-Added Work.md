# Value-Added Work

Every action a worker takes in a production process falls into one of three categories. The goal of TPS improvement activity is to raise the proportion of value-added work toward 100%.

## The Three Levels

**Value-added work**
Work that physically transforms the product — changes its shape, character, or composition in a way the customer values. Examples: forging, welding, assembling components, painting bodies, tempering gears. This is the only category that justifies the existence of the step.

**Non-value-added but necessary work**
Actions required under current conditions but that add no value to the product: walking to retrieve parts, opening supply packages, pressing machine start buttons, repositioning a workpiece. A customer would not pay for these steps if they knew they existed. They cannot be eliminated immediately because of current layout, tooling, or equipment constraints — but they are targets for future elimination through process redesign.

**Waste (muda)**
Unnecessary, repetitious motion that should be eliminated immediately: waiting for a machine to complete, stacking subassemblies that are not yet needed, producing parts ahead of the downstream process's demand. See [[Seven Wastes (Muda) ⭐]].

## The Ratio Problem

Ohno's observation: the ratio of truly value-added work in most production operations is far lower than managers believe. Workers appear busy — but close observation reveals that much of the motion is repositioning, waiting, retrieving, correcting, or producing ahead.

*"Moving is not necessarily working. Working means actually advancing the process toward completing the job."*

## Manpower Reduction Through Ratio Improvement

True manpower reduction does not mean asking workers to move faster. It means redesigning the work so that a smaller number of workers perform the same total value-added content — because the non-value-added and waste categories have been eliminated.

If a ten-person line has 50% value-added ratio, it is doing five people's worth of actual work. Eliminating waste and reducing non-value-added activities until the ratio reaches 80% allows the same output with six people — not by working harder but by eliminating what was not work in the first place.

## Observing the Ratio

To assess the value-added ratio, Ohno's method is direct observation: stand in the work area, watch each worker's motion, and classify every action. This cannot be done from a desk or from standard production reports. The classification only becomes visible when someone stands on the floor and watches.

This is why Ohno's plant-first principle (Chapter 2) is not optional: without direct observation, the waste is invisible and improvement targets are guesswork.
