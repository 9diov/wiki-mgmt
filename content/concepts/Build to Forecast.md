# Build to Forecast

Producing output in anticipation of future orders rather than waiting for an actual order to arrive.

## Why It's Necessary
When manufacturing throughput times are longer than customers are willing to wait, building to order is not an option. You must commit resources based on a forecast of future demand.

Intel builds to forecast because customers expect timely delivery, even though fabrication cycles are long. The same applies to college recruiting (graduates are available only seasonally) and software products (developed for an anticipated market, not a specific order).

## The Risk
A forecast is a bet. If demand doesn't materialize—or materializes for a different product—you're left with unwanted inventory. The manufacturer has already consumed capital with no corresponding revenue.

## Two Parallel Flows
Delivering a forecasted product requires two flows to converge simultaneously:
1. **Manufacturing flow**: raw material → production stages → finished goods warehouse
2. **Sales flow**: prospect identification → selling → customer order

Ideally, the customer order and the finished product arrive at the shipping dock at the same time. In practice, neither flow is perfectly predictable, so deliberate slack (inventory) is built in to absorb mismatch.

## Managing Forecast Accuracy
- Both manufacturing and sales should produce their own forecasts, so each is accountable to their own predictions
- Use **stagger charts** to track forecast drift over time; variance between successive forecasts is the most valuable signal of underlying business trend
- Hold inventory at the **lowest-value stage** to maximize flexibility at minimum cost
