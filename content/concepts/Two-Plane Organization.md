# Two-Plane Organization

A person's organizational life cannot always be represented on a single chart. They may belong to multiple planes simultaneously—each a coherent hierarchy with its own purpose—without conflict between them.

## The Planes

**Operating plane**: Where day-to-day work happens. Clear reporting relationships, direct supervisors, observable daily performance. This is the primary plane; most people spend most of their time here.

**Coordinating plane**: Where cross-unit problems are solved. Peer groups, coordinating committees, standards bodies. These exist to handle issues that span multiple operating units—issues that no single operating manager can resolve alone.

**Task force plane**: Temporary. Formed for a specific purpose, dissolved when done. Handles problems that neither the operating nor the coordinating plane is structured to address.

## The Key Insight: Planes Don't Conflict
Membership on multiple planes is like simultaneously belonging to a company, a church, and a volunteer organization. Each has its own hierarchy; each has separate purposes; neither interferes with the other. A person who belongs to Intel and also sits on a process coordinating group simply has two separate organizational memberships, each operating on its own plane.

## Plane Reversal
In multi-plane organizations, a senior person in the operating plane may be a peer or subordinate in a coordinating plane. Grove, as Intel's president, reports to a division controller (his operating subordinate) who chairs a strategic planning group. This is not a contradiction—it is a feature: it enables the best-qualified person to lead each group regardless of rank.

## Leverage for Know-How Managers
Coordinating planes multiply a specialist's leverage. Cindy the process engineer influences one plant on the operating plane; on the coordinating plane, she helps set process standards for *all* plants. The two-plane structure gives her know-how organization-wide reach without reorganizing her formal job.

## Transitory Teams
Many coordinating groups are temporary—task forces formed for a purpose, informal groups that dissolve when the problem is solved. In fast-moving businesses where formal organization can't shift fast enough to match technology and market change, transitory teams on temporary planes are the primary adaptive mechanism.
