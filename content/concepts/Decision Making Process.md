# Decision Making Process

The ideal model for making decisions in a know-how business runs through three stages, each with a predictable failure mode.

## Stage 1: Free Discussion
All points of view and facts are openly aired. The word *free* matters most when disagreement is high—precisely when participants are most tempted to wait and see which side is winning before speaking. Withholding opinion or information degrades the quality of whatever decision follows.

## Stage 2: Clear Decision
A decision is reached and framed with precision. The word *clear* matters most when the decision is controversial—precisely when the temptation is to obscure the outcome to avoid conflict. Vague decisions don't prevent arguments; they defer them, usually at greater cost.

## Stage 3: Full Support
Every participant commits to back the decision—even those who disagree. Agreement is not required; commitment is. An organization doesn't survive on consensus; it survives on people supporting the direction of the business even when they'd have chosen differently.

## Timing
The most common error is intervening too early (before all views have been heard) or too late (allowing discussion to circle indefinitely). The senior person must read the room: push for a decision when all sides have genuinely been heard, and step in to decide if consensus cannot be reached.

## Decision at the Lowest Competent Level
Decisions should be made by people closest to the situation who have both technical knowledge *and* the judgment that comes from experience. When no single person has both, assemble the best available mix—and ensure everyone participates as an equal during free discussion regardless of rank.
