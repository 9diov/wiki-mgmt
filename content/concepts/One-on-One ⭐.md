# One-on-One

A regularly scheduled meeting between a supervisor and a single subordinate. The principal mechanism for maintaining their working relationship and the primary vehicle for mutual teaching and information exchange.

## Purpose

Two-way: the subordinate gives the supervisor detailed ground-level information; the supervisor coaches, imparts values and methods, and helps the subordinate work through problems. Neither can get this from any other interaction type.

## The Subordinate Owns It

The one-on-one should be regarded as the *subordinate's* meeting. They set the agenda and prepare an outline in advance. Reasons:
- A supervisor with eight subordinates would otherwise prepare eight agendas; each subordinate prepares only one
- Preparing an outline forces the subordinate to think through their issues in advance
- The outline lets the supervisor gauge the pace needed for each item

Typical agenda items: performance indicators, current hiring/people/org problems, future plans, and—critically—*potential* problems and nagging concerns that haven't fully surfaced yet.

## Frequency and Duration

Frequency should match the subordinate's **[[Task Relevant Maturity ⭐]]** for their current work:
- Inexperienced on a specific task: weekly
- Experienced veteran: every few weeks
- Fast-changing domains (e.g. marketing): more frequent regardless of maturity

Duration: at minimum one hour. Less than that pressures the subordinate to avoid complex issues that need time to surface.

Location: in or near the subordinate's workspace when possible—the supervisor learns from seeing the environment firsthand.

## Note-Taking as Commitment

Both parties should take notes. Notes keep attention focused, aid digestion, and—crucially—a subordinate writing down a follow-up action immediately after agreeing to it acts as a handshake commitment. The supervisor can follow up at the next meeting.

## The "Hold" File

Both supervisor and subordinate keep a file of items that are important but not urgent enough to warrant an interrupt. Reviewing these at the one-on-one applies the batching principle and eliminates ad hoc contact (calls, drop-ins) for non-urgent matters.

## Leverage

90 minutes of supervisor time every two weeks can improve the quality of a subordinate's work for 80+ hours—an exceptionally high-leverage investment. The mechanism is the development of a shared information base and aligned ways of handling things, which is the prerequisite for effective [[Delegation ⭐]].

## Examples
- **[[Intel Sales One-on-One]]** — 90 minutes with Grove's sales head produced a major strategic posture shift that cascaded across all business groups; high-leverage information-sharing through a common base
- **[[Grove Learning from Subordinates]]** — Grove's subordinates taught him memory design and manufacturing; established Intel's one-on-one culture and shows that information flow in a one-on-one runs both directions
