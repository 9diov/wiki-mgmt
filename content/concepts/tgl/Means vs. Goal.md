# Means vs. Goal

A critical distinction Rogo draws in Chapter 5: the things a manufacturing organization *must do well* are not the same as what it *exists to accomplish*. Confusing the two is the root of most bad management decisions.

## The List of Means

Rogo writes out everything people treat as "the goal" and recognizes them all as means:

| Treated as goal | Why it's actually a means |
|---|---|
| Cost-effective purchasing | Enables lower production cost → more money |
| Employing good people | Enables capable execution → more money |
| High technology | Enables competitive products → more money |
| Producing quality products | Enables customer satisfaction → more money |
| Capturing market share | Enables revenue growth → more money |
| Customer satisfaction | Enables repeat sales → more money |

Every item on the list has value *only insofar as it contributes to making money*. A company can excel at all of them and still fail — which is exactly the situation at Rogo's plant.

## The Test

If an action or objective passes this test, it's a means:
> *"Does this matter even if the company isn't making money?"*

If the answer is no — if quality, efficiency, or technology becomes irrelevant in the absence of profitability — then it's a means, not the goal.

## Why the Confusion Is Costly

When means are mistaken for goals, the organization:
- Optimizes local metrics (efficiency, utilization) that don't connect to the actual goal
- Defends activities that feel productive but move the company away from making money
- Loses the ability to make trade-offs, because everything looks like an equal priority

The [[Local Optimization Trap]] is, in part, a consequence of this confusion: high utilization and efficiency are treated as ends in themselves, so machines run constantly generating inventory that clogs the system and prevents shipments — the one activity that actually produces revenue.
