# The Goal

Every organization has one true goal. Productivity, efficiency, and every other operational metric are only meaningful insofar as they move the company closer to that goal.

## Jonah's Formulation

> *"Productivity is meaningless unless you know what your goal is. And, by the way, there is only one goal, no matter what the company."*

The implication is radical: most managers are optimizing against proxies (efficiency ratios, cost per part, utilization rates) without ever asking whether those proxies actually connect to what the company is supposed to accomplish.

## Why It Matters

Without a clear goal:
- Local improvements look like wins even when the system deteriorates
- Metrics multiply (quality, cost, delivery, morale) because no one can agree what actually counts
- Managers mistake *activity* for *progress*

Rogo's plant runs high utilization, high efficiency, near-full capacity — and loses money while shipping late. The measurements aren't wrong in themselves; they're just measuring the wrong things, because no one has anchored them to the goal.

## What the Goal Is

**The goal of a manufacturing organization — and any for-profit business — is to make money.**

Rogo arrives at this in Chapter 5 by elimination. He works through every candidate (purchasing efficiency, employment, production, quality, technology, market share, sales) and finds that each one either breaks down under a counterexample or only matters *because* it enables money to be made. None of them can substitute for money as the organizing purpose.

All other operational objectives — quality, efficiency, customer satisfaction, technology — are [[Means vs. Goal|means, not the goal]]. They are necessary conditions for making money, but they are not the goal itself. A company with a quality product, high efficiency, and industry-leading technology that isn't making money is still failing.
