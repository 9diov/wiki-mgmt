# Balance Flow, Not Capacity

Jonah's first rule for managing a plant with bottlenecks:

> **Balance flow, not capacity.**

## What It Means

The conventional approach — matching every resource's capacity to market demand — is the [[Balanced Plant Fallacy]]. It fails because dependent events and statistical fluctuations cause a balanced system to produce falling throughput and rising inventory.

The correct target is not capacity balance but *flow* balance: the rate at which finished product moves through the plant and out to the market should match demand. The bottleneck governs that flow rate. Everything else should be managed to support it.

Practically: the bottleneck should run at a rate equal to (or just slightly less than) market demand. Non-bottlenecks should run at whatever rate keeps the bottleneck fed — not at their own maximum capacity.

## Why "Slightly Less Than" Demand

If flow equals demand exactly and demand drops, the plant loses money immediately. A small buffer below demand provides flexibility. The goal is reliable flow, not maximum utilization.

## The Shift in Management Question

| Old question | New question |
|---|---|
| How much can each resource produce? | How fast should product flow through the system? |
| Is each machine fully utilized? | Is the bottleneck running at the right rate? |
| Are all departments hitting their quotas? | Are we shipping at the rate the market demands? |

## Relationship to Bottleneck Management

Once the bottleneck is identified, "balance flow" means:
- The bottleneck sets the drumbeat for the entire plant
- All upstream operations feed the bottleneck; all downstream operations process what the bottleneck releases
- Non-bottlenecks should not run faster than the bottleneck can absorb — doing so only builds inventory
