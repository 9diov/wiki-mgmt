# Productivity

Goldratt's redefinition: productivity is not efficiency, utilization, or cost reduction. It is the act of bringing a company closer to its goal.

## The Definition

> *"Every action that brings a company closer to its goal is productive. Every action that does not bring a company closer to its goal is not productive."*

This reframes every operational decision. The question is never "did we improve this metric?" but "did this move the company toward its goal?"

## Why the Conventional Definition Fails

Rogo's plant illustrates the trap. By standard measures, the plant is performing well:
- Robot-equipped department: 36% efficiency improvement
- Overall efficiencies above 90%
- Cost per part reduced

Yet the plant is losing money and ships nothing on time. Jonah diagnoses this with three questions:

1. **Did you ship more products per day?** No.
2. **Did you reduce headcount?** No — people were reassigned, not eliminated.
3. **Did inventories go down?** No — they went up.

If none of these changed, the local improvement did not increase system-level productivity. The 36% gain was *efficiency theater* — real at the workstation, invisible at the shipping dock.

## The Measurement Trap

High local efficiency creates the illusion of productivity while actively harming the system:
- Keeping every machine busy generates work-in-process inventory
- Inventory piles up at downstream constraints
- The plant becomes slower and more expensive, not faster and cheaper

The measurements aren't being falsified — they're measuring exactly what they're designed to measure. The problem is that nobody asked whether those measurements connect to the goal.
