# Financial Measurements

To know whether a company is achieving its goal (making money), you need exactly three measurements. Each captures a dimension the others miss.

## The Three Measurements

**Net profit** — the absolute amount of money made after expenses. Answers: *did we make money?* But alone it's insufficient — a $10M profit is great on a $1M investment and terrible on a $1B one.

**Return on investment (ROI)** — money made relative to money invested. Answers: *how efficiently are we using capital?* But a company can show positive net profit and good ROI and still go bankrupt.

**Cash flow** — money coming in versus money going out, period by period. The survival line: *can we meet our obligations?* A company below zero on cash flow fails regardless of its profit or ROI figures.

## Why All Three Are Necessary

Each measurement can be gamed in isolation:
- Net profit can be inflated by cutting R&D (real profit shifted to future years)
- ROI can be improved by shedding assets
- Cash flow can be managed by delaying payments

The goal is not to optimize any one of them but to **increase all three simultaneously**. A company that does this is genuinely making money — not playing accounting games.

## The Problem They Don't Solve

Net profit, ROI, and cash flow are the right measurements for the organization as a whole, but they don't translate to operational decisions. They can't tell a plant supervisor whether to run a particular batch, prioritize a setup, or reassign workers. That's the gap [[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐]] is designed to fill.
