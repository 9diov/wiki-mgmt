# Five Focusing Steps

The explicit process that the Bearington team extracted from what they had been doing intuitively. Derived in chapter 36 by reconstructing each major decision the plant made.

## The Steps

**1. IDENTIFY the system's constraint(s).**
Find the weakest link. In a physical plant, this is the resource with the least capacity relative to demand — the bottleneck. At higher levels of organization, it is the policy, measurement, or assumption that limits the system's performance most. The S in "constraint(s)" acknowledges that complex organizations may have a few independent chains, each with its own constraint.

**2. Decide how to EXPLOIT the system's constraint(s).**
Get the most from what you have before spending more. For a machine bottleneck: eliminate idle time, stop defective parts from consuming it, remove unnecessary work from it. For a policy constraint: apply it only where it's needed; stop letting the bad rule govern decisions where it has no legitimate use.

**3. SUBORDINATE everything else to the above decision.**
Every non-constraint in the system should be governed by the constraint's requirements, not its own internal logic. Non-bottleneck machines should not run at full capacity; material should not be released faster than the bottleneck can absorb it; schedules should be built from the bottleneck's drum, not from each work center's preference.

**4. ELEVATE the system's constraint(s).**
If exploiting the constraint is not enough, add more of whatever is scarce: buy more machine time, add a shift, bring in a sister plant's resources, change the policy. Elevation involves cost; it should follow exploitation, not replace it.

**5. WARNING: If in a previous step a constraint has been broken, go back to step 1, but do not allow INERTIA to cause a system's constraint.**
When a constraint is broken, the system changes. A new weakest link will emerge — possibly in a completely different part of the system. Rules and procedures put in place for the old constraint may now be obstacles. The warning is the most important addition to the original four steps: your own previous solutions can become the next constraint.

## The Chain Analogy

Lou's formulation: every organization is a chain (or a grid of chains). Throughput is the output of the entire chain. The strength of a chain is determined by its weakest link. Managing every link equally (the cost world) is therefore wrong: the constraint sets the ceiling for the whole system, not the average link.

## Cost World vs. Throughput World

The five steps are only coherent in the throughput world. In the cost world, improvement means finding cost savings anywhere — which distributes effort uniformly across all links. In the throughput world, improvement means focusing on the constraint — which concentrates effort on one link at a time.

Rogo's team's results: throughput and inventory changed by tens of percent; operating expenses moved by less than 2%. The five-step process made no attempt to optimize operating expense. It focused on the constraint, elevated it, found the next one, and repeated.

## Applicability Beyond Physical Constraints

The team recognized that even in the plant, the real constraints were never the machines. The NCX-10 and the oven were indicators of where the constraint was — but the underlying constraints were the policies: efficiency incentives, batch-size rules, quality placement, material release logic. Machines were changed; the real work was changing the rules around them.

At the division level, no visible machine constraint exists. All constraints are policies. The five steps still apply, but step 1 ("identify") now requires a method for finding the root policy that causes all the visible bad effects — which Goldratt later developed into the Thinking Processes (Current Reality Tree, Conflict Resolution Diagram, Future Reality Tree).
