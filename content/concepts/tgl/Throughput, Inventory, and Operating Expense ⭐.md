# Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense

Jonah's three operational measurements, designed to express the goal of making money in terms that generate actionable rules for running a plant. They cover everything a manager manages.

## The Definitions

**Throughput (T)** — the rate at which the system generates money *through sales*.
- Key word: *sales*, not production. Producing something that sits in a warehouse does not count as throughput.
- This distinction directly challenges the conventional factory incentive to keep machines running and push output — production without sales is not throughput.

**Inventory (I)** — all the money the system has invested in purchasing things it intends to sell.
- Includes raw materials, work-in-process, and finished goods.
- Does *not* include the value added by labor — that goes into Operating Expense instead. This eliminates the accounting ambiguity over whether labor in a product is an asset or an expense.

**Operating Expense (OE)** — all the money the system spends to turn inventory into throughput.
- Includes direct labor, indirect labor, utilities, depreciation — everything spent to make the operation run.

## The Simplest Summary

Lou, Rogo's plant controller, offers the most concise framing:

> *"Throughput is the money coming in. Inventory is the money currently inside the system. And operational expense is the money we have to pay out to make throughput happen."*

One measurement for incoming money, one for money stuck inside, one for money going out. The direction each should move is immediate: T up, I down, OE down.

## Expressing the Goal

The goal (make money) can be restated as:
> Increase throughput while simultaneously reducing inventory and operating expense.

Or more precisely: actions that **increase T** and/or **decrease I and OE** move the company toward its goal. Actions that decrease T or increase I/OE move away from it.

This gives a direct test for any operational decision — something the [[Financial Measurements|financial measurements]] (net profit, ROI, cash flow) cannot provide at the plant floor level.

## Why the Definitions Are Non-Standard

Conventional accounting treats labor as part of the "value" added to inventory (work-in-process is carried at cost including labor). Goldratt rejects this because it creates a perverse incentive: producing and stockpiling inventory *increases* the accounting value of assets, making a plant look more productive even as it consumes cash and clogs the system.

By treating labor as Operating Expense rather than embedded inventory value, the T/I/OE framework removes that incentive. Inventory is just the money tied up in material — period.

## Relationship to Local Optimization

The [[Local Optimization Trap]] exists precisely because conventional metrics optimize local T, I, or OE without asking whether the *system* as a whole is moving toward the goal. Jonah explicitly warns Rogo: "We are not concerned with local optimums." The three measurements always apply to the organization as a whole.
