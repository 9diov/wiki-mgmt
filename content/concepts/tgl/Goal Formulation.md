# Goal Formulation

The goal of making money, expressed through T/I/OE as a decision rule for daily operations:

> **Increase throughput while simultaneously reducing inventory and operating expense.**

## Why This Formulation Matters

"Make money" is the goal but it's too abstract to guide a shift supervisor's decisions. Net profit, ROI, and cash flow are the right board-level measurements but they don't translate to the plant floor either.

The T/I/OE formulation bridges the two levels. It answers the practical question: *is this specific action moving us toward the goal or away from it?*

| Action | Effect on T/I/OE | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Ship a backlogged order | T↑ | Productive |
| Release excess material to keep robots busy | I↑, OE↑, T→ | Not productive |
| Reduce batch sizes to free up capital | I↓ | Potentially productive |
| Hire workers for a non-bottleneck | OE↑, T→ | Not productive |

## Priority Order

When trade-offs arise, Goldratt argues the measurements have an implicit priority:

1. **Throughput first** — the ability to generate revenue has no ceiling; increasing T is the primary lever
2. **Inventory second** — tied-up capital has direct costs and hides problems
3. **Operating expense third** — cutting OE has a floor (you can't cut below zero); focus here tends to produce local optimizations

This ordering challenges the conventional management instinct, which focuses on cost reduction (OE) first and treats revenue growth as secondary.

## The Test for Any Decision

Before any operational choice, ask:
- Does this increase throughput (through sales)?
- Does this reduce inventory?
- Does this reduce operating expense?

If none of the three improve — or if one worsens while others don't improve enough to compensate — the action is moving away from the goal, regardless of what local efficiency metrics say.
