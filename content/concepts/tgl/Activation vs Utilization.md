# Activation vs Utilization

> **Activating** a resource means pressing the ON switch — the resource runs and produces output regardless of whether that output will ever become throughput.
>
> **Utilizing** a resource means using it in a way that moves the system toward the goal.

These are not the same thing. A non-bottleneck that produces parts faster than the constraint can process them is activated but not utilized. Its output becomes inventory, not throughput.

## The Non-Bottleneck Rule

From Jonah's four-case analysis of bottleneck (X) and non-bottleneck (Y) combinations:

> **The level of utilization of a non-bottleneck is not determined by its own potential, but by some other constraint in the system.**

A non-bottleneck has more capacity than the system needs from it. If it runs at 100%, it produces more than the constraint can absorb. The excess has nowhere to go except inventory.

The correct utilization rate for a non-bottleneck is whatever rate keeps the constraint fed — no more. That rate is determined by the constraint's capacity, not the non-bottleneck's capacity.

## Why This Matters

Standard management thinking equates activity with productivity. If a machine is running, it is "working." If a worker is busy, they are contributing. This logic is applied to set efficiency targets (90%, 95%) that are measured and rewarded.

But in a system with a constraint, a non-bottleneck running at 95% may be producing inventory at 95% of maximum speed while adding zero to throughput. The efficiency metric looks excellent. The system gets worse.

## What Correct Non-Bottleneck Behavior Looks Like

- A non-bottleneck that finishes its share of demand and sits idle is **working correctly**
- A non-bottleneck that is starved for parts (because X upstream is the bottleneck) is **working correctly**
- A non-bottleneck that runs continuously is **probably creating inventory**, unless it has been confirmed that 100% of its output will be absorbed by the system as throughput

Idle time at a non-bottleneck is not a problem to be solved. It is the natural consequence of a system properly subordinated to its constraint.

## The Management Trap

The incentive to keep workers busy is rational at the individual level — idle workers look wasteful and managers are measured on utilization. But at the system level, keeping non-bottlenecks activated beyond what the constraint can absorb is:

1. The direct cause of excess inventory accumulation
2. The mechanism by which non-bottlenecks become *false* bottlenecks (overloaded by excessive releases of material)
3. A violation of the goal (excess inventory is against the goal, by the T/I/OE definition)

*"Activating a non-bottleneck to its maximum is an act of maximum stupidity."* — Jonah
