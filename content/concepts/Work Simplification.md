# Work Simplification

A structured method for improving productivity by reducing the number of steps in any production or administrative process.

## The Process
1. **Create a complete flow chart** of the process as it actually exists. Include every step—no omissions to make things look cleaner than they are.
2. **Count the steps.**
3. **Set a reduction target.** In practice, a first round of simplification typically yields a 30–50% reduction.
4. **Question every step**: Why does this step exist? What would happen if we removed it? Many steps persist by tradition or formal procedure with no practical justification.
5. **Eliminate** any step that cannot justify its existence under scrutiny.

## Why Steps Accumulate
Steps are added over time for reasons that once seemed valid. They persist because:
- Nobody questions whether they're still needed
- Removing them feels risky even when they add no value
- Tradition and procedure have inertia

## Application Beyond Manufacturing
Work simplification originated in industrial engineering for widget factories, but applies equally—and is *underused*—in administrative, professional, and managerial work. The main obstacle in "soft" professions is defining what the output is. Without a clear output definition, you can't distinguish between steps that contribute to it and steps that don't.

The U.S. Embassy visa processing case is an example: processing 100% of applicants was an unreflective default. A sampling approach could have processed the same volume more efficiently without questioning a single tradition—just by asking what the actual output requirement was.

## Examples
- **[[US Embassy Visa Processing]]** — processing every visa applicant individually was an unreflective default; applying a sampling approach would eliminate steps and reduce friction without increasing error rates
