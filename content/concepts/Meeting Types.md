# Meeting Types

Meetings come in two fundamental kinds, each with a different purpose, structure, and ideal frequency.

## Process-Oriented Meetings

Held on a regular schedule. Their purpose is to share knowledge, exchange information, and keep supervisor and subordinates aligned. Because they recur predictably, attendees can prepare in advance and the meetings can be batched—using a single mental setup to handle many similar transactions.

Three types at Intel:
- **[[One-on-One ⭐]]**: supervisor + one subordinate; mutual teaching and information exchange
- **[[Staff Meeting]]**: supervisor + all direct subordinates; peer interaction and group problem-solving
- **[[Operation Review]]**: cross-level forum; junior managers present to senior reviewers they don't normally interact with

## Mission-Oriented Meetings

Held ad hoc to produce a specific output—almost always a decision. Cannot be scheduled far in advance because the triggering problem is unpredictable. Success depends on the chairman, who must clarify the objective, assemble the right people (maximum 6–8 for a decision-making meeting), and send clear minutes afterward.

## The 25% Rule

Ideally, all routine issues are handled in process-oriented meetings and mission-oriented meetings are the exception. Grove's reframe of Drucker's rule: it's not total meeting time that signals malorganization—it's spending more than 25% of time in *ad hoc, mission-oriented* meetings. That indicates the organization isn't catching problems early enough in its regular cadence.

## Meetings as Medium

A meeting is not an activity—it is a medium through which managerial activities (information-gathering, information-giving, decision-making, nudging) are performed. Reducing meetings doesn't reduce managerial work; it just forces that work into less efficient channels.

## Examples
- **[[Philippines Plant Location Meeting]]** — a well-run mission-oriented meeting: clear objective (decide a location), roles pre-assigned, agenda sent in advance, structured time blocks
- **[[Meeting Cost Calculation]]** — 10 managers × 2 hours = $2,000 in managerial time; illustrates meetings as resource allocation decisions requiring the same discipline as capital expenditures
