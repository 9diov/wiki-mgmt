# Performance Appraisal

The single most important form of task-relevant feedback a manager can provide. A review influences a subordinate's performance—positively or negatively—for a long time, making it one of the manager's highest-leverage activities.

## Purpose

The fundamental purpose is to **improve the subordinate's future performance**—not to cleanse the supervisor's observations, justify raises, or achieve psychological comfort. It does this through two lenses:
1. **Skill level**: What is missing? How can it be remedied?
2. **Motivation**: How can the subordinate be moved to a higher performance curve for the same skill level?

The review is also the only formal moment where a manager is mandated by the organization to act as judge and deliver that judgment face to face.

## Assessing Performance

**Output measures vs. internal measures**
Output measures are tangible and chartable: sales quota, yield, completed designs. Internal measures capture how the work is being done and what is being set up for the future: team health, pipeline quality, people development. Neither alone is sufficient. The right weighting (50/50, 90/10, or otherwise) depends on the situation and may shift month to month.

**The time offset**
There is often a lag between a subordinate's activity and the output it produces. A manager may look excellent on output indicators while current work is quietly deteriorating—or vice versa. Supervisors must judge the work *performed during the review period*, not just read the lagging output indicators. When internal measures and output measures conflict, the internal measures are often more current and should be trusted.

**The potential trap**
The most common error is rating *potential* (form, polish, style) rather than actual output. A manager's performance rating cannot be higher than the rating accorded to his organization's results. Promoting or rating highly on appearance signals to the whole company that one needs only to *act* like a good manager—not perform like one.

## Delivering the Assessment — The Three Ls

**Level**: Be totally frank. Credibility of the entire review system depends on it. Praising someone clearly can be just as hard as criticizing without embarrassment—both require the same directness.

**Listen**: Use all sensory capabilities—watch verbal and nonverbal responses—to ensure the message is actually *received*, not just transmitted. If the subordinate is emotional, defensive, or checked out, the review is wasted. It is the supervisor's responsibility to keep at it until satisfied the message has landed.

**Leave yourself out**: The review is for and about the subordinate. The supervisor's own insecurities, guilt, and anxieties must be suppressed. This is the subordinate's day in court.

## Review Types

**"On the one hand, on the other hand"**: Mixed positive/negative. Key pitfall is overwhelming the subordinate with too many messages. Prepare a worksheet: list everything, then cluster related items into a small number of messages the subordinate can actually absorb and remember.

**The Blast**: A serious performance problem that, unless addressed, could end in termination. The subordinate will likely move through stages: ignore → actively deny → blame others → assume responsibility → find the solution. The supervisor's job is to get the subordinate to the assumption-of-responsibility stage; only then is finding a solution tractable. See [[Conflict Resolution Stages]].

**Reviewing the Ace**: High performers tend to receive retrospective reviews focused on justifying the rating rather than identifying how they could do better. This reverses the right priority—stars account for a disproportionate share of output, so improving their performance is highest-leverage.

## Commitment Over Agreement

When the subordinate does not agree with the assessment, the supervisor does not need agreement—only commitment to a course of action. Insisting on agreement is about psychological comfort, not operational need. Any outcome that includes genuine commitment to change is acceptable. If the subordinate reaches a deadlock at the blame-others stage, the supervisor must invoke position power: "I want you to do this"—and secure a commitment, then monitor against it.

## Delivery Logistics

Give the written review to the subordinate *before* the face-to-face discussion. This allows private reading, emotional reaction, and digestion. By the time the formal meeting occurs, both parties are better prepared and the conversation is more substantive.

## Examples
- **[[Time Offset Review ⭐]]** — Grove gave an excellent rating based on strong output indicators while ignoring internal warning signs; the next year's collapse revealed the rating had been wrong; illustrates the time offset between activity and output
- **[[Intel Potential Trap]]** — a general manager rated highly despite his unit missing every target; his supervisor cited the manager's personal qualities, not his organization's output; Grove rejected the review
- **[[Superficial Review Rewrite]]** — Grove's subordinate disagreed that rewriting a shallow review was worthwhile but committed to do it anyway; illustrates that commitment to action, not agreement, is the operational goal
