# Training

A manager has two levers for raising subordinate performance: increasing motivation and increasing individual capability. Training is the primary vehicle for the second. It is one of the highest-leverage activities a manager can perform—yet it is routinely delegated to people who can't do it effectively.

## Why the Manager Must Do It

**Only the manager can be an authentic role model.** Training delivered by an outside specialist or a training department may be technically correct, but it lacks the authority of someone who is seen practicing what they teach. The person in front of the class must be a believable, practicing authority on the subject.

**Training must match actual practice.** Generic or externally-designed courses often teach methods that conflict with how the organization actually operates. When there is a gap between what is taught and what is practiced, participants become confused and demoralized. See [[Intel Career Development Course]].

**Training is how a manager extends his output.** The manager's output is the output of his organization. Teaching subordinates better ways to work is how that output is raised—and it cannot be delegated any more than motivation can.

## The Leverage Calculation

Four one-hour lectures, prepared over twelve hours, delivered to ten students whose combined work year is roughly 20,000 hours:
- A **1% improvement** in subordinate performance = **200 hours** of additional output per year
- Cost: **12 hours** of the manager's time

Few activities available to a manager approach this return.

## Training Must Be a Process, Not an Event

Employees should be able to count on systematic, scheduled training. A rescue course summoned to fix an immediate problem is not training—it is damage control. The system only produces results if it is reliable and ongoing.

## Two Types of Training

| Type | What It Covers | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| **New-employee training** | Skills needed to perform the job | Set by hiring rate + turnover |
| **New-skill training** | New principles, methods, or tools for existing staff | Can be 5× larger; must be prioritized carefully |

## How to Start

1. List everything your subordinates should know—from practical skills to organizational values.
2. Ask subordinates what they feel they need; expect surprises.
3. Inventory available manager-teachers and materials.
4. **Start with one short course** (three to four lectures) on the most urgent topic.
5. Develop only the first lecture before committing to a schedule—then go.
6. **Treat the first run as a throwaway.** Deliver it to more experienced subordinates who will help critique it without being confused by its rough edges.
7. Develop subsequent lectures only after giving the previous one.
8. Collect anonymous feedback; iterate.

## What Teaching Does for the Teacher

Teaching requires much deeper knowledge of a task than simply doing it. Managers who design and deliver training consistently find that:
- They discover gaps in their own understanding
- The process sharpens and clarifies their own thinking about the work
- Watching a subordinate practice something they taught produces one of management's more satisfying experiences

## Examples
- **[[Restaurant Reservations Training Failure]]** — a new employee not told to inform callers about the missing liquor license; the maitre d' spent the evening apologizing to every table; illustrates cost of skipping basic job training
- **[[Intel Ion Implanter Failure ⭐]]** — operator not trained to recognize out-of-tune machine conditions; nearly $1M of wafers scrapped; illustrates the compounding cost of insufficient training in high-stakes environments
- **[[Intel Career Development Course]]** — outside consultants taught career planning methods incompatible with Intel's actual practices; participants demoralized; illustrates why training must be tied to how things are actually done
