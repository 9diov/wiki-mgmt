# Leverage

The output generated by a given managerial activity. The central measure of managerial effectiveness.

## The Formula

> Managerial Output = Σ (Leverage × Activity)

For every activity a manager performs, the organization's output increases by some amount—the leverage of that activity. High leverage means a small investment of time produces a large effect. Low leverage means the effort barely moves the needle.

## Three Ways to Increase Managerial Output
1. **Increase activity rate** — do the same activities faster
2. **Increase leverage** — shift to activities that produce more output per hour
3. **Shift the mix** — spend more time on high-leverage activities, less on low-leverage ones

Of these, shifting the mix is usually the most powerful.

## Sources of High Leverage
- **Scale**: one manager affects many people simultaneously (e.g., defining the annual planning process for 200 participants)
- **Duration**: a short action affects someone's performance over a long time (e.g., a well-delivered performance review, setting up a tracking system)
- **Unique knowledge**: a specialist whose insight unlocks the work of many others (e.g., a pricing engineer, a geologist in an oil company)

## Negative Leverage
Leverage can work against you:
- Arriving unprepared to a meeting wastes everyone's time, not just yours
- A manager's visible depression or indecisiveness spreads through an organization and is hard to reverse
- **Meddling**: overriding subordinates rather than letting them work through problems. Over time, subordinates stop taking initiative, reducing the organization's total output

## Timeliness
The same activity has vastly different leverage depending on *when* it's performed. Work done in advance of a planning process has high leverage; scrambling to fix it afterward has low leverage. Acting immediately when a valued employee is about to quit can save the relationship; waiting a week cannot.

## Examples
- **[[Robin Annual Planning]]** — defining the annual planning process for 200+ participants is high-leverage: one activity shapes the work of an entire organization
- **[[Intel Pricing Engineer]]** — a single specialist whose pricing insight unlocks decisions across multiple groups (unique-knowledge leverage)
- **[[Depressed Manager]]** — a manager's visible depression spreads through their organization; negative leverage working against output
- **[[Intel Orientation Lecture]]** — one lecture transmitting Intel values to hundreds of new employees simultaneously
- **[[Intel Sales One-on-One]]** — 90 minutes produced a strategic posture shift that propagated across all of Grove's business groups
- **[[Calendar as Production Tool ⭐]]** — treating the calendar as a production schedule to maximize time spent on high-leverage activities
