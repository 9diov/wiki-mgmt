# Maslow's Hierarchy

A model of human motivation in which needs organize into a hierarchy. When a lower need is satisfied, a higher one takes over as the dominant motivator. A satisfied need no longer motivates—which means the source of motivation must always involve some unsatisfied need.

## The Hierarchy

**Physiological**: Basic necessities—food, shelter, clothing. Money at this level buys survival. Fear of deprivation is the motivator. Historical motivation by punishment operated here.

**Safety/Security**: Protection from sliding back to physiological deprivation. Medical benefits, job security. Often not a dominant motivator when present, but their absence degrades performance.

**Social/Affiliation**: The need to belong to a group whose members share something in common. Powerful enough to override pay differences—people take lower-paying jobs to be around a group they identify with.

**Esteem/Recognition**: The need for acknowledgment from people whose opinion matters to you. Highly relative: esteem is in the eyes of the beholder. A high-school athlete values recognition from a top player; their family doesn't understand why.

**Self-Actualization**: The need to become what one is capable of becoming. "What I can be, I must be." The only need that is *not* self-limiting—once a person is operating here, there is no ceiling on their drive. Two paths:
- *Competence-driven*: mastery for its own sake; the violinist who practices endlessly to play better, not to impress anyone
- *Achievement-driven*: testing the outer limits of capability in all tasks; spontaneously seeks the point where success is uncertain

## The Key Property: Self-Limiting vs. Unlimited
All needs below self-actualization extinguish themselves when satisfied. A promotion that was the goal of a career suddenly feels empty once attained. Self-actualization is unique: achieving more feeds the drive for more, rather than satisfying it.

## Money as Motivator and Measure
- At physiological/security levels: money motivates by buying needs; stops motivating once the need is met
- At esteem/self-actualization levels: money is a *measure of achievement*, not a means to material ends. The venture capitalist working for a second $10M is driven by what it means, not what it buys.

A simple test: if the *absolute size* of a raise matters most, the person is in the lower levels. If what matters is *how their raise compares to others*, they are in esteem or self-actualization mode—money is a scorecard.

## Fear at the Upper Levels
At self-actualization, fear shifts from fear of punishment to fear of *not satisfying yourself*—fear of failure. This can spur performance or paralyze it. A person too preoccupied with failure will play it safe, like the ring tosser who stands directly over the peg to guarantee a score.
