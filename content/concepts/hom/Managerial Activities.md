# Managerial Activities

The four types of work a manager performs to produce output. These are the means; the organization's output is the end.

## Information Gathering
The foundation of everything else. A manager cannot make good decisions, give useful direction, or set appropriate priorities without current information.

- Most valuable source: timely, informal, verbal exchanges—faster than any written report
- Also valuable: plant/office visits (efficient for two-minute transactions that would take thirty minutes in a formal meeting), standard reports (as a safety net and archive), external conversations (customers, competitors, analysts)
- Information sources should be **redundant** and **complementary**: no single channel is sufficient

**On written reports**: their primary value is forcing the *writer* to think precisely. The act of writing is the discipline; the document itself is less important than the thinking it required.

## Information Giving
Transmitting knowledge, priorities, preferences, and values so that subordinates can make decisions independently—and make them in a way the manager would endorse.

A manager who doesn't communicate their values and priorities forces subordinates to wait for instructions on everything. A manager who does creates an organization that can operate without constant oversight.

## Decision-Making
Two kinds:
- **Forward-looking**: capital allocation, annual planning, strategic bets
- **Reactive**: responding to problems, crises, or personnel issues

For every direct decision a manager makes, they participate in many more—supplying inputs, debating alternatives, reviewing and approving or vetoing others' decisions. Participation matters as much as authorship.

## Nudging
Steering a situation in a preferred direction without issuing a direct command. A phone call suggesting a decision be made a certain way. A comment during a presentation. A note expressing how you see a situation.

Nudging is not decision-making (no firm directive) and not pure information-giving (it advocates a position). It is ubiquitous: for every formal decision, a manager nudges things a dozen times. Its power is easy to underestimate because it leaves no clear record.

## Being a Role Model
Values and behavioral norms are not transmitted effectively through memos or speeches. They are transmitted by what people *see* their manager do.

How a manager handles questions, manages their time, treats people, and responds under pressure sets the standard for everyone watching—subordinates, peers, and supervisors alike. Leadership is not a single activity; it is the aggregate of visible behavior over time.
