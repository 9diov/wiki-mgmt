# Manager's Output

A manager's output is not their individual work. It is the output of the organizations they supervise and influence.

## The Definition

> Manager's output = Output of their organization + Output of neighboring organizations under their influence

This means a manager who does brilliant individual work but whose team underperforms has not produced high output. Conversely, a manager who spends their time enabling others to work better—even on things that seem peripheral—is doing their job.

## Why This Matters
It shifts the question from "what did I do today?" to "what did my organization produce?" Activities like teaching a course, attending a meeting, writing a memo, or visiting a manufacturing floor are only valuable insofar as they increase the output of the organization.

## Know-How Managers
The definition extends beyond people with direct reports. Individual contributors who gather and disseminate critical knowledge—internal consultants, pricing analysts, technical specialists—exert influence on neighboring organizations and should be understood as middle managers. Their leverage can be enormous.

## Activities Are Not Output
Managers routinely describe their output in terms of activities: "I gave direction," "I made decisions," "I trained people." These are the *means*, not the *end*. The output is what the team produced as a result.

A surgeon's output is a healed patient. The cutting and suturing are just the activities required to get there.
