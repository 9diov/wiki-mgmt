# Conflict Resolution Stages

When a subordinate is confronted with a serious performance problem, they typically move through a predictable sequence of stages. Understanding where the subordinate is in this sequence tells the supervisor what to do next.

## The Stages

| Stage | What It Looks Like | What It Requires |
|---|---|---|
| **Ignore** | Passively acts as if no problem exists | Facts and specific examples to establish the problem's reality |
| **Actively Deny** | Explicitly rejects that a problem exists | More evidence to overcome resistance |
| **Blame Others** | Acknowledges the problem but attributes it to external forces | The hardest step: getting the subordinate to own it |
| **Assume Responsibility** | Accepts that the problem is theirs to solve | The pivotal emotional transition |
| **Find the Solution** | Works toward remedying the problem | Intellectual, not emotional — relatively tractable once responsibility is assumed |

## The Critical Transition

The move from **blaming others** to **assuming responsibility** is an emotional step. The subordinate has to say: "If it is *my* problem, I have to do something about it—and that is going to be unpleasant and a lot of work." This is why progress often stalls at the blame-others stage.

The move from assuming responsibility to finding the solution is *intellectual*, and therefore much easier once the emotional hurdle is cleared.

## The Supervisor's Role

The supervisor's job is to bring the subordinate through all stages together with them—not to leap ahead. If the supervisor tries to solve the problem while the subordinate is still in denial or blame-others, nothing can happen. Knowing which stage the conversation is in allows the supervisor to apply the right pressure at the right moment.

If the subordinate cannot move past blame-others, the supervisor must invoke position power: "As your manager, I am instructing you to do this." Secure a commitment to action and monitor against it.

## Commitment, Not Agreement

An acceptable outcome is any outcome that includes a commitment to action—even if the subordinate disagrees with the assessment. Insisting on agreement (when commitment would suffice) is about the supervisor's psychological comfort, not operational need.

- **[[Superficial Review Rewrite]]** — Grove's subordinate never agreed the rewrite was necessary but committed to do it; the result was a thorough, well-done review; illustrates that commitment is the goal
