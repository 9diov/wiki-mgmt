# Compensation as Feedback

Money serves two distinct motivational roles depending on where a person sits on [[Maslow's Hierarchy]]:
- At lower levels (physiological, safety): money is material necessity. The *absolute* amount of a raise matters.
- At higher levels (self-actualization): money is a measure of worth in a competitive environment. The *relative* amount—what you got compared to others—is what matters.

A manager's job is to use compensation as task-relevant feedback: a signal that communicates how performance is valued and guides future behavior.

## The Problem

Middle managers cannot be paid by the piece. Their contribution is woven into team output, making individual performance hard to isolate. Yet if compensation is decoupled from performance entirely, it stops being feedback—and stops affecting behavior.

## Performance Bonuses

A performance bonus is the primary vehicle for delivering compensation as feedback for managers. Design principles:

**Percentage should rise with seniority**: For senior managers, where absolute dollars matter less, the bonus should represent up to 50% of total compensation. For middle managers, 10–25% is appropriate—enough to signal significance without causing personal hardship through large fluctuations.

**Individual vs. team**: Determine whether the performance being rewarded is primarily individual, team-level (department), or company-wide. A well-designed scheme might combine all three in proportion.

**Time lag**: Bonuses should be paid close enough to the work period that the subordinate can remember why they were awarded. This is in tension with the [[Performance Appraisal ⭐]] principle that output often lags activity—but the feedback signal decays if the reward is too delayed.

**Measurable vs. subjective criteria**: Financial performance and quantifiable objectives are more defensible; subjective elements risk turning the process into a beauty contest.

No scheme is perfect. The goal is to *call attention* to performance and create a signal, not to engineer a precise measurement.

## Salary Administration

Three models for base salary:

| Model | Logic | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Experience-only** | Pay tracks tenure, regardless of performance | Signals performance doesn't matter; common in government, unions, large Japanese firms |
| **Merit-only** | Pay tracks performance, ignoring tenure | Impractical; experience is hard to ignore entirely |
| **Compromise** | Everyone starts similarly; advancement speed and ceiling differ by performance | Requires comparative ranking—someone must finish last |

The experience-only model is easiest to administer (point to the book), but signals that output is irrelevant. Any merit-based or compromise scheme requires a competitive, comparative evaluation of individuals—uncomfortable, but necessary if salary is to function as feedback.

## Promotions

Promotions are high-visibility compensation events. Because the entire organization watches who is promoted, every promotion decision communicates organizational values. Promotions must be based on performance—this is the only way to keep performance salient throughout the company.

See [[Peter Principle]] for how promotion cycles interact with performance levels over a career.

## Examples
- **[[Teacher Pay Analogy]]** — in many school systems, good and poor teachers earn the same salary based on tenure alone; Grove argues this explains the pass/fail grading system—when pay is disconnected from performance, the incentive to distinguish output disappears
