# Peter Principle

The classic Peter Principle states that people in a merit-based organization are promoted until they reach their level of incompetence, at which point they stay. Grove accepts the observation but offers a more precise and actionable version.

## Grove's Refinement

An achiever does not simply rise until broken. He alternates between two performance ratings throughout his career:

1. **Meets requirements** — when newly promoted into a harder role
2. **Exceeds requirements** — as he develops skill, experience, and motivation in that role

Every time he reaches "exceeds," he becomes a candidate for promotion. Upon promotion, he drops back to "meets" in the new, more demanding role. This cycle repeats. Eventually, he reaches a level where he settles at "meets requirements" and is promoted no further—this is his effective ceiling.

## Why This Is Not a Problem (Usually)

The alternative—leaving an "exceeds" performer in an unchallenging role—is worse. Without new demands, the person atrophies. His performance drifts back to "meets" anyway, but now at a lower job level. The organization has wasted capacity.

Two types of "meets" performer result:
- **Noncompetitor**: settled and satisfied; no drive for more
- **Competitor**: at "meets" temporarily because he was just promoted; will exceed again and be promoted again

Promoting competitors is the right call even knowing the cycle.

## The Real Problem: Over-Promotion

The system breaks down when someone is promoted into a role *far* above his capability—too large a jump, too quickly. He performs below average for too long. The typical organizational response is to push him out, rationalized as "for his own sake."

Grove argues this is wrong. Management misjudged the employee's readiness; the error belongs to management, not the employee.

## Recycling

The correct response to over-promotion is **recycling**: deliberately moving the person back to the job he did well before the over-promotion.

Why organizations avoid it:
- The employee experiences it as public failure
- Management is reluctant to acknowledge its own misjudgment

Why it works:
- The embarrassment is short-lived when handled openly and with management support
- The person returns to work they are *known* to do well
- With confidence restored, they often become strong candidates for a more carefully calibrated promotion later

The organization retains a competent, experienced contributor rather than losing them entirely.
