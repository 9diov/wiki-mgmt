# Six Decision Questions

A checklist to define the structure of a decision before it begins. Answering these in advance prevents surprise vetoes, scope confusion, and the political friction that follows when the wrong people are included or excluded.

## The Six Questions
1. **What** decision needs to be made?
2. **When** does it have to be made?
3. **Who** will decide?
4. **Who** needs to be consulted before the decision?
5. **Who** will ratify or veto the decision?
6. **Who** needs to be informed of the decision afterward?

## Why Each Matters
- *What* forces a precise scope definition, preventing drift during discussion.
- *When* works backward from implementation needs (applying time offsets) to set the decision deadline.
- *Who decides* clarifies whose meeting it is and who owns the outcome.
- *Who consults* ensures knowledge-power holders contribute input before the decision, not complaints after.
- *Who ratifies* surfaces the veto authority early, preventing a last-minute overrule that demoralizes participants.
- *Who informs* determines who receives the outcome without having been part of making it.

## The Cost of Skipping It
When a ratifier or vetoer is not identified in advance, they may legitimately overrule a decision the group worked hard to reach. Even a valid veto, arriving as a surprise, looks like political maneuvering and erodes trust in the process. Applying this structure *before the fact* is the most reliable way to keep decision-making from looking like politics. #pitfall 

## Examples
- **[[Philippines Plant Location Meeting]]** — Grove explicitly walks through all six questions for the Philippine plant expansion: what (site choice), when (within a month, working back from a two-year construction timeline), who decides (parallel managers from construction and manufacturing), who consults (their staffs), who ratifies (Grove), who informs (Gordon Moore)
