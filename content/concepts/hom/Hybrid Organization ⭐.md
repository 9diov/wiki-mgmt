# Hybrid Organization

The structural resolution of the centralization/decentralization tension: combine mission-oriented business units with functional shared-service groups, rather than choosing one pure form.

## The Two Pure Forms

**Mission-oriented (fully decentralized)**
Each unit handles all aspects of its operation independently—its own purchasing, personnel, merchandising, real estate. Maximum responsiveness to local conditions; no economies of scale; expertise cannot be shared. The *only* advantage: staying close to local needs and responding rapidly when they change.

**Functional (fully centralized)**
Each function (sales, manufacturing, finance, R&D) serves all units from one central group. Maximum economies of scale and expertise leverage; minimum local responsiveness. Disadvantages: information overload at the functional group, slow response, intense inter-unit competition for shared resources.

## Grove's Law
All large organizations with a common business purpose end up in a hybrid form. This is not a design choice—it is inevitable, because:
- The value of local responsiveness always justifies some mission-oriented structure
- The value of scale and expertise always justifies some functional centralization
- Neither pure form can serve a real business adequately on its own

## Functional Groups as Internal Subcontractors
In a hybrid, functional groups act as internal subcontractors—providing shared services (manufacturing, IT, finance, sales) to all business units, which in turn concentrate on their specific missions. At Intel, roughly two-thirds of employees work in functional groups.

**Advantages of functional groups:**
- Economies of scale: expensive equipment runs at full capacity across all units instead of sitting idle in each
- Resource flexibility: manufacturing capacity can be shifted to match corporate-wide priorities without friction
- Expert leverage: know-how specialists' expertise applies across the entire corporation

## The Hybrid's Central Challenge
The most important problem: optimum, timely allocation of shared resources and efficient resolution of conflicts over that allocation. Central allocators cannot handle the volume—the transaction load is too heavy (the Hungarian central planning failure illustrates what happens when allocation is centralized past its practical limit). The answer lies with middle managers, who are both numerous enough and close enough to the actual resource flows to resolve conflicts in real time.

## Examples
- **[[ABC Technologies Realignment]]** — a startup reorganizes into three mission-oriented product divisions while keeping sales and manufacturing as centralized functional groups; illustrates Grove's Law in action
- **[[Hungary Central Planning]]** — a central planning body unable to match even predictable seasonal film demand; illustrates why central allocation fails beyond a certain scale
