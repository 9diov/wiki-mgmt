# Indicators

Measurements that let a manager track the output and health of an operation. Without them, you can only react to crises; with them, you can often prevent them.

## Core Principles

**Focus each indicator on a specific operational goal.** An unfocused metric directs attention nowhere.

**Measure output, not activity.** A salesman is measured by orders placed (output), not calls made (activity). An accounts-payable team is measured by vouchers processed, not hours worked.

**Use physical, countable things** wherever possible. Subjectivity makes indicators hard to act on and impossible to compare across groups.

**Pair indicators** to prevent overreaction. Every metric pushes behavior in one direction; a paired counter-metric catches the side effect.
- Track inventory levels *and* shortage frequency
- Track vouchers processed *and* error rate
- Track output quantity *and* customer satisfaction

## Types of Indicators

**Leading indicators**
Show you in advance what the future output is likely to be, giving time for corrective action. Only useful if you believe in them enough to actually act on their warnings.

**Linearity indicator**
Plots actual cumulative progress against the ideal straight-line path to a goal. A gap early on shows you must accelerate to hit the target—before it's too late to do anything.

**Trend indicators**
Output over time vs. a standard or expected level. Force you to ask *why* results diverged from expectations.

**Stagger chart**
A rolling forecast updated monthly, showing multiple prior forecasts side by side. Reveals whether your outlook is improving or deteriorating month-over-month—more valuable than any single forecast in isolation. Holds forecasters accountable by exposing forecast drift.

## Indicators for Administrative Work
Administrative functions often lack objective output measures, but they can be defined:

| Function | Output Indicator |
|---|---|
| Accounts payable | # Vouchers processed |
| Custodial | # Square feet cleaned |
| Customer service | # Sales orders entered |
| Employment | # People hired (by type) |

Comparing the same indicator across groups doing the same work creates healthy competitive pressure and improves performance.
