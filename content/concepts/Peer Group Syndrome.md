# Peer Group Syndrome

When organizational equals meet to make a decision, they tend to circle without committing. Each participant waits to sense the group's direction before voicing a position, to avoid being associated with the losing side. The result: motion without progress.

## Why It Happens
- Fear of being wrong in front of peers
- Fear of being overruled by a senior person after the fact
- Fear of simply sounding dumb
- No one feels authorized to call the decision

The auto-industry version: waiting for the boss to speak first, then supporting their view. The peer version: waiting for a consensus to crystallize, then attributing it to the group ("I think *our* position seems to be…"). Different dynamics, same outcome—real opinions never surface.

## The Peer-Plus-One Fix
Add one person who is organizationally senior to the group—not necessarily the most knowledgeable, but someone whose presence gives the group the confidence to commit. Their role is to facilitate and, if consensus fails, to make the call.

The senior person should participate as an equal during free discussion, ignoring status. They shift to authority mode only when the group cannot close. This is not the same as letting position power override knowledge power—it is using it at the right moment, not throughout.

## When to Apply It
Any time a peer group must make a decision and lacks a natural decision-maker. If no one steps up voluntarily, the person with the most at stake should take charge; failing that, ask the most senior person present.
