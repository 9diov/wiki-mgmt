# Modes of Control

Three invisible, pervasive mechanisms govern behavior in organizations. The manager's task is to identify which mode is most appropriate for a given situation—not to apply any single mode universally.

## The Three Modes

**Free-Market Forces**
Each party acts purely in self-interest; goods and services are exchanged at a price. Works when what is exchanged has a clearly definable dollar value. Efficient and requires no supervision. Breaks down when value can't be precisely measured—you can price tires, but you can't price each engineer's contribution to a team project.

**Contractual Obligations**
Rules, standards, and agreements define what work is expected and to what standard, even when specific daily tasks can't be specified in advance. Requires monitoring and overhead to enforce. Enables predictability when free-market pricing is impractical. Examples: the employment relationship, tax compliance, regulated utilities. Expectations can bind as tightly as legal documents.

**Cultural Values**
The group's interest takes precedence over individual self-interest. Operates through trust, shared values, shared objectives, and shared methods—developed only through extensive common experience. Requires no direct oversight, but requires investment in culture-building. Applicable when rules can't keep pace with environmental change, or when situations are too ambiguous for any contract to cover in advance.

## The CUA Factor
A composite measure of a working environment's **Complexity, Uncertainty, and Ambiguity**. High CUA = the environment changes faster than rules can be written, involves conflicting demands, and resists precise advance specification.

## Choosing the Right Mode

| Motivation | CUA Factor | Appropriate Mode |
|---|---|---|
| Self-interest | Low | Free-market |
| Group-interest | Low | Contractual |
| Group-interest | High | Cultural values |
| Self-interest | High | *No mode works — chaos* |

The chaos quadrant is important: when a person is primarily self-interested and the environment is highly complex and ambiguous, no control mechanism works well.

## Application to New Hires and Promotions
New employees arrive with high self-interest. Assign them to low-CUA jobs with clear structure. As they accumulate shared experience and shift toward group interest, promote them into more complex, ambiguous roles. Hiring a senior person from outside places someone with high self-interest immediately into a high-CUA environment—the most difficult combination; it often fails.

Promotion from within is favored by organizations with strong cultures precisely because it develops group interest and shared experience gradually before exposing people to high-CUA environments.

## Management's Role by Mode
- *Free-market*: no management supervision needed; parties govern themselves
- *Contractual*: set and modify rules, monitor adherence, evaluate and correct performance
- *Cultural values*: develop and nurture shared values through articulation and, more powerfully, through personal example

## Examples
- **[[Bob Marketing Supervisor Day]]** — Bob's day moves through all three modes: cafeteria lunch (market), showing up to work (contractual), strategic planning participation (cultural)
- **[[Barbara Sales Training Program]]** — a single work project exhibits all three modes: buying binders (market), training expectations (contractual), coordinating cross-division messages (cultural)
- **[[Intel Sales Commission Contest]]** — divisions used market-mode incentives (contests, prizes) to compete for sales reps' attention; reps responded rationally, ignoring low-commission products; the marketing managers who complained had created the problem themselves
- **[[Intel Division Sales Force Loyalty]]** — sales engineers stayed at Intel for nearly a year with no product to sell, foregoing available commissions elsewhere; cultural values overriding self-interest
