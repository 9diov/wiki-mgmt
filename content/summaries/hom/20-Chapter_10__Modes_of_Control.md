# Chapter 10: Modes of Control

## Core Idea
Three invisible mechanisms govern behavior in organizations—free-market forces, contractual obligations, and cultural values. The manager's task is to identify which mode is most appropriate for a given situation, determined by the combination of individual motivation (self-interest vs. group interest) and the CUA factor of the environment (complexity, uncertainty, ambiguity).

## Key Concepts

**[[Modes of Control ⭐]]**
The three modes:

*Free-market forces*: Parties act in pure self-interest; exchange is priced and efficient. Works when the value of what is exchanged can be clearly defined. Requires no supervision. Breaks down when contributions are hard to measure—e.g., an engineer's value in a team project can't be priced per "bit."

*Contractual obligations*: Rules and standards define expected work and conduct. Requires monitoring and overhead. Covers situations where free-market pricing is impractical but behavior still needs predictability. Applies to employment relationships, tax systems, regulated utilities. Expectations bind as tightly as legal documents.

*Cultural values*: Group interest supersedes self-interest. Operates through trust, shared values, and shared methods—built only through extensive common experience. No overhead required, but requires cultural investment. Governs behavior in high-complexity, high-ambiguity environments where rules can't keep pace with change.

**The CUA Factor**
Complexity, Uncertainty, and Ambiguity—a composite measure of how difficult an environment is to govern by rules or prices. When CUA is low, market or contractual modes suffice. When CUA is high and group interest is present, cultural values become the most effective mode. When CUA is high and self-interest dominates, *no mode works*—the result is chaos.

**Matching Mode to Situation**
New employees arrive with high self-interest; give them low-CUA jobs with clear structure. As they accumulate shared experience and develop group interest, promote them into higher-CUA roles. Hiring senior people from outside is risky: they arrive with high self-interest and are placed directly into high-CUA environments, with no base of shared experience to draw on.

Management's role differs by mode: free-market needs no oversight; contractual requires monitoring and correction; cultural values requires building shared experience, articulating values, and—most powerfully—demonstrating them through personal example.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Bob Marketing Supervisor Day]]**: Bob's day cycles through all three modes—cafeteria lunch (market), showing up (contractual), strategic planning (cultural). Demonstrates that all three modes coexist for the same person within a single day.

**[[Barbara Sales Training Program]]**: A single field training project runs on all three modes simultaneously—buying binders (market), training expectations (contractual), coordinating cross-division messages (cultural). A manager must operate in all three at once.

**[[Intel Sales Commission Contest]]**: Divisions competed for salespeople's time using prizes and bonuses; sales reps rationally optimized for commissions. The marketing managers who complained had designed the market-mode system that produced this behavior. Instilling a mode tends to establish the motivation it assumes.

**[[Intel Division Sales Force Loyalty]]**: With no product to sell for nearly a year, Intel sales engineers stayed rather than taking other jobs. Belief and faith—cultural values—overrode immediate self-interest. The same reps who responded to commission incentives in one context stayed loyal in another: mode of control is situational, not fixed.
