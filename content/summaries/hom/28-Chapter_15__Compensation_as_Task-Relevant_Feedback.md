# Chapter 15: Compensation as Task-Relevant Feedback

## Core Idea
Money functions differently at different levels of [[Maslow's Hierarchy]]: first as a material necessity, then as a measure of worth in a competitive environment. For managers, compensation is the tool for delivering task-relevant feedback at scale—but only if it is honestly tied to performance through merit-based bonuses, salary administration, and promotion decisions.

## Key Concepts

**[[Compensation as Feedback]]**
A manager cannot be paid by the piece, so compensation must be designed as a proxy for task-relevant feedback. A performance bonus—structured as a percentage of total pay that rises with seniority (10–25% for middle managers, up to 50% for senior executives)—can spotlight performance even when individual contribution is hard to isolate. Bonus design requires deciding whether performance is individual or team-based, choosing the right time lag, and picking measurable vs. subjective criteria. No scheme is perfect; the goal is to call attention to performance.

**Salary Administration: Experience vs. Merit**
At one extreme, salary tracks experience only—time in the role determines pay, regardless of output. This is the model used by large Japanese companies, many unions, and government jobs; it signals that performance doesn't matter much. At the other extreme, merit-only pay ignores tenure entirely. Most companies compromise: everyone starts at the same level but advances at different speeds toward different ceilings depending on performance. Any merit-based system requires comparative ranking—someone must finish last—which is emotionally difficult but necessary if salary is to function as feedback.

**[[Peter Principle]]**
The Peter Principle says people are promoted until they reach their level of incompetence. Grove's more precise version: an achiever alternates between "meets requirements" and "exceeds requirements" throughout his career. Every time he exceeds requirements, he becomes promotable; upon promotion, he drops back to meets requirements in the new role. This is not a flaw—it is the correct use of human capacity. The alternative (leaving an "exceeds" performer unchallenged) causes atrophy and eventual decline to "meets" anyway. The real problem is *over-promotion*—when someone lands far above his capability for too long. The right response is recycling.

**Recycling**
When a promotion proves to be too large a step, management should acknowledge its own misjudgment and deliberately move the person back to the job he did well. This is almost never done; instead, the over-promoted employee is typically pushed out of the company under the rationalization that it is "for his own sake." Grove argues this is wrong: recycling openly—with management support through the employee's embarrassment—produces a person doing work they are known to do well, and in Grove's experience, often makes them a stronger candidate for a second promotion later.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Teacher Pay Analogy]]**: In many school systems, a good teacher and a poor teacher earn the same salary if they have equal tenure. Compensation is entirely disconnected from performance. Grove suggests this may explain the pass/fail grading system—a pass/fail grader has no incentive to distinguish levels of student achievement, just as management has no incentive to distinguish levels of teacher performance.
