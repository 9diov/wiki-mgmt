# Chapter 16: Why Training Is the Boss's Job

## Core Idea
Training is one of the highest-leverage activities a manager can perform, and it must be done by the manager himself—not delegated to outside specialists—because effective training must be tied to how things are actually done in the organization and delivered by a credible role model.

## Key Concepts

**[[Training ⭐]]**
A manager has two levers for raising subordinate performance: motivation and capability. Motivation is universally accepted as the manager's job; so is increasing capability through training. Training must be done by the manager (not proxies), must be tied to actual organizational practices (not generic outside curricula), and must be a continuing process rather than a one-time event. A manager who trains his subordinates is also training himself—teaching requires far deeper knowledge of a task than simply doing it.

**Leverage of Training**
Twelve hours of preparation for four one-hour lectures with ten students produces roughly 200 hours of improved work output per year from a 1% improvement in subordinate performance. This is high [[Leverage ⭐]] by any measure. The calculation only holds if the training accurately addresses what people need to know to do their jobs better—which is why outside or generic courses often fail to deliver.

**Two Types of Training**
New-employee training (teaching skills to new hires) and new-skill training (teaching new principles or methods to existing staff) have very different scales. Training 20% of a department annually is a substantial undertaking; training 100% of existing staff in something new is five times larger. Managers must prioritize and sequence accordingly.

**Starting a Training Program**
Begin with one short course (three to four lectures) on the most urgent topic. Develop only the first lecture before committing to a schedule. Treat the first run as a throwaway—give it to more experienced subordinates who will not be confused and who will help critique it. Develop subsequent lectures only after delivering the previous one. Request anonymous feedback and iterate.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Restaurant Reservations Training Failure]]**: A new reservations employee wasn't told to inform callers that the restaurant had lost its liquor license. Guests arrived expecting wine; the maitre d' spent the evening apologizing to every table. One untrained employee created a broken experience for every customer that night.

**[[Intel Ion Implanter Failure ⭐]]**: A relatively new operator wasn't trained to recognize when the ion implanter had drifted out of tune. She continued operating it normally. By the time the problem was discovered, nearly $1M of almost-finished silicon wafers had been scrapped and customer deliveries slipped.

**[[Intel Career Development Course]]**: Outside consultants taught a career development course at Intel using a highly structured, academic approach—multi-year career plans and coordinated job rotations—that was completely at odds with Intel's actual free-market approach to internal mobility. Participants became demoralized by the gap between what was taught and what was practiced.
