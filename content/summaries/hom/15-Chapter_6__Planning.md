# Chapter 6: Planning: Today's Actions for Tomorrow's Output

## Core Idea
Planning is not a periodic exercise but an ordinary managerial activity: compare what the environment will demand with what you are on track to produce, then take actions *today* to close *tomorrow's* gap. The output of planning is not a document—it is the decisions made and actions taken as a result of the thinking process.

## Key Concepts

**[[Planning Process]]**
Three steps, borrowed directly from production thinking: (1) determine environmental demand—what will your customers, competitors, and environment require of you in the future? (2) establish present status—what will you actually produce if nothing changes? (3) close the gap—what actions must you take today to make projected output converge with projected demand? The key insight: today's gap is a failure of *past* planning. Acting now on tomorrow's problems is what planning actually means.

Strategy is the most abstract summary of your gap-closing actions. Tactics are how you implement that strategy. Crucially, one level's strategy is the next higher level's tactics—the mailroom manager's printer rollout plan is his strategy, but is just a step in the communications manager's larger electronic-mail initiative.

**[[Management by Objectives ⭐]]**
MBO operationalizes planning for shorter horizons. An objective answers "Where do I want to go?" Key results answer "How will I pace myself to see if I'm getting there?" Key results must be concrete and dated—no ambiguity at deadline time. Objectives nest: if a subordinate meets their objective, their supervisor's objective should be advanced automatically. MBO is a feedback and pacing tool, not a legal performance contract—Columbus missed his stated objective (trade route to China) while delivering the New World, and performed well by any reasonable measure.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Bruce Marketing Staffing Plan]]**: Three staff, too many projects, fixed budget. The [[Planning Process|three-step planning process]] produces concrete near-term actions (redistribute tasks, bring in a summer intern, file a headcount request) even when full gap closure is impossible. The plan itself is evidence that the gap is real.

**[[Cindy Process Engineering Plan]]**: Cindy maps her environment (production as customer, engineering groups as vendors), works backward from deployment dates using time offsets, negotiates a shared schedule with her "vendors," and publishes weekly performance results. All three planning steps applied to a coordination role.

**[[Columbus MBO]]**: Isabella's objective (increase Spain's wealth) → Columbus's objective (find a trade route to the Orient) → Columbus's dated key results. Columbus met his key results, found the New World instead of a trade route, and far exceeded Isabella's objective. Demonstrates nesting hierarchy and why [[Management by Objectives ⭐|MBO]] is a pacing tool rather than a strict performance contract.

**[[Intel Philippines MBO]]**: The Far East construction manager's four dated key results for the Philippine plant expansion decision, and how his objective maps exactly onto one of his supervisor's key results—a textbook nesting example.
