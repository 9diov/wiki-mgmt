# Chapter 14: Two Difficult Tasks

## Core Idea
Interviewing a job candidate and persuading a valued employee not to quit are two of the most emotionally charged tasks a manager faces. Both are high-risk, require active listening over talking, and demand that the manager act immediately rather than defer.

## Key Concepts

**[[Interviewing]]**
The interview's purpose is fourfold: select a good performer, educate the candidate about the company, determine mutual fit, and sell the job. The candidate should do 80% of the talking; the manager controls the conversation by being an active listener who redirects when things go off track. Information to gather falls into four categories: technical skill, what the candidate did with that skill, discrepancies between capability and performance, and operational values. The exercise increases the odds of a good hire but guarantees nothing—careful interviews can still produce disastrous hires.

**[[Handling Resignation]]**
When a valued employee says "I quit," the manager's *immediate* response is decisive. Deferring to a later conversation confirms to the employee that he is unimportant—and seals the outcome. Drop everything, sit him down, make him talk through his reasons without arguing, and buy time. Then escalate to your own supervisor and pursue every avenue—including transfer to another department—to retain the employee for the company. The goal is to identify and address the real underlying issue, not to win the argument in the opening conversation.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Intel Cost Accountant Interview]]**: Grove interviewed a Harvard MBA from the food service industry for a cost accounting role. Neither knew the other's domain. Grove walked him through the semiconductor manufacturing process and asked him to derive the finished cost of a wafer. The candidate worked through the problem from first principles and got it right. He was hired—and the exercise correctly predicted his first-rate problem-solving capacity.

**[[Rickover Chair Trick]]**: Admiral Rickover reportedly seated candidates on a three-legged chair to test character under embarrassment. Grove uses this as a cautionary contrast: tricks create false first impressions that take a long time to correct once the person is hired. The interview should show the company and manager as they really are.
