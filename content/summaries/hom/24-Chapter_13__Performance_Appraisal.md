# Chapter 13: Performance Appraisal — Manager as Judge and Jury

## Core Idea
The performance review is the single most important form of task-relevant feedback a manager can provide, yet most managers do it poorly. Its fundamental purpose is to improve the subordinate's future performance—not to cleanse the supervisor's system of observations, justify compensation, or achieve psychological comfort.

## Key Concepts

**[[Performance Appraisal ⭐]]**
The review is the manager's highest-leverage feedback tool, influencing a subordinate's performance curve for a long time. It serves two aims: assessing skill level (what's missing, how to remedy it) and intensifying motivation. It is also the only formal moment where a manager is mandated by the organization to act as judge and deliver that judgment face to face—a uniquely uncomfortable act in a culture that avoids confrontation.

**Assessing Performance**
Assessment requires weighing both *output measures* (tangible results: sales, yield, completed designs) and *internal measures* (how the work was done: team health, pipeline quality, future-oriented investments). No fixed ratio applies; the right weighting shifts by situation. Crucially, there is often a *time offset* between a subordinate's activity and the output it produces—a manager may appear to be performing excellently on lagging indicators while current work is quietly deteriorating. Supervisors must judge the work *during the review period*, not just read the output indicators.

**The Potential Trap**
The most common rating error is assessing *potential* (form, style, polish) rather than actual performance. A manager's rating cannot be higher than the rating accorded to his organization's output. Promoting or rating highly based on appearance signals to the whole company that you need only *act* like a good manager—not perform like one.

**Delivering the Assessment — The Three Ls**
Level: be totally frank; credibility depends on it. Listen: use all sensory capabilities to ensure the message is actually received, not just transmitted—watch for verbal and nonverbal signals that comprehension is breaking down. Leave yourself out: the review is for the subordinate; suppress your own anxieties and guilt.

**[[Conflict Resolution Stages]]**
A subordinate confronted with a serious performance problem typically moves through stages: *ignore → actively deny → blame others → assume responsibility → find the solution.* The transition from blaming others to assuming responsibility is an emotional step, not an intellectual one; the supervisor's job is to help the subordinate get there. Only after responsibility is assumed does finding the solution become tractable.

**Commitment Over Agreement**
The supervisor does not need the subordinate to agree with the assessment—only to commit to a course of action. Insisting on agreement is about the supervisor's psychological comfort, not operational need. Any outcome that includes genuine commitment to change is an acceptable resolution, even if the subordinate remains unconvinced.

**Reviewing the Ace**
High performers tend to receive retrospective reviews focused on justifying their rating rather than identifying how they could improve further. This reverses the right priority: stars account for a disproportionate share of output, so improving their performance is the highest-leverage use of the review. No level of performance is beyond 20/20-hindsight improvement.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Time Offset Review ⭐]]**: A manager's organization had an outstanding year on all output measures; Grove gave him a very positive review. The next year everything collapsed. On reflection, the excellent output had been produced by work done years earlier; the warning signs (high turnover, grumbling) were internal indicators of current deterioration. The superior rating was wrong—Grove should have trusted the internal measures and had the judgment to rate lower despite the output indicators.

**[[Intel Potential Trap]]**: A general manager's business unit lost money, missed revenue forecasts, and slipped schedules. His supervisor rated him highly anyway, arguing the manager himself was outstanding—it was the *organization* that failed. Grove rejected the review: a manager's rating cannot exceed the rating of his organization's output.

**[[Superficial Review Rewrite]]**: Grove told a subordinate his review was too shallow and needed to be rewritten. The subordinate disagreed—he didn't think it was important enough to redo. After a deadlock, Grove simply said, "I want you to do it." The subordinate remained unconvinced but committed and produced a thorough rewrite. The illustration: commitment to action is the goal, not emotional agreement.
