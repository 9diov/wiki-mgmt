# Chapter 5: Decisions, Decisions

## Core Idea
Decision-making in knowledge businesses cannot follow the traditional hierarchy model because technical expertise and organizational authority rapidly diverge. The ideal process runs through three stages—free discussion, clear decision, full support—but human psychology works against each stage, requiring deliberate structure to overcome it.

## Key Concepts

**[[Knowledge vs Position Power]]**
In traditional industries, authority tracks rank. In know-how businesses, a new engineer arrives with current expertise; as they advance over the years, position power grows while domain knowledge ages. The result: those with the authority to decide are often not the best-equipped to decide. Middle managers are the critical link, responsible for getting these two types of power to mesh.

**[[Decision Making Process]]**
The ideal model has three stages: (1) *free discussion*—all views openly aired, no position-power prejudice; (2) *clear decision*—framed precisely, not obscured to avoid conflict; (3) *full support*—all participants commit to back the decision, even those who disagree. The model breaks at each stage for predictable reasons: people hang back during discussion, obscure the outcome to avoid controversy, and resist committing to decisions they opposed.

Decisions should be made at the *lowest competent level*—by people closest to the situation who combine technical knowledge with the judgment that comes from experience.

**[[Peer Group Syndrome]]**
When peers meet without a clear authority figure, each participant waits for a consensus to emerge before voicing a position. Nobody risks being on the losing side, so the group circles without progress. The fix is *peer-plus-one*: adding a senior person whose presence gives the group the confidence to commit. The senior person participates as an equal during free discussion, then steps in to decide if consensus cannot be reached.

**[[Six Decision Questions]]**
Before a decision begins, settle: (1) What decision needs to be made? (2) When? (3) Who will decide? (4) Who needs to be consulted? (5) Who will ratify or veto? (6) Who needs to be informed afterward? Answering these in advance prevents surprise vetoes—which, even when legitimate, look like political maneuvering and demoralize participants who have invested effort in the outcome.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Philippines Plant Location Meeting]]**: Grove applies the [[Six Decision Questions|six decision questions]] explicitly to the Philippine plant expansion. Who decides (parallel managers from construction and manufacturing), who ratifies (Grove), who informs (Gordon Moore). The group reaches a decision; Grove listens and ratifies.

**[[Intel Peer Group Role Play]]**: At Intel's first management training session, peers went in circles for fifteen minutes before the returned chairman intervened and resolved the issue quickly—the founding demonstration of the peer-plus-one approach.
