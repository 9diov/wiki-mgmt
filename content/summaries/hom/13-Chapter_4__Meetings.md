# Chapter 4: Meetings—The Medium of Managerial Work

## Core Idea
Meetings are not a waste of time—they are the medium through which managerial work is performed. The goal is not fewer meetings but better ones. The real sign of malorganization is spending more than 25% of time in ad hoc, [[Meeting Types|mission-oriented meetings]].

## Key Concepts

**[[Meeting Types]]**
Two kinds: *process-oriented* (regularly scheduled, for sharing knowledge and information) and *mission-oriented* (ad hoc, for producing a specific decision). Process-oriented meetings apply the production principle of [[Leverage ⭐|batching]]—using a single setup to handle many similar transactions.

**[[One-on-One ⭐]]**
A regular meeting between supervisor and subordinate—the principal vehicle for their working relationship. Its purpose is mutual teaching and information exchange. The subordinate sets the agenda; the supervisor asks questions and coaches. Frequency should match the subordinate's [[Task Relevant Maturity ⭐|task-relevant maturity]] for their current work: weekly for new or difficult assignments, less often for experienced hands. A 90-minute one-on-one can improve a subordinate's work quality for an entire two-week period—enormous [[Leverage ⭐|leverage]].

**[[Staff Meeting]]**
A meeting of a supervisor and all direct subordinates. Its primary value is peer interaction—hearing different views on the same problem produces better understanding than any one-sided briefing. The supervisor's role is moderator and facilitator, not lecturer. Agenda items should affect more than two people present; anything narrower should be taken offline.

**[[Operation Review]]**
A formal presentation forum for managers who don't otherwise interact—typically junior presenters, their supervisors, and senior reviewers several levels apart. Its purpose is cross-level teaching: juniors get senior feedback; seniors get ground-level detail. The reviewing senior manager sets the tone and must not preview materials in advance—spontaneous reaction is the point.

**[[Task Relevant Maturity ⭐]]**
How experienced a subordinate is with a *specific task*, not their general seniority. Introduced here as the driver of one-on-one frequency; developed fully in Chapter 12.

**[[Meeting Types|Mission-Oriented Meetings]] and the Chairman**
Ad hoc meetings called to produce a decision. Success depends almost entirely on what the chairman does *before* the meeting: clarifying the objective, ensuring the right people attend (max 6–8 for decisions), sending an agenda with each person's expected role. After the meeting, the chairman must send clear minutes stating what was decided and who does what by when.
