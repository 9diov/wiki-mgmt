# Chapter 11: The Sports Analogy

## Core Idea
The manager's single most important task is eliciting peak performance from subordinates. Since motivation must come from within, the manager's job is to create an environment where motivated people flourish—ideally one that gives work the motivational properties of competitive sports, driving people toward self-actualization.

## Key Concepts

**[[Maslow's Hierarchy]]**
Needs organize into a hierarchy: physiological → safety/security → social/affiliation → esteem/recognition → self-actualization. A satisfied need no longer motivates; a higher need takes over. The first four levels are self-limiting—once met, they stop driving behavior. Self-actualization is the exception: it has no ceiling, and the drive for more grows with achievement rather than diminishing.

Two paths to self-actualization:
- *Competence-driven*: mastery for its own sake—the violinist who practices endlessly to play better
- *Achievement-driven*: spontaneous seeking of the boundary between success and failure—the ring tosser who positions themselves at the hardest productive distance

Money's motivational character changes with level: at physiological/security levels, it buys needs and motivates to the threshold. At self-actualization, money is a *measure of achievement*—the second $10 million is as motivating as the first, because it is a scorecard, not a means to any material end.

**[[Competitive Environment]]**
People who care little about work at the office will push themselves to the limit running a marathon—because there is something to beat. The manager's job is to recreate that structure at work: establish objective measures, make comparative performance visible, and set goals high enough that achievement is genuinely uncertain. The [[Intel Building Czar]] case shows how dramatically this works: years of mediocre facilities maintenance improved immediately once buildings were scored and compared publicly. Nothing else changed—no pay increases, no other incentives. The score was the racetrack.

The manager in this model is a coach: takes no personal credit for the team's success, is tough and demanding, and understands the work from having done it themselves.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Intel Building Czar]]**: Facilities maintenance performance improved immediately when buildings were periodically scored by a "building czar" and compared publicly. No additional reward. The competition itself was the motivation.

**[[Chuck at Harvard Business School]]**: Tracks movement through Maslow levels within a single academic year—physiological fear → safety study groups → social affiliation with the class section. Also shows regressive movement: an earthquake at an Intel plant instantly reduced motivated employees to pure survival behavior.

**[[Caribbean Assembly Plant Workers]]**: Intel Caribbean plant workers quit after reaching their personal savings target. Money motivated powerfully at the physiological level—and stopped exactly when that need was met. Illustrates the self-limiting property of lower-level needs.

**[[Venture Capitalist Second Million]]**: A VC with $10M working hard for another $10M. Money at self-actualization is a measure, not a means—the second million is as motivating as the first.

**[[Ring Toss Experiment]]**: Volunteers spontaneously positioned themselves at the boundary of capability without instruction or reward. Distinguishes gamblers (high risk, no skill), conservatives (guaranteed success), and achievers (boundary-testing)—the natural behavior of achievement-driven self-actualization.
