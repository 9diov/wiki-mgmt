# Chapter 8: Hybrid Organizations

## Core Idea
Every large organization with a common business purpose inevitably ends up as a hybrid: a mix of mission-oriented business units (for local responsiveness) and functional shared-service groups (for economies of scale and expertise leverage). This is not a design choice—it is a structural inevitability Grove calls "Grove's Law."

## Key Concepts

**[[Hybrid Organization ⭐]]**
The two pure organizational forms are opposites. A *mission-oriented* organization is fully decentralized: each unit handles its own purchasing, personnel, production, and sales, staying maximally responsive to local conditions. A *functional* organization is fully centralized: each function serves all units from one group, maximizing scale and expertise leverage. In practice, neither pure form works. The mission-oriented form sacrifices all economies of scale; the functional form is too slow and creates destructive competition among units for shared resources. Every real organization blends the two—hence, all large companies are hybrids.

At Intel, roughly two-thirds of employees work in functional groups (manufacturing, sales, R&D, data processing, finance), which act as *internal subcontractors* to the mission-oriented business divisions. The business divisions can concentrate on their specific products and markets; the functional groups deliver shared services to everyone.

The hybrid's central challenge is allocating shared resources efficiently. Central allocators can't handle the volume—attempting it produces the [[Hungary Central Planning|Hungarian film shortage]] problem at corporate scale. The answer is middle managers: numerous enough to cover the range of operations, close enough to the actual resource flows to resolve conflicts in real time. Dual reporting (Chapter 9) is the mechanism that makes this work.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[ABC Technologies Realignment]]**: A three-year-old company creates three mission-oriented product divisions while keeping sales and manufacturing centralized—a textbook [[Hybrid Organization ⭐|hybrid]] forming spontaneously as the product line broadens. Illustrates Grove's Law: the pattern appears even at small scale.

**[[Hungary Central Planning]]**: A central planning body unable to match even predictable seasonal demand for photographic film. Illustrates why central allocation of shared resources fails beyond a practical limit—and why the hybrid's resource conflicts must be resolved by middle managers, not headquarters.
