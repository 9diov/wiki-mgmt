# Chapter 9: Dual Reporting

## Core Idea
Dual reporting is the mechanism that makes hybrid organizations function: employees have two supervisors—one for mission/operational priorities, one for functional/technical standards—so that neither local responsiveness nor technical excellence must be sacrificed. Extended further, people can exist on multiple organizational "planes" simultaneously, enabling leverage and coordination without permanent structural change.

## Key Concepts

**[[Dual Reporting]]**
In a hybrid organization, a single supervisor can't cover both dimensions. The mission-oriented manager (plant manager, division GM) provides operational priorities and can observe daily performance, but often lacks technical expertise. The functional manager (corporate security, finance VP, manufacturing head) holds the expertise and sets standards, but can't observe remote performance. Dual reporting assigns both roles simultaneously—one supervisor per dimension.

Intel stumbled onto this when deciding who security personnel at outlying plants should report to: the plant manager could monitor day-to-day behavior; only the corporate security manager could define standards. Both were necessary; the answer was [[Intel Plant Security Dual Reporting|both]].

The functional supervisor doesn't have to be a single individual. When a general manager lacks technical expertise in one of their functions, the specialists across divisions can form a *peer coordinating group* that collectively provides the technical supervision no individual boss can give. The [[Intel Manufacturing Peer Group|Intel manufacturing managers]] who began meeting over coffee and eventually formed a formal coordinating group illustrate this: members must voluntarily surrender some individual autonomy to the group's collective judgment, which requires trust in peers and a strong corporate culture.

**[[Two-Plane Organization]]**
A person's organizational life doesn't fit on a single chart. On the *operating plane*, they have clear daily reporting relationships. On *coordinating planes*, they belong to peer groups or committees that tackle cross-unit problems—each with its own hierarchy. On *task force planes*, they work on temporary teams formed for a specific purpose and dissolved when done.

These planes don't conflict—they're simply separate memberships, like belonging to a company and a church. Crucially, they can *reverse* the supervisory relationship: Grove, as Intel's president, reports to a division controller (his operating subordinate) who chairs a strategic planning group. In that plane, the controller is Grove's boss. This is a feature: it enables the best-qualified person to lead each group regardless of rank, and allows senior people to contribute as peers or foot soldiers when appropriate.

**Transitory Teams**
Many coordinating groups are temporary—task forces formed for a purpose, dissolved when done. In fast-moving businesses where formal structure can't shift fast enough to match technology change, transitory teams on temporary planes are the primary adaptive mechanism. They depend on dual reporting and peer decision-making to function.

## Illustrative Examples

**[[Intel Plant Security Dual Reporting]]**: Security staff at outlying plants report jointly to the plant manager (monitors performance) and corporate security manager (sets standards). Intel's founding experience with dual reporting, born from a staff meeting argument about who should be in charge.

**[[Intel Manufacturing Peer Group]]**: Manufacturing managers from different divisions—whose GMs lack manufacturing backgrounds—form a peer coordinating group to fill the technical supervisory gap. Membership requires voluntary surrender of individual decision-making to the group.

**[[Cindy Two-Plane Organization]]**: Cindy spends 80% on the operating plane (plant engineering manager); 20% on a cross-plant process coordinating group (its own chairman). Two separate planes, two hierarchies, no conflict—and doubled leverage: her expertise shapes one plant on the operating plane, all plants on the coordinating plane.

**[[Grove in Strategic Planning]]**: Grove reports to a division controller (his operating subordinate) in a strategic planning group. Illustrates plane reversal and why it enables organizational flexibility: senior people can contribute as peers or subordinates when someone else is better positioned to lead.
