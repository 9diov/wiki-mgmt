# Chapter 3: Managerial Leverage

## Core Idea
A [[Managers Output ⭐|manager's output]] is not their individual work—it is the output of their organization plus the output of neighboring organizations they influence. To be effective, maximize the [[Leverage ⭐]] of every activity.

## Key Formula

> Managerial Output = Σ (Leverage × Activity)

Higher leverage per activity → more output from the same time.

## What Managers Actually Do
Four types of activity, all toward producing output (see [[Managerial Activities]]):
1. **Information gathering** — the foundation; most valuable input is timely, verbal, informal
2. **Information giving** — transmitting knowledge, priorities, and values so subordinates can make aligned decisions independently
3. **Decision-making** — both direct decisions and participating in others' decisions (approving, vetoing, nudging)
4. **Being a role model** — values and norms are transmitted by visible behavior, not memos

"Nudging"—steering a situation without issuing a direct command—is far more common than formal decision-making and deserves deliberate attention.

**On written reports**: their value is mostly in forcing the writer to think precisely. Reading them is less important than writing them.

## High-Leverage Activities
Three sources of high [[Leverage ⭐]]:
- One manager affects *many people* (e.g., [[Robin Annual Planning|defining the annual planning process]] for 200 participants)
- A brief action affects someone's performance over a *long time* (e.g., a performance review, setting up a tickler file)
- A person with *unique knowledge* influences work broadly (e.g., [[Intel Pricing Engineer|the pricing engineer]] whose decisions affect every salesperson)

**Timeliness matters**: the same action at the right moment has far more leverage than the same action too late (e.g., acting immediately when a valued employee resigns).

## Negative Leverage
[[Leverage ⭐]] can go negative:
- Arriving unprepared to meetings wastes everyone's time
- [[Depressed Manager|Depression or waffling]] spreads through an organization, yet is hard to reverse
- **Managerial meddling**: overriding subordinates using superior knowledge instead of letting them work through problems. Result: subordinates stop taking initiative, output drops

## [[Delegation ⭐]] as Leverage
[[Delegation ⭐]] multiplies a manager's reach—but only works when delegator and delegatee share a common information base and problem-solving approach.

Key rules:
- **Delegation ≠ abdication**: you remain responsible; monitor the task
- Delegate tasks you know *well* (easier to monitor), not just the ones you dislike
- Monitor at the [[Lowest Value Stage|lowest-added-value stage]] (review rough drafts, not polished finals)
- Use *variable monitoring*: frequency should match the subordinate's task-relevant maturity for that specific task, not their general ability

## Increasing Output Rate
Beyond [[Leverage ⭐]], a manager can also increase output by working faster—processing more activities per hour. But shifting to higher-leverage activities is usually more impactful than simply speeding up.

## Time as the Scarcest Resource
Money, people, and capital can be increased. A manager's time cannot. How you allocate your own time is the most important signal you send as a leader and role model.
