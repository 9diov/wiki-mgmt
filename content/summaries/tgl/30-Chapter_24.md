# Chapter 24

## What Happens

**The record.** Friday afternoon: champagne cork through the ceiling. The plant shipped 57 orders this month at a value of ~$3 million — the previous record was 31 orders at $2 million. Work-in-process inventory is down 12%. Bill Peach calls to say thanks; a sales manager named Jons had reported that late orders were finally being resolved. The team celebrates, Rogo ends up drunk, Stacey drives him home.

**The misunderstanding.** Julie had driven over that night on impulse to surprise Rogo. She waited six hours with his mother. When Stacey and a laughing, unsteady Rogo tumble through the front door together, Julie sees exactly the wrong thing and drives off. By Monday, Stacey has called Julie to explain. Julie calls Rogo to apologize. They schedule Wednesday.

**New bottlenecks.** Monday morning, Stacey arrives with a different problem. Parts shortages at final assembly have begun appearing on *green-tagged* (non-bottleneck) parts. One order needed 172 different parts; 17 were missing, only 1 was red. The red part arrived by Friday morning — the 16 green parts are still missing.

The diagnosis: making the original two bottlenecks more productive increased throughput. More throughput means more demand on all downstream work centers. Some work centers that previously had excess capacity have now been loaded past 100% — and new bottlenecks have formed.

> *"We've run out of capacity on some other resources in addition to heat-treat and the NCX-10. Making the bottlenecks more productive has put more demand on the other work centers."*

Rogo calls Jonah and walks him through everything done since his visit. When Rogo mentions the red/green tag system, Jonah says: *"Maybe I'd better come have another look."*

## The Constraint Migration

Chapter 24 introduces a dynamic that will recur throughout any serious improvement program: **fixing the constraint creates a new constraint.** The NCX-10 and heat-treat were the bottlenecks because demand exceeded their capacity. As that capacity was recovered and expanded, more product flowed through — and the extra flow has now saturated resources that previously had slack.

The improvement worked. The plant is producing more. The cost of that success is that the system's limit has moved. Managing a production system is not a one-time fix but a cycle: find the constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything to it, elevate it — and when it's no longer the constraint, find the new one.

## Management Observations

- **Improvement changes the system.** A more productive bottleneck loads non-bottlenecks more heavily. What was excess capacity before may not be excess capacity now.
- **Declining inventory is not always a clean signal.** The drop in WIP was real and good. But it also meant the buffer in front of former bottlenecks was being consumed — and some of it was masking downstream capacity problems.
- **The green-tag assumption was wrong.** The system was built on the premise that non-bottleneck parts would always be available when needed. That premise depended on non-bottlenecks having excess capacity, which is no longer universally true.
