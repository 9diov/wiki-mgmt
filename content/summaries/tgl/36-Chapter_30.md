# Chapter 30

## What Happens

**Month-end results.** 17% bottom-line improvement — beating Peach's 15% target. The plant is solidly in the black. Inventory is at 40% of its level three months ago. Throughput has doubled. The Burnside order was the decisive factor.

**The audit.** The division controller's team arrives. They catch Lou's cost-base change (using the last 2 months instead of the standard 12). Recalculating by the book: 12.8% improvement — below the 15% Rogo promised. The division is still pleased overall, but the number is smaller than reported.

**Hilton Smyth's visit.** Smyth arrives with a TV crew to film the robot (NCX-10) for a corporate productivity video. Problem: with inventory at 40% of former levels and DBR governing material releases, the robot has nothing to do. It sits idle on camera. Smyth is furious; the plant looks incompetent. Rogo can't explain the full logic without a long lecture, so he takes the hit.

**Burnside helicopter.** Johnny Jons arrives by helicopter — with Burnside himself. Burnside personally thanks the plant for saving his production line. He mentions that his company buys $10 million/year in the product category; he's thinking about consolidating suppliers. The prospect of a 10,000-unit/year contract is in play. Jons, who bet against the 6-week delivery, owes Rogo a pair of Guccis.

**Performance review.** Peach schedules a formal review at division headquarters. Rogo prepares.

**Domestic.** Rogo and Julie sit on a bench by the river. Full reconciliation. They agree they both lost sight of what was important. Julie acknowledges she should have been more patient; Rogo acknowledges he shut her out. They agree to "remarry" — Vegas, just the two of them.

## Management Observations

- **A good result can be undermined by how it's measured.** 17% actual improvement becomes 12.8% when accounting conventions are applied. The underlying reality didn't change; the reported number did. Lou's workaround was pragmatically sound but exposed the plant to audit risk.
- **Visible idle time is politically costly even when operationally correct.** An idle bottleneck machine — especially on camera — signals waste to any observer who doesn't understand DBR. The plant is performing correctly; it looks broken. This is an ongoing communication problem for constraint-based management.
- **Burnside is the constraint moving to the market.** The plant now has capacity; the question is whether it has enough orders. The Burnside relationship directly addresses this — a 10,000-unit/year contract would consume the freed capacity.
