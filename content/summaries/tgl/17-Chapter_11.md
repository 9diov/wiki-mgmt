# Chapter 11

## What Happens

Rogo and Julie fight before he leaves for New York. She's not wrong: he hasn't been home for dinner reliably in weeks, the kids notice, and "it's for the plant" is wearing thin. He leaves anyway, makes the flight, spends the night calling home to an empty house, and meets Jonah for breakfast at 7 AM.

Jonah confirms Rogo's goal formulation is correct, then pivots: forget the robots. They're a symptom, not the disease. The deeper problem is how Rogo manages capacity.

**Jonah's first counterintuitive claim:** A plant where everyone is working all the time is very inefficient.

Rogo resists this. Idle workers are waste — every metric says so. Jonah points back at him: the only way to generate excess inventory is with excess manpower. Rogo's plant has a lot of excess inventory. Therefore Rogo's plant has excess manpower — manpower that is being kept "busy" producing things that pile up rather than ship. The efficiency numbers look good. The plant is failing.

**Jonah's second claim:** The goal of balancing capacity exactly to market demand — which every manufacturing manager in the western world strives for — moves a plant closer to bankruptcy, not away from it.

The logic: when you balance capacity to demand and trim all slack, throughput goes *down* and inventory goes *up*. This can be proved mathematically. The result is that even the one measurement you thought you were improving (OE) doesn't improve as much as expected, because inventory carrying costs rise.

**Why?** Two phenomena, found in every plant, whose combination causes this:

1. **[[Dependent Events and Statistical Fluctuations|Dependent events]]** — steps in a production sequence where each must complete before the next can begin
2. **[[Dependent Events and Statistical Fluctuations|Statistical fluctuations]]** — the inherent variability in how long each step takes (the chef might drop an egg; the waiter might take four minutes or forty)

Jonah is clear: neither phenomenon alone causes the problem. It's their *combination* that makes a balanced plant unworkable. He won't explain why — that's Rogo's homework.

He shakes hands, apologizes for leaving, gets into a client's limousine, and disappears into New York traffic. Rogo is left in a cab with two concepts and no answer.

## The Setup for the Hiking Trip

This chapter plants the intellectual seeds for one of the most famous teaching moments in business literature — the Boy Scout hike in Chapter 13 (where the consequences of dependent events + statistical fluctuations are demonstrated physically). Jonah's refusal to just give the answer is deliberate: Rogo needs to *experience* the insight, not just receive it.

## Management Observations

- **Keeping everyone busy is not free.** The cost of 100% utilization is excess inventory — and excess inventory is money stuck inside the system, driving up OE through carrying costs and hiding quality problems. Idle time at a non-bottleneck is not waste; it may be the system working correctly.
- **The [[Balanced Plant Fallacy]] is near-universal.** Jonah notes it's "practically universal in the western business world." The drive to trim capacity to the bone is rational from a local cost-reduction perspective and destructive at the system level.
- **"If I told you what to do, you would ultimately fail."** Jonah's teaching method is explicitly about understanding, not instruction. A rule followed without comprehension breaks the moment conditions change; an insight derived personally holds.
