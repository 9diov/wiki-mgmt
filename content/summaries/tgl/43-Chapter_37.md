# Chapter 37

## What Happens

**Step 3 and inertia.** Ralph notices something missing in the five steps. Step 3 says "subordinate everything else to the constraint decision." But when the constraint shifted from machines → material release → market, the team didn't fully re-examine what non-constraint behaviors should change. The red/green tag system, designed when machine bottlenecks existed, was never revisited.

**Stacey's discovery.** The red/green tag system prioritizes red (bottleneck-bound) parts at every work center that has a queue. That's fine at the actual bottlenecks. But CCRs (capacity constraint resources) also have queues — and they process many green-tag parts that go to assembly directly, not through the bottleneck. Always doing red first delays green parts to assembly. Stacey's team had been catching this late, scrambling to reprioritize. Her solution: **eliminate the tags**. With DBR controlling material release, everything released to the floor is of equal importance. First-in, first-done (FIFO) is better than priority-based sequencing when all parts are needed equally.

**A larger warning.** This reveals a deeper problem. Even solutions implemented to support the constraint can become obstacles when the constraint moves. The revised step 5 now reads:

> **WARNING!!!! If in the previous steps a constraint has been broken, go back to step 1, but do not allow INERTIA to cause a system's constraint.**

Lou's reaction: this is much worse than expected. He thought only cost accounting was the source of bad practices. But even their own improvements created inertia. Stacey built finished goods buffer to protect the "bottleneck" machines — after those machines were no longer the constraint. Ralph's release schedule still treated NCX-10 and the oven as constraints after they'd been bypassed by market demand. Their own correct solutions had fossilized.

**20% spare capacity.** The fictitious orders Stacey placed to keep the machines busy represent about 20% of current bottleneck load. Canceling them reveals the plant has significant unused capacity — enough to support another major sales push.

## Management Observations

- **Inertia is itself a constraint.** Rules and procedures put in place for the right reason calcify into obstacles when conditions change. The five-step process must include a warning that solutions from the previous cycle can become constraints in the next. This is the most practically important addition to the framework.
- **FIFO beats priority queuing when you control input.** Once DBR governs material release, all released parts are essentially equally urgent. Priority tags create a secondary scheduling problem (always delaying green parts) that DBR already solved at the input level. The simpler rule is better.
- **Solutions can hide capacity.** The protective finished goods buffer Stacey built looked like responsible constraint management. It was also consuming bottleneck capacity on products with no current demand. Removing it reveals slack that enables further growth.
