# Chapter 1

## What Happens

Alex Rogo, plant manager at a UniCo manufacturing plant, arrives at 7:30 AM to find his parking space taken by Bill Peach's red Mercedes. Peach, the division VP, is already inside — and has been for an hour, personally expediting a late customer order (41427, seven weeks overdue) after the customer called him at home the night before.

By the time Rogo walks in, the plant is in chaos: a machinist was ordered to undo a setup mid-run, refused, was threatened with firing, and the union steward showed up. Rogo de-escalates the floor conflict, then faces Peach.

Peach delivers the ultimatum in Rogo's own chair: the plant is losing money, it's the worst performer in the division, and Rogo has **three months to turn it around** — or Peach recommends closure.

The fallout is immediate. The insulted machinist (Tony) quits — but not before deliberately leaving two nuts untightened on the NCX-10, the plant's only machine of that type. By the time Rogo learns this from production manager Bob Donovan, the machine is half torn apart on the floor.

## The Problem Set Up

The plant's dysfunction is on full display in the first scene:

- **Everything is late.** The internal priority system has four levels — Hot, Very Hot, Red Hot, Do It NOW — and nothing ships on time anyway.
- **Local firefighting creates system damage.** Peach's intervention to fix one order directly triggers a labor dispute, a skilled machinist quitting, and a key machine being damaged. The cure is worse than the symptom.
- **Efficiency metrics don't match reality.** Peach cites good efficiency numbers while demanding more output — Rogo knows the two don't add up, but can't yet articulate why.
- **The plant is losing money** despite being busy. High utilization and late shipments coexist — a paradox the rest of the novel will explain.

## Management Observations

- **Crisis management compounds the crisis.** Peach bypasses the chain of command to expedite one order; the resulting disruption costs more than the late shipment would have.
- **Urgency without diagnosis is destructive.** "Build the damn products" is not a strategy. The pressure is real, but the direction is empty.
- **The three-month clock is the narrative engine.** Goldratt uses this deadline to force Rogo (and the reader) toward finding a fundamentally different way to think about plant performance.
