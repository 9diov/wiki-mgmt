# Chapter 34

## What Happens

**Evening reflection with Julie.** Rogo describes the day's conversations. Julie points out what Jonah warned about: Rogo would be calling Jonah right now for answers on Lou's measurement project, Bob's sales-engineering idea, Stacey's CCR problem, and Ralph's information system. Each of those is essential, and Rogo has no answers for any of them. The dependency would grow, not shrink. The only solution is to answer Jonah's question first: what are the techniques needed for management?

**The team works on it.** Rogo gathers the team for afternoon sessions. He poses the most naive first question: "What are the first things I should do when I assume my new position?" They suggest the standard onboarding moves: fact-finding meetings, plant tours, client visits. He draws their suggestions as colored shapes on the whiteboard — circles, rectangles, stars, overlapping blobs — until the board is a mess.

His point: this is what fact-finding produces. Not knowledge — chaos. A pile of facts with no intrinsic order. Any arrangement they propose (by size, by color, by type) is arbitrary. The usual responses to this pile are:
1. Write thick reports
2. Reorganize the company

Neither achieves anything. They leave with no answer.

## Management Observations

- **Data without intrinsic order is not knowledge.** Fact-finding is the standard first step because it feels productive. But a pile of facts with no organizing principle is just a pile. Any order imposed on it is arbitrary — which is exactly what thick reports and organizational reshufflings are.
- **The reorganization reflex.** When an executive doesn't know what to do, reorganizing is available — "shuffling the cards" as Bob calls it. Large companies cycle between centralization and decentralization every five to ten years. This is the organizational equivalent of arbitrary classification: not a solution, a performance of action.
- **The team's knowledge has limits.** The team that saved the plant cannot answer the management technique question because they solved a physical production problem. The divisional problem requires something more general: a method that works on policies, measurements, and human behavior, not just machines and schedules.
