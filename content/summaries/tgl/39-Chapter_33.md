# Chapter 33

## What Happens

**Lou's accounting bomb.** Lou reveals that the plant's actual net profit was over 20% in each of the last three months — not the 12.8% the audit recorded, and well above even the internally estimated 17%. The hidden distortion: inventory is reported as an **asset** on the balance sheet, valued at production cost (materials + value-added labor). As the plant drained 50% of WIP and 25% of finished goods, the reduction in inventory showed up as a write-down of assets — which hit the bottom line as a loss. Reducing inventory the right way made profits look smaller, not larger.

Lou's rule: "We were penalized for doing the right thing." The inventory accounting system creates incentives to hold excess inventory (which appears as asset growth) and penalizes reducing it (which appears as asset shrinkage).

Frost understood this before the review, which is why he was supportive despite the official numbers.

**Team succession.**

- **Lou** asks to come along to the division, not to retire. He wants one last job: designing a measurement system that actually induces the parts to do what's good for the whole. He won't stop because he sees now that efficiency reports and product-cost calculations have been actively leading workers and managers to do the wrong thing.

- **Bob** declines the divisional production role (to Rogo's surprise). He wants to stay in the plant and develop what the Burnside order revealed: **production can engineer a sale**. Instead of quoting rigid lead times, the plant should calculate availability case-by-case against bottleneck load, and re-engineer delivery terms to fit both customer need and plant capability. He wants to make this standard.

- **Stacey** accepts the production manager job. She explains why she wanted it: her buffer-monitoring work revealed a set of **Capacity Constraint Resources (CCRs)** — six or seven work centers that aren't bottlenecks but whose sequencing has become critical. Holes in the assembly buffer trace back to these CCRs every time. She wants to use buffer management not just to track problems but to focus local improvement efforts — preventing CCRs from becoming bottlenecks as sales grow.

- **Ralph** wants to stay and build better information systems: one for "engineering sales" (Bob's project), one for buffer management, one for local performance measurement. His insight: information is the answer to a specific question asked, not just stored data. He finally feels like part of the team.

## Management Observations

- **Inventory accounting creates perverse incentives.** Valuing inventory at production cost means reducing it looks like destroying assets. Companies building excess finished goods to "generate profits" (common in slow periods) are exploiting this distortion. Eliminating the distortion is not a minor bookkeeping change — it removes a major driver of bad decisions throughout the organization.
- **The Burnside episode revealed a new role for production.** Production is traditionally a cost center that executes sales orders. The Burnside response — re-engineering the order terms rather than saying yes or no — is a different model: production as active participant in structuring what can be sold. Bob wants to formalize this.
- **CCRs are not bottlenecks but require active management.** A Capacity Constraint Resource is a non-bottleneck whose sequencing becomes critical because it feeds the bottleneck and assembly. It won't starve the plant outright, but it creates holes in buffers if not managed — and those holes generate the expediting behavior everyone wants to avoid.
