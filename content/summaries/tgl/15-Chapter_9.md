# Chapter 9

> **Note:** Chapters 7 and 8 are missing from the source epub extraction. Chapter 9 picks up the morning after chapter 6, with Rogo waking at his mother's house.

## What Happens

Rogo wakes at 11 AM on his childhood mattress, calls the plant to check in, and learns that corporate wants to film CEO Granby at the plant — standing in front of a robot. The director likes the robot's color. Rogo notes the irony: the chairman is coming to celebrate productivity improvements that, as Jonah showed, didn't actually improve productivity.

Driving home to clean up, Rogo makes the connection he missed the night before. Jonah's three diagnostic questions from O'Hare weren't arbitrary — they map precisely to the T/I/OE measurements:

| Jonah's question | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Did you ship more products? | Did **throughput** go up? |
| Did you reduce headcount? | Did **operating expense** go down? |
| Did inventories go down? | Did **inventory** go down? |

From this, the goal restated through [[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐|T/I/OE]] becomes clear:

> *Increase throughput while simultaneously reducing both inventory and operating expense.*

This is the [[Goal Formulation]] — the operational version of "make money." Any action that moves T up and I/OE down is productive. Any action that does the opposite is not.

Back at the plant, Rogo pulls together Lou (controller), Stacey (inventory control), and Bob Donovan (production) to audit the robots. The findings confirm the [[Local Optimization Trap (synth)]] in detail:

1. Robots launched at 30% efficiency. Finance complained — expensive capital sitting idle.
2. To raise robot utilization, Stacey released more raw materials to the floor.
3. Robot efficiencies climbed. Reports looked good.
4. But the robots produced parts faster than downstream steps could consume them — because other needed components weren't ready.
5. Piles of robot-made parts accumulated, waiting for matching components that never arrived in sync.
6. Throughput: no increase. Inventory: significantly up. Operating expense: up (depreciation + carrying costs).

The robots didn't increase productivity by any measure that matters. They increased local efficiency while making the system worse. Stacey names the underlying pattern: *"Whenever efficiencies take a drop, everybody draws against the future forecast to keep busy. We build inventory."*

Rogo ends the meeting: "I think I need to explain a few things." Chapter 10 will be the briefing.

## The Pivot from Diagnosis to Shared Understanding

Chapters 1–8 were Rogo's private reckoning. Chapter 9 is the first time he brings others into the new frame. The robot audit isn't just a postmortem — it's a demonstration to his team that the measurements they've been managing against (efficiency, cost-per-part, utilization) systematically mislead them.

## Management Observations

- **Jonah's questions were T/I/OE in disguise.** The diagnostic from Chapter 4 and the framework from Chapter 6 are the same tool. Rogo didn't need to be told this — he worked it out himself on the drive home. Jonah's method: plant the question, let the student connect it.
- **The efficiency trap is self-reinforcing.** When robot efficiency dropped, the *correct* response by every conventional metric was to release more material. That action was rational under the old framework and destructive under the new one.
- **The carrying cost of inventory is operational expense.** Rogo pushes back on Lou's "cost-per-part went down" claim: if inventory carrying costs went up and throughput didn't, the real cost went up. Local cost reduction and system cost reduction are different things.
