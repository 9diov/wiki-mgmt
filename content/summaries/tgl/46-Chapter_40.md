# Chapter 40

## What Happens

**Division-level reality.** Rogo and Lou spend two weeks learning the division. Donovan's plant is the only bright spot — it's the sole source of profits while the rest of the division loses money. When they separate out Donovan's results, the picture is "quite disastrous."

**The receivables trap.** Lou wants to fix open receivables (four days' cash). Rogo redirects him: in the same time, Lou could address inventory accounting, which distorts finished-goods levels by 70 days, blocks new product launches, and actively destroys throughput. The receivables optimization is real but marginal — it's the wrong constraint to work on first.

**Policy constraints vs. machine constraints.** Rogo makes a key argument: the real constraints at the plant were never the machines. The NCX-10 and the oven appeared to be bottlenecks, but the plant squeezed almost twice the throughput from the same machines without buying new capacity. The actual constraints were the **policies** — efficiency incentives, batch-size rules, material release logic, inventory valuation. Machines gave them a visible target; the real work was changing the rules around the machines.

At the division, there are no visible machine bottlenecks. The constraints are pure policy: erroneous measurements, outdated products, no viable marketing strategy, a culture of blame-shifting. All of these are connected — they are likely expressions of one or a few root causes, not independent problems.

**Three questions.** Rogo and Lou revisit the five steps in light of the division-level problem. They arrive at three questions that the management techniques must answer:

1. **What to change?** — requires a technique for identifying the core problem (the root that causes all the visible undesirable effects)
2. **What to change to?** — requires a technique for constructing and verifying solutions that fix all bad effects without creating new ones
3. **How to cause the change?** — requires a technique for implementing change in the face of inertia and resistance, converting resistance into enthusiasm

These are not operational improvements. They are thinking processes. Julie predicted it: the techniques Jonah would teach would not be mere techniques — they would be ways of reasoning.

**The call to Jonah.** Rogo now knows what to ask. He will call Jonah and ask to be taught the three thinking processes.

## Management Observations

- **Constraints at higher levels of an organization are almost always policies, not resources.** The farther up the organization you go, the less likely you are to find a physical bottleneck. You find instead: wrong metrics that reward local optimization, bad product decisions driven by accounting artifacts, resistance to change embedded in habit and culture. The five-step process still applies — but "identify the constraint" now means identifying the root policy, not the loaded machine.
- **The three questions are the core job of management.** Every management activity is ultimately an attempt to answer one of these three questions. If a manager cannot answer all three, they are reacting to crises rather than leading change. Jonah built his entire guidance of Rogo around forcing Rogo to find these answers himself — which is the same answer to the question "how to cause the change."
- **Rogo's journey ends where it began — with a question from Jonah.** Jonah never gave answers. He gave questions that forced Rogo to construct the answers, ensuring ownership and conviction. The final question ("what techniques are needed for management?") is itself the technique.
