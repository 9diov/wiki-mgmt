# Chapter 35

## What Happens

**Mendeleev's periodic table.** Ralph tells the story. By the mid-19th century, 63 chemical elements had been identified — a mess of different substances with no apparent order. Most chemists gave up on finding one and focused on practical combination work. Mendeleev took a different approach: he looked for the intrinsic order underlying the elements, using atomic weight as a quantitative identifier. He arranged the elements in a table with repeating columns (elements with similar chemical behavior), and where the pattern demanded an element that hadn't been found yet, he invented it — predicted its properties. His predictions proved accurate to within fractions of a percent.

**The key distinction.** The team's earlier attempts to arrange their colored shapes on the whiteboard were all arbitrary — they imposed external order on a random collection. Mendeleev revealed an intrinsic order that already existed in the data. The reason his table was powerful: it reflected something real about the world. Their shapes didn't.

Ralph's formulation: *"If you just arrange data in some order to produce reports and histograms, you're playing games. If you reveal the intrinsic order, you can predict things that haven't been found yet."*

**The management parallel.** An intrinsic order exists in how organizations work — if you can find it, you can reason from it. Scientists use IF...THEN logic: start from one phenomenon, hypothesize a cause, derive what else must be true if the hypothesis is correct, verify. This builds chains of connected facts from a single cause.

Julie, reading Plato's dialogues, makes the connection: Socrates works the same way — IF you believe X, THEN you must also believe Y. The method applies equally to material facts and human behavior. Together they point to what Jonah's "thinking processes" must be: a method for reasoning from observations to root causes and back.

Rogo's self-correction: Jonah said not to develop the techniques — only to determine what they should be. The answer to that question must come from examining how he currently operates and what he actually needs, not from reading physics books.

## Management Observations

- **The difference between knowledge and data is intrinsic order.** A table of atomic weights is data. Mendeleev's periodic table is knowledge — it reveals what must be true about undiscovered elements. The distinction matters for management: gathering more data doesn't produce knowledge if no organizing principle is applied.
- **IF...THEN logic scales from physics to human behavior.** The same reasoning structure that Newton used for gravity and Mendeleev used for the elements works for understanding organizational dynamics. This is the basis for Goldratt's "Thinking Processes" introduced in later books.
- **Common sense is intuition masked by common practice.** The solutions look obvious afterward. But so did the periodic table. The difficulty is not in the answer — it's in peeling away the practiced assumptions that obscure it.
