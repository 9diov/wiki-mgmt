# Chapter 5

## What Happens

Rogo can't concentrate in Peach's meeting. The management jargon sounds like noise — "consistent parameters," "matrix of advantage," "operational indices" — and he realizes with sudden clarity that no one in the room, including himself, can say what productivity actually means or what the company is fundamentally trying to do. He quietly packs his briefcase and walks out.

He drives aimlessly for a couple of hours, then stops at a pizza place, loads up on food and beer, and parks on a gravel hillside overlooking the plant. Sitting alone in the sun, he finally has space to think.

He works through [[The Goal|the goal question]] Jonah left unanswered. He tries each candidate and eliminates it:

- **Cost-effective purchasing?** Purchasing acts like it is — they've rented warehouses to store thirty-two months of copper wire bought at great prices. Clearly not the goal.
- **Employing people?** The plant has already laid off 600. Jobs are a consequence, not a purpose.
- **Producing products?** Warehouses full of finished goods nobody has bought. Production alone proves nothing.
- **Quality?** Rolls-Royce nearly went bankrupt. Quality is necessary but not sufficient.
- **Efficiency + quality together?** Volkswagen stopped making Bugs; Douglas stopped making DC-3s. Efficient production of quality products isn't enough if nobody wants them anymore.
- **Technology?** R&D sits off to the side in every org chart. Leading-edge technology is a tool, not the destination.
- **Market share?** You can dominate a market and still lose money — "we'll make it up on volume" is a famous last phrase.

Looking at the two warehouses full of unsold finished goods beyond the plant, the answer clicks: the company doesn't exist to fill a warehouse. It exists **to make money**.

> *"The goal of a manufacturing organization is to make money."*

Everything else — quality, efficiency, technology, customer satisfaction — enables money to be made. But none of them is the goal. None of them is worth anything if the company isn't profitable. And nothing else can substitute for money as the organizing purpose; a company that breaks even is still dying.

He reformulates Jonah's productivity definition through this lens: an action that moves the plant toward making money is productive; an action that moves it away is not.

## The Pivot

This is the chapter where Rogo's thinking shifts from reactive (fighting fires, blaming competition) to diagnostic (what are we actually optimizing for?). The answer sounds obvious — of course companies exist to make money — but the rest of the book will show that almost every standard management practice in the plant is optimizing for something *other* than money.

## Management Observations

- **The goal is obvious in hindsight but invisible in practice.** Everyone running the plant would agree "we need to make money," yet every daily decision is made against metrics — efficiency, utilization, cost per part — that have no direct connection to it.
- **Naming the goal exposes all the proxies.** Once Rogo writes down the list of "goals" everyone chases, they immediately look like [[Means vs. Goal|means, not ends]]. The list is a catalogue of things the plant is currently optimizing at the expense of the actual goal.
- **A company that breaks even is still failing.** Rogo's realization that breaking even wouldn't save the plant is important: the goal is not survival, it's making money. The distinction forces him to think about the positive — how does the plant generate profit — rather than just minimizing losses.
