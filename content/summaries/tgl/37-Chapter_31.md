# Chapter 31

## What Happens

**The review.** Rogo arrives at UniCo headquarters for his performance review. Peach is absent — "involved in negotiations." Hilton Smyth runs the meeting with the division controller Neil Cravitz. Smyth's agenda: Rogo violated EBQ procedures, product costs went up, he should be called to order.

Rogo opens with a question: "Is the goal of UniWare to reduce costs?" Smyth says yes. Rogo says no — the goal is to make money. He then spends an hour and a half explaining bottlenecks, flow, and inventory.

Smyth dismisses all of it as "simple common sense" and "nothing fundamental." The argument crystallizes around three false assumptions that Rogo names explicitly:

1. Balance capacity to demand first, then manage flow — wrong. Balance flow to demand; excess capacity is required.
2. Worker utilization is determined by his own potential — wrong. For non-bottlenecks, utilization is determined by the constraint.
3. Utilization and activation are the same — wrong.

Smyth refuses to accept any of it. His final position: cost of products went up, profits must go down, that's his report to Peach.

**Corner office.** Rogo bypasses the elevator and walks directly into Peach's office. Peach listens. He calls in Smyth, Frost, and Jons. Smyth recommends closing the plant. Frost notes the plant has turned profits while releasing cash. Jons calls the plant capable of "miracles." Smyth insists it's temporary.

Peach: "Do you think we're such bad managers that we would close a gold mine?"

**The promotion.** Peach, Frost, and Jons are moving up to group level. They've chosen Rogo to replace Peach as division manager. Smyth, who ran the review that was supposed to end Rogo's career, is in the room when it's announced.

**Aftermath.** Rogo reaches Jonah. Jonah congratulates him but declines to come help. His reason: Rogo needs to reduce dependency on external guidance as his responsibilities grow, not increase it. He gives Rogo an assignment: determine what techniques are needed for effective management. Not develop them — just determine what they should be.

## Management Observations

- **The three false assumptions are structural.** They are not incidental errors; they are built into standard management practice. Cost accounting, efficiency incentives, and activation/utilization confusion all flow from them. Naming them explicitly is the first step to replacing them.
- **Bottom-line results are hard to argue with.** Smyth had all the cost data on his side. What defeated him was profit, cash flow, and a customer who arrived by helicopter to say thank you. Rogo's position was only defensible because the results were undeniable.
- **Jonah refuses to be a dependency.** His final lesson: the job of a guide is to make himself unnecessary. Rogo must learn to reason without a guide — particularly as his responsibilities grow beyond what any single advisor could know in detail.
