# Chapter 10

## What Happens

An hour and a half after the robot audit ends, Rogo has walked Lou, Stacey, and Bob through the full framework on the conference room whiteboard: the goal, the three financial measurements, and the T/I/OE definitions. The team works through what the definitions actually cover.

**Bob** immediately challenges the word "sales" in throughput — manufacturing has nothing to do with sales. Rogo points him to the window: two warehouses full of finished goods nobody's bought. Would the company stay in business just filling those? Bob concedes.

**Lou** offers the cleanest summary of [[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐|T/I/OE]] in the book:

> *"Throughput is the money coming in. Inventory is the money currently inside the system. And operational expense is the money we have to pay out to make throughput happen."*

One measurement for incoming money, one for money stuck inside, one for money going out.

**Stacey** pushes on why labor isn't embedded in inventory value. The team works it out: we buy employee time but don't sell it — the product is what's sold, not the time inside it. All employee time, whether idle or active, is operating expense. Lou adds the practical benefit: it eliminates the accounting game of reclassifying idle time as process time to inflate inventory value.

**Bob** runs a stress-test, throwing edge cases at the framework: tooling, machines, the building, lubricating oil, scrap, knowledge from consultants, R&D, and — his trump card — Granby's chauffeur. Everything fits:
- Machines: depreciation → OE; residual sellable value → inventory
- Oil, scrap losses: OE (not going to be sold)
- Knowledge: OE if it improves the process; inventory if it will be sold (patent, license)
- Chauffeur: OE — he helps Granby generate throughput, even if indirectly

The team reaches agreement: the framework is complete. Everything in the plant falls into one of the three.

Then Rogo delivers the other news: three months or Peach closes the plant. Bob and Stacey are stunned.

The deadlock arrives quickly. Stacey suggests stopping the excess material releases to reduce inventory. Bob objects: if production slows, efficiencies drop, and Peach will see bad numbers and close them faster. Lou agrees. The team has the new framework but no path forward under the old measurement system.

Rogo calls Jonah in New York. Jonah, woken from sleep, offers breakfast tomorrow at 7 AM at his hotel. Rogo books a flight that night.

## The Framework Takes Hold

This chapter converts the T/I/OE framework from Rogo's private insight to a shared language for his team. The classification exercise isn't just housekeeping — it stress-tests the framework and confirms it is genuinely complete. Every asset, every cost, every investment lands somewhere.

## Management Observations

- **Lou's "money in/inside/out" framing is the most useful mnemonic for T/I/OE.** It immediately tells you which direction each measurement should move: T up (more money in), I down (less money stuck), OE down (less money out).
- **The efficiency-vs-inventory deadlock is the central practical problem.** The team can *see* that releasing excess material to feed the robots was wrong, but they can't act on that knowledge without destroying the efficiency numbers Peach watches. The new framework and the old measurement system are in direct conflict.
- **Classification exercises expose implicit assumptions.** Bob's edge cases (chauffeur, knowledge, scrap) aren't just jokes — they force the team to articulate exactly what each measurement means and why the boundaries are drawn where they are. The team that can classify Granby's chauffeur can classify anything.
