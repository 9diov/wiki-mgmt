# Chapter 38

## What Happens

**The French order.** Rogo, Lou, and Ralph drive to headquarters to meet with Johnny Jons. Rogo's ask: $10 million in additional orders to fill 20% spare capacity. Jons laughs — the market is brutal, pricing is zero-margin. He mentions in passing a French distributor, Djangler, who offered $701/unit for Model 12 (vs. their standard price of $992 and the Burnside price of $827). Jons killed the negotiation — below cost, impractical.

**The contribution margin insight.** Lou confirms the material cost of Model 12 is $334. Rogo accepts the French order immediately. His logic: with spare capacity, the only incremental cost of production is materials. Every dollar above materials goes straight to the bottom line. The "cost" calculation that made the order look unprofitable assumed 100% overhead allocation — which assumes the capacity was otherwise generating throughput. It wasn't. It was idle.

**Three-week delivery as competitive moat.** Rogo's additional offer: promise Djangler three-week delivery for any reasonable quantity ordered. European lead times are 8–12 weeks. This isn't just pricing — it's a competitive differentiator that makes a price war irrelevant. Jons locks in a one-year contract with slightly higher prices than Djangler's original offer, in exchange for the delivery flexibility.

**The financial result.** The contribution to net profit: seven digits, calculated to the last cent by Lou on the drive home. The order that Jons "killed" turns out to be the most profitable thing the plant has done.

**Physics as a management model.** Rogo visits the public library and reads popular science. He observes that physicists don't start with data collection. They start with one phenomenon, hypothesize a cause, and derive what else must be true using IF...THEN logic. He reports this to Julie. She recognizes it as exactly how the Socratic dialogues work — same reasoning structure applied to human behavior rather than material facts. Together they sketch the outlines of what Jonah's "thinking processes" must involve.

## Management Observations

- **Marginal cost is the relevant cost for spare capacity.** Product cost calculations allocate overhead to each unit. When capacity is idle, the overhead is paid whether the unit is produced or not. The true incremental cost of a new order is only the materials it consumes. This principle is widely known in economics (marginal cost pricing) and widely ignored in corporate accounting.
- **Lead time is a competitive weapon.** Three-week delivery in a market where eight weeks is standard makes price comparisons almost irrelevant. Customers who need certainty will pay for it. The plant's speed advantage is a direct product of DBR and batch size reduction — every prior chapter built toward this moment.
- **The below-cost pricing instinct is almost always wrong when capacity exists.** Jons's reflex was to kill the Djangler order as a "loss." Lou's reflex was the same until redirected. The correct question is not "does this cover our full cost?" but "does this cover our marginal cost and contribute something to fixed costs we're paying anyway?"
