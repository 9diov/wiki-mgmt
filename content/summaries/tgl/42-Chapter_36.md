# Chapter 36

## What Happens

**The goal of a manager.** Stacey opens: if the company's goal is "to make more money now as well as in the future," then the manager's job is to move the organization toward that goal — continuously. Since "more" is open-ended, the job is never done. Lou reformulates: "A good job will be to start the division on a **process of on-going improvement.**"

**Cost world vs. throughput world.** Lou writes T/I/OE on the board. He observes: everywhere else, "improvement" is nearly synonymous with cost reduction — operating expenses are treated as the primary measurement. What the plant actually did was switch scales. Throughput became number one. Then inventory (because it affects throughput). Operating expense came last. Their numbers confirm it: throughput and inventory changed by tens of percent; operating expenses moved by less than 2%.

Lou names this: moving from the **cost world** into the **throughput world**.

**The process.** Bob then makes the key observation. The distinction isn't just about scale — it's about the word "process" in "process of on-going improvement." They didn't launch a series of improvement projects; they followed a specific **process**. Bob circles the word "process" on the board.

The team reconstructs what they did. Ralph's framing: in the cost world, strength comes from managing every link. In the throughput world, strength comes from managing the weakest link. The chain analogy. The first step is obvious: identify the weakest link — the bottleneck.

The five steps emerge naturally:

1. **Identify** the system's constraints
2. Decide how to **exploit** the system's constraints
3. **Subordinate** everything else to the above decision
4. **Elevate** the system's constraints
5. If a constraint has been broken, go back to step 1

## Management Observations

- **"Cost world" vs. "throughput world" is a paradigm shift, not an adjustment.** In the cost world, every link matters equally — so improvement means finding cost anywhere. In the throughput world, the constraint determines everything — so improvement means focusing only on the constraint. These frames produce completely different behaviors, even when people use the same words.
- **The five steps are a closed loop, not a one-time fix.** Every time a constraint is elevated to the point of breaking, the system shifts and a new constraint emerges. The process is not a project with an endpoint; it is the ongoing discipline of management.
- **The process was followed without naming it.** The team moved through these steps multiple times (NCX-10 → heat-treat → material release → market) without having a label for what they were doing. Naming it is necessary for teaching it to others and applying it in new contexts.
