# Chapter 15

## What Happens

Still on the trail after lunch, Rogo works through why the balanced model failed. He recalls a principle from a math class: covariance — in a linear dependency, fluctuations downstream will accumulate around the maximum deviation set by any preceding variable. This is the math behind what he watched: the statistical dips don't average out; they stack.

He also discovers that while he was thinking, the boys rearranged themselves. Herbie drifted to the back to avoid holding anyone up. The rest spontaneously sorted into fastest-at-front, slowest-at-back — the intuitive "optimal" order.

Rogo watches what happens: the gaps widen faster than ever. In this order, each boy walks at his own maximum pace, unimpeded. Nobody is stuck behind anyone. But the column is exploding — spreading fastest at the front, where the fastest boys are running away from the constraint entirely.

The insight: this arrangement maximizes *individual* efficiency and destroys *system* throughput. Herbie at the back constrains only Rogo. The gap between the front and Herbie grows continuously. Throughput — Rogo's pace at the end — is still governed by Herbie's rate. It's just now also accumulating the maximum inventory (trail gap) possible.

He also sees something sharper: before lunch, Herbie was trying extra hard. During that effort, the constraint wasn't obviously Herbie — it was whoever happened to be slowest at any given moment, shifting around the troop. The constraint can *float*. But Herbie has the least overall capacity, so he ultimately governs the system rate.

**Rogo reverses the order.** He stops the column, has everyone join hands, and physically walks Herbie to the front. New rule: nobody passes anyone. Herbie leads.

It works immediately. No gaps form. When one boy pauses to adjust his pack, everyone simply walks a little faster for a moment and catches up — because now there is spare capacity in the back (the fast boys) to absorb fluctuations. The column stays together.

Then the fast boys start complaining. Rogo's response: *"If you want to go faster, you have to figure out a way to let Herbie go faster."*

They open Herbie's pack. It weighs a ton — six-pack of soda, canned spaghetti, candy bars, pickles, tuna fish, a rain coat, rubber boots, tent stakes, an iron skillet, and a collapsible shovel. Rogo redistributes the weight across the other boys. Herbie, relieved, shoots to the front.

The troop now moves at twice its previous speed and stays together. They cover the remaining four miles in two hours. Inventory is down. Throughput is up.

That evening, Rogo and Dave talk through everything from the tent. Dave tells him he was proud of him. Rogo tells him what he learned — the stuff that will fix the plant.

## The Two Moves

The chapter resolves the hike with two discrete actions that map directly to plant management:

**1. Put the constraint at the front (subordinate):** Removing Herbie from the back stops the gap formation. Now all excess capacity is *behind* the constraint and can close fluctuations rather than run away ahead of the constraint. The system's pace is still Herbie's pace — but now it's the actual output rate, not the source of accumulating inventory.

**2. Redistribute Herbie's pack (elevate):** Once the constraint governs the system, increasing the constraint's capacity increases system throughput directly. Taking weight off Herbie is the only action that can actually make the troop faster. Adding weight to Andy helps nothing — Andy wasn't the limit.

## Management Observations

- **Fastest-at-front is locally optimal and systemically destructive.** The spontaneous rearrangement looks efficient — everyone walks without restriction. It is the worst possible configuration for the system. High individual utilization with the constraint at the tail maximizes inventory and minimizes throughput.
- **The constraint can float.** Any resource can temporarily become the constraint. But overall capacity determines which resource is the *chronic* constraint — the one that governs system rate over time.
- **Spare capacity behind the constraint absorbs fluctuations.** With Herbie at the front, the faster boys behind him have excess capacity. When anyone slows momentarily, the excess capacity closes the gap without drama. This is why slack is not waste — it is the buffer that makes the system resilient.
- **To speed up the system, speed up the constraint.** Rogo takes most of Herbie's load himself, because he is the biggest. The manager's job is to carry the weight the constraint cannot.
