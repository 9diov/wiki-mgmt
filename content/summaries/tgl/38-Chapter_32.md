# Chapter 32

## What Happens

**Restaurant dinner with Julie.** They are celebrating Rogo's promotion. But he doesn't want to toast it as a step up the pyramid — to him the family paid too high a price for it to feel like a clean win. Julie pushes back: he fought fairly, didn't step on anyone, and if he had given up the failure would have spoiled the marriage anyway. They toast the promotion as "reassurance to an exciting, worthwhile journey."

**Who gets the credit.** Rogo argues that Jonah deserves most of the credit — he provided all the answers. Julie refuses to accept this. Her point: Jonah didn't hand solutions on a silver platter. Rogo and the team sweated through the questions for weeks before implementing anything. The conviction they gained through the struggle is what gave them the courage to act. Ownership was a prerequisite to implementation.

**Why couldn't they see it themselves?** Julie presses: the solutions look like common sense — so why couldn't the plant have figured it out without Jonah? Rogo's answer: what we call "common sense" is something we already know intuitively. But intuition gets masked by **common practice**. The Socratic method is effective precisely because it peels away those layers of practiced assumption without telling people what to believe — it leads them to construct the conclusion themselves. Telling people the answer either goes over their heads or provokes defensiveness ("constructive criticism" is worse than regular criticism).

**The next question.** Rogo identifies his next learning goal: not just the answers, but the method. How to lead others through the same process — the "thinking processes" that let a scientist or a Socrates build from one fact to another through IF...THEN logic, revealing intrinsic order. Julie notes that Plato's dialogues work exactly the same way. She's been reading them.

## Management Observations

- **Ownership of a solution is a prerequisite to implementing it.** People act on conclusions they arrived at themselves; they deflect conclusions handed to them. This is why consulting engagements that hand over a report fail, and why Jonah only asked questions.
- **Common practice is the enemy of common sense.** The solutions that saved the plant were intuitively obvious after the fact. What blocked them for years was a deep layer of practiced behavior that felt equally obvious. Peeling that layer requires guided questioning, not argument.
- **Effective persuasion requires more than questions.** The Socratic method isn't just "ask questions." Improvising it fails; Rogo tried and it came across as patronizing. Jonah appeared to know Rogo's next question before Rogo did. The technique requires understanding the logical structure of the other person's beliefs before beginning.
