# Chapter 6

## What Happens

Rogo returns to the plant at 4:30 PM. Walking the floor, he catches three workers sitting idle and orders their supervisor to keep them busy. Then he stops himself: those three guys are now moving materials from one side of an aisle to the other. They're working — but is that productive? Does *busy* equal *making money*?

He can't answer the question. The standard measurements — parts per hour, labor variances, efficiency ratios — describe activity, not progress toward the goal. He needs a cleaner link between daily operations and whether the company is actually making money.

He calls in Lou, the plant controller, and works through it with him. Together they land on three [[Financial Measurements|financial measurements]] that define whether a company is making money:

1. **Net profit** — absolute dollars earned after expenses
2. **Return on investment (ROI)** — earnings relative to money invested
3. **Cash flow** — the survival line: go below zero and no amount of profit or ROI saves you

Lou confirms what Rogo already fears: the plant is losing cash. Two years from retirement, Lou lights a cigarette and goes quiet. Rogo commits to the three months — but he still doesn't know how to translate net profit, ROI, and cash flow into decisions on the plant floor.

He misses dinner again, sees his daughter's all-A report card, can't sleep. Alone in the kitchen at midnight, he decides: he needs Jonah.

He drives to his childhood home to find an old address book. Three hours of rummaging through the attic and basement follow — old report cards, model airplanes, a photograph of Uncle Paul before the embezzlement arrest — before finding it in the top drawer of his childhood desk. He calls Israel, is passed through multiple contacts, and finally reaches Jonah in London at 3 AM.

Jonah confirms the goal. Then Rogo asks the real question: the three financial measurements don't translate to the plant floor. How does he know if any specific action — a setup, a batch run, a staffing decision — is productive or not?

Jonah's answer: he developed a different set of measurements, three of them, that express the goal but can also generate operational rules. He gives Rogo the definitions:

- **[[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐|Throughput]]** — the rate at which the system generates money *through sales* (not production)
- **[[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐|Inventory]]** — all the money the system has invested in purchasing things it intends to sell
- **[[Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense ⭐|Operating Expense]]** — all the money the system spends to turn inventory into throughput

Jonah hangs up before explaining how to use them. Rogo falls asleep on his childhood mattress, three definitions written on a notepad.

## The Bridge from Finance to Operations

The chapter solves the problem Chapter 5 left open. "Make money" is the goal — but net profit, ROI, and cash flow are board-level abstractions. They can't tell Eddie the second-shift supervisor whether to run a particular batch tonight.

Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense (T, I, OE) are designed to solve exactly that: they restate the goal in terms that connect to what happens on the plant floor. The rest of the book will show how.

## Management Observations

- **Busy ≠ productive.** The three workers moving materials are a microcosm of the whole plant: high activity, unclear connection to making money. Every standard factory metric has this same problem — it measures effort, not contribution to the goal.
- **Cash flow is the survival constraint.** You can show net profit and a good ROI and still go bankrupt if cash runs out. Peach's urgency isn't about bad quarterly numbers — the division is bleeding cash.
- **The T/I/OE definitions are precise by design.** Jonah is careful: throughput is through *sales*, not production. Producing without selling doesn't count. This distinction, which seems minor, will have large consequences for how the plant is managed.
