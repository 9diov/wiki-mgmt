# Chapter 6: Surviving the Low-Growth Period

## Core Argument

High growth masked two problems: the false efficiency of automation that still required fixed headcounts, and the hidden costs of overproduction. When growth stopped, both became visible. Toyota's response was not to retreat but to extend its system further — designing lines and machines to operate with variable numbers of workers, so that falling volume could be absorbed by reducing headcount rather than accumulating inventory. The distinguishing capability of TPS is not producing efficiently at high volumes. It is producing efficiently when volume falls.

## Key Ideas

**The high-growth illusion: automation without autonomation**
During Japan's high-growth period, companies raced to buy large, high-performance machines — transfer machines, robots, automation. In a period when everything made was sold, these machines appeared effective. But they had a critical flaw: they could not detect abnormalities or stop themselves. Operators were still required to supervise them. Manual work "changed names" rather than disappearing. The number of workers did not fall with automation; costs did not fall either.

Ohno's diagnosis: *"It would be all right if we were prepared to reduce the number of workers by half using high-performance machines. But that did not happen."* True worker saving requires [[Autonomation (Jidoka) ⭐]] — machines that monitor themselves and free operators entirely under normal conditions. Without it, automation is expensive capital investment that leaves the labor cost unchanged.

**The oil crisis reveals fixed-worker fragility**
When the 1973 oil crisis forced a 50% production reduction, a specific problem emerged: autonomated machines that required two operators (one at input, one at output) still required two operators at half volume. The fixed operator assignment — inherited from an assumption that the machine would always run at full rate — created a situation where the cost per unit doubled when volume halved.

This was the problem that high growth had hidden. At full production, two workers per machine looked fine. At half production, it was an anchor.

**[[Variable Workforce (Shoninka)]] — the structural response**
Toyota's answer was to redesign lines and machines to be operable by a variable number of workers. A five-worker line is configured so that four workers can operate it if one is absent — at 80% output, proportional to headcount. Multi-skill training is the prerequisite: workers must be capable of covering adjacent processes so that fewer people can span the same range of operations.

The goal: *"A production line or a machine can be operated by one, two, or any number of workers."* Volume drops by 30%? Reduce the line to 70% staffing. Volume recovers? Add workers back. The line flexes; the cost per unit stays roughly constant regardless of volume.

This is the distinction between "labor saving" (reducing workers at a fixed volume) and "worker saving" (designing the system so that the number of workers tracks the required volume continuously).

**Productivity improvement when volume falls — the harder problem**
Ohno closes with what he regards as Toyota's most important distinguishing capability:

> *"There must be hundreds of people around the world who can improve productivity and efficiency by increasing production quantity. Few people in the world can raise productivity when production quantities decrease. With even one such person, the character of a business operation will be that much stronger."*

Anyone can lower unit cost by spreading fixed overhead over more units. The difficult skill — and the one that determines survival in low-growth periods — is reducing cost as volume falls. This requires variable workforce design, relentless waste elimination, and the willingness to make the system work with less rather than adding more.

**Inverse thinking as a continuous discipline**
Ohno's closing reflection: his mind tends to crystallize, so he renews his determination daily. The entire TPS was built by inverting common sense — moving the car instead of the parts, pulling instead of pushing, stopping instead of running through defects, making less instead of more. This inversion habit is not a technique to be learned once; it is a discipline to be practiced every day.

## Management Observations

- **Automation without judgment is expensive supervision, not efficiency.** A machine that cannot stop itself requires continuous human monitoring. The capital cost buys speed, not independence. Worker count stays the same; unit economics worsen.
- **Fixed staffing assumptions are hidden liabilities.** Any machine or line designed around a fixed operator count is optimized for one volume and fragile at all others. The oil crisis made this visible; TPS addressed it structurally.
- **The test of a production system is how it behaves when demand falls.** High growth tolerates inefficiency. Low growth exposes it. A system that only works well at high volume is not a production system — it is a bet on perpetual growth.
- **Multi-skill training is infrastructure, not overhead.** The ability to flex workforce with volume depends entirely on workers who can cover multiple processes. This training investment is what makes variable staffing possible; without it, the line is rigid regardless of the mechanical design.
