# Chapter 4: Genealogy of the Toyota Production System

## Core Argument

TPS did not emerge from theory or consulting — it was the product of two men's unyielding pursuit of Japanese originality under real constraints. Toyoda Sakichi gave TPS autonomation (learned from watching a loom); Toyoda Kiichiro gave it just-in-time (learned from watching America and refusing to copy it). Ohno's role was to operationalize what both men envisioned. The system's genealogy explains its character: empirical, floor-rooted, born from necessity rather than designed from principle.

## Key Ideas

**The two founders and the two pillars**

Each pillar of TPS traces directly to one founder:

- **[[Autonomation (Jidoka) ⭐]]** ← Toyoda Sakichi. He spent a full day watching his neighbor's hand loom as a young man. That observation — standing in place, watching until understanding emerged — became Toyota's method. Ohno connects this directly to Five Whys: *"This Toyota procedure is actually adapted from Toyoda Sakichi's habit of watching."* The auto-activated loom that stopped on a broken thread was the physical expression of this philosophy.

- **[[Just-in-Time ⭐]]** ← Toyoda Kiichiro. After touring the U.S. automobile industry in 1930 and witnessing Ford's mass production firsthand, Kiichiro returned and refused to copy it. Japan's situation was different: small volumes, many models, no capital for dedicated tooling at American scale. His 1933 statement: *"We shall learn production techniques from the American method of mass production. But we will not copy it as is. We shall use our own research and creativity to develop a production method that suits our own country's situation."* Just-in-time was his answer — the organizing principle of a Japanese production system.

**Observation as the root of invention**
Both Toyoda men share the same intellectual habit: sustained, direct observation of the real thing. Sakichi watched the loom all day. Kiichiro went to America himself to see cars, visited plants, toured factories. Neither worked from desk analysis or borrowed theory. Ohno traces his own Five Whys discipline to this lineage — standing on the production floor, watching until the problem reveals itself.

**"Catch up with America" — but not by copying**
Kiichiro's 1945 instruction — catch up in three years — was not an instruction to replicate Ford. It was a challenge to find the Japanese equivalent. The difference matters: copying would have produced a smaller, worse version of American mass production. Refusing to copy forced the invention of something different. The impossibility of doing what America did (vast capital, enormous volumes, few models) was the constraint that generated JIT.

**The genealogy of context: textiles → automobiles**
Ohno joined Toyota from Toyoda Spinning and Weaving in 1943, transferring at age 30. His textile background was not incidental — the relationship between workers and machines in textile production was the template he carried into automobile manufacturing. Autonomation came directly from Sakichi's loom; multi-process operation came from watching one woman tend 40–50 looms. The automobile plant problems were new, but the underlying logic of human-machine coordination was already familiar.

**Dialectic evolution**
Ohno describes Toyota's development from Sakichi through Kiichiro to the present as "dialectic evolution" — not a straight line but a stream with rapid sections, slow sections, and apparent drying-up. The continuity is not smooth progress but a persistent thread: *something in the stream has been continual, solid, and based on Japanese creativity.* Successes and failures, favorable and adverse conditions, are all part of the same ongoing evolution.

## Management Observations

- **Constraints generate invention.** Japan couldn't afford American-scale mass production. The impossibility of copying forced JIT into existence. The constraint was the gift. Systems built from necessity are more robust than systems built from abundance.
- **The genealogy of an idea matters.** JIT and autonomation are not abstract principles — they each have a specific person, a specific observation, and a specific problem at their origin. Understanding the origin explains why the principles have the shape they do and where their limits lie.
- **The floor is where invention happens.** Neither Sakichi nor Kiichiro worked from an office. Sakichi's laboratory was his living room; his research method was watching. Kiichiro's insight came from touring foreign factories. Both men had the chessplayer's view — tactical detail and strategic overview simultaneously — because they never stopped observing the actual game.
