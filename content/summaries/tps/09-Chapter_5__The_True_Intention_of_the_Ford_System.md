# Chapter 5: The True Intention of the Ford System

## Core Argument

Ford invented the work-flow system — the foundation that both Ford and Toyota share. But Ford's successors misread his intention: they froze the flow at the final assembly line and reverted to large lots in every earlier process. Toyota took Ford's original idea further than Ford's own company did, extending synchronized flow all the way back to machining, stamping, and casting. In doing so, Toyota also recovered Ford's philosophy of waste elimination and true efficiency — which was never about quantity and speed, but about doing work in the best way known.

## Key Ideas

**The shared foundation: work flow**
Both systems are built on work flow — material moves through a sequence of processes, transformed at each step. Ford's original insight, as described by Charles Sorensen, was to move the chassis along the line rather than move the stockroom to the chassis. Toyota took the same principle and extended it: *"The difference is that, while Sorensen worried about warehousing parts, Toyota eliminated the warehouse."*

**Small lot sizes and quick setup — the reverse of Ford's successors**
Ford's successors embraced large lots as the cost-reduction strategy. Toyota does the opposite: small lot sizes, quick die changes, mixed-model sequencing. The rationale is total waste elimination — large lots generate inventory, and inventory generates every secondary waste downstream. To make small lots viable, setup time must approach zero. This is not a preference; it is a structural requirement of the system.

**Ford's true intention — misread by successors**
Ohno's argument is generous toward Ford and critical of what came after. Ford used the word "synchronization" — meaning he envisioned flow connecting all processes, not just final assembly. His successors stopped at the assembly line and built large-lot production in everything upstream. The result: smooth flow at the front, damned flow everywhere else. Toyota simply finished what Ford started.

The reason Ford's successors stopped: competitive pressure from General Motors in the 1920s. Sloan's full-line policy changed the market from a few models in high volume to many models across price points. In the resulting competitive scramble, studying the appropriate development of the Ford system halted. The large-lot compromise became permanent.

**Ford on waste — labor, not material**
Ford's waste philosophy deserves direct quotation: *"My theory of waste goes back of the thing itself into the labour of producing it... Material costs mean nothing. It is of no account until it comes into the hands of management."* Material gains value only through labor. Wasting material means wasting the labor that produced and transported it. The ideal is not clever recycling — it is producing nothing that needs to be salvaged. Ford saved 80 million pounds of steel per year from scrap by this logic.

This framing is identical to Ohno's: waste is the consumption of resources — human and material — without adding value that justifies the cost.

**Standards must be set by workers, not imposed from above**
Ford: *"No body of men could possibly have the knowledge to set up standards, for that knowledge must come from the inside of each manufacturing unit and not at all from the outside."* A standard imposed from outside production will bar progress — workers will make the standard rather than make for the customer. Both Ford and Ohno agree: standards emerge from the people doing the work, are posted and visible, and exist to be improved.

**[[Preventive Maintenance]] — the structural consequence of JIT**
JIT eliminates inventory buffers. With no safety stock, a machine breakdown immediately stops the downstream process. This makes prevention — not repair — the only viable strategy. *"If we think to keep inventory in anticipation of machine problems, why not consider preventing trouble before it occurs?"* Preventive maintenance is not optional in a JIT system; it is the mechanism that makes the absence of buffers survivable.

Ford had parallel thinking: he built hospitals and studied preventive medicine, arguing that finding the perfect food to maintain health was more valuable than building better treatment facilities. Ohno draws the analogy directly: a strong constitution prevents illness; preventive maintenance prevents breakdown.

**True efficiency — not quantity and speed**
Ford: *"Efficiency is merely the doing of work in the best way you know rather than in the worst way."* Speed and quantity are not measures of efficiency — they are temptations that generate overproduction. Toyota's suppression of overproduction is the practical expression of Ford's definition: produce what is needed using the best known method, at exactly the rate the market requires.

Ohno's inverse conception habit — questioning every assumption, looking at things upside-down — is explicitly credited to reading Ford: *"Is cotton the best material we can use here?"* is the same question as *"Is this the best method for producing this part?"*

## Management Observations

- **Large lots are a failure of nerve, not a law of production.** The belief that large lots reduce cost is true only within a narrow calculation that ignores inventory costs, secondary waste, and inflexibility. Toyota's die-setup discipline proved that what looked like an unavoidable constraint (2–3 hour changeovers) was a habit, not a physical law.
- **Synchronization requires extending flow backward, not just forward.** Final assembly line flow is easy; every visitor can see it. The harder discipline is establishing synchronized flow in stamping, casting, machining — the processes where large lots feel most natural. This is where most of the waste lives.
- **A buffer is a symptom, not a solution.** Keeping inventory to protect against machine breakdown defers the problem while multiplying its cost. The correct response to machine unreliability is to make machines reliable — then remove the buffer.
- **Reading competitors charitably is strategically valuable.** Ohno's careful reading of Ford reveals that TPS is not the rejection of the Ford system but its completion. This framing is both more intellectually honest and more practically useful than dismissing mass production entirely.
