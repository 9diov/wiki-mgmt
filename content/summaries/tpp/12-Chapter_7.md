# Chapter 7

## What Happens

Steve's assistant Stacy calls Bill to set up an impromptu meeting with Erik Reid — a potential board member in town for one day only. Bill arrives expecting a technology executive and finds a rumpled man in wrinkled khakis unpacking doughnuts, who can't seem to remember anyone's name correctly. He introduces himself as Erik.

Erik's opening assessment is blunt: IT Operations has lodged itself in the critical path of every major work flow, executives are furious, the company is in the newspaper, and anyone who knows Phoenix knows more bad news is coming. Bill deflects by noting he's been in the role for three days.

Erik challenges Bill's claim to know what "work" is. Bill names Phoenix — getting the project out the door. Erik says that's one type of work, and Bill is missing three others. Without understanding all four, any conversation about controlling work or fixing the organization's problems is premature.

Erik drives Bill to MRP-8, one of Parts Unlimited's manufacturing plants. From a catwalk overlooking the floor, he explains the plant's history: in earlier decades, WIP piled so high you couldn't see across the building. Jobs were released by whoever was free at the first work station, not based on the tempo of the bottleneck. Every day was an emergency. The company held a standing record as best customer of the air freight company from shipping thousands of pounds of finished goods overnight to angry customers.

He ties this to Goldratt's Theory of Constraints: improvements made anywhere other than the bottleneck are an illusion. Improvements before the bottleneck just pile more inventory at it; improvements after it are starved for work. The plant was transformed by freezing job releases and controlling work based on bottleneck capacity.

Bill pushes back — IT is not a factory, it's knowledge work. Erik rejects the framing: *"Your job as VP of IT Operations is to ensure the fast, predictable, and uninterrupted flow of planned work that delivers value to the business while minimizing the impact and disruption of unplanned work."*

He introduces the Three Ways briefly:
- **First Way:** fast flow of work from Development into IT Operations through to the customer
- **Second Way:** shorten and amplify feedback loops to fix quality at the source, avoid rework
- **Third Way:** culture of experimentation, learning from failure, and mastery through repetition

Erik gives Bill his phone number and a single task: name the other three types of work (beyond business projects). When he has them, call.

Back at his desk, Bill tries to puzzle out the answer. He considers IT infrastructure projects as a second type. He wonders about emails. He concludes Erik meant organizational-level work, not individual tasks. The chapter ends with his antique laptop emitting a deafening alarm that won't stop until Bill slashes the tape holding the battery and yanks it out.

## The Turning Point

Chapter 7 is the conceptual pivot of the book. Every chapter before it has been accumulating evidence of a system in crisis — incidents, missed deadlines, audit findings, capacity overload. Erik's arrival reframes all of it: the individual problems are symptoms, and the underlying disease is that IT does not understand its own work well enough to manage it.

The manufacturing plant visit makes the argument concrete. The plant floor is an analogy for IT Operations:
- **WIP on the floor = unfinished work in IT** — tickets, projects, incidents, changes in flight simultaneously
- **Job release by first-station availability = work accepted based on whoever asks first** — no constraint awareness
- **Bottleneck capacity = Brent** — and everything else in the system piles up waiting for him
- **Air freight expediting = heroic all-nighters** — the visible symptom of an invisible system problem

The Three Ways are introduced as a framework but not yet explained. They will be developed over the rest of the book.

## Management Observations

- **Erik's "four types of work" question is the book's Socratic challenge.** Like Jonah in The Goal, Erik does not lecture — he poses a question and walks away. Bill must derive the answer himself. The question is: if business projects are one type of work, what are the other three? The answer, when Bill arrives at it, will reframe how IT Operations thinks about capacity, prioritization, and the source of its chronic instability.
- **"IT is not a factory" is the wrong objection.** Bill's pushback is the standard resistance. Erik's counter is that the underlying principles — work in process accumulates, bottlenecks determine system throughput, improvements away from the constraint don't improve the system — apply regardless of whether the work is physical or informational. The medium is different; the dynamics are the same.
- **Erik's job description for Bill is precise and demands rereading.** *"Ensure the fast, predictable, and uninterrupted flow of planned work that delivers value to the business while minimizing the impact and disruption of unplanned work."* This is not a list of tasks. It is a systems definition of the VP of IT Operations role — one that makes the management of work itself, not just the execution of work, the primary responsibility.
- **The Three Ways will structure the rest of the novel.** Flow (First Way), feedback (Second Way), and continuous learning (Third Way) map directly onto the book's three-act structure: getting work flowing through the system, then building the feedback mechanisms to catch and fix problems fast, then building the organizational culture that sustains improvement over time.
