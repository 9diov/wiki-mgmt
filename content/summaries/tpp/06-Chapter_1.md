# Chapter 1

## What Happens

Bill Palmer, Director of Midrange Technology Operations at Parts Unlimited, is driving to the office when he gets an unexpected call from Laura Beck, VP of HR. She won't say what it's about over the phone.

When he arrives, he learns that Luke (CIO) and Damon (VP of IT Operations, Bill's direct boss) have both been terminated — quietly, the same morning. CEO Steve Masters has personally chosen Bill to take Damon's role: VP of IT Operations.

Bill refuses twice. Steve meets with him anyway and makes the pitch directly: Parts Unlimited is losing market share, the stock price has halved in three years, and Project Phoenix — the company's e-commerce transformation initiative, already years late — is existential. Steve needs someone reliable to keep IT running while Chris Allers (VP of Application Development) focuses on getting Phoenix shipped. Bill's record on a previous integration project is the reason Steve picked him.

Bill tries to decline again but finds himself saying "yes, sir" — ambushed by Steve's directness and the appeal to the company's four thousand employees. His mission, as Steve frames it: keep IT running like a toilet (invisible, always available, never floods the building). His constraint: nothing in his area of responsibility can distract from Phoenix.

The chapter ends with Steve handing Bill his first crisis before he has even officially started: an email from CFO Dick Landry reporting that the week's payroll run is failing and thousands of employees won't be paid by 5 PM unless it's fixed.

---

## The Situation Set Up

Parts Unlimited's dysfunction is visible from the first page:

- **IT leadership has a revolving door.** CIOs last roughly two years each — long enough to disrupt existing work, not long enough to fix anything. "CIO stands for 'Career Is Over'" is a joke Bill's peers tell daily.
- **The business is losing the technology race.** A competitor radio ad midway through Bill's drive — a service that lets customers customize cars with friends online — captures what Parts Unlimited cannot do and cannot close the gap on.
- **Phoenix is the company's entire bet.** The project has been in flight for years, is late, has Wall Street impatient, and is directly responsible for the board losing confidence in Steve. Its success is the difference between survival and the company being broken up.
- **IT Ops is a second-class function.** Bill's building needs paint. Budget requests are denied. The implicit deal: do the dirty, unglamorous work, keep your head down, survive. Bill's career strategy has been exactly that — until today.

## Management Observations

- **The promotion is a problem disguised as an opportunity.** Bill reads the situation accurately: two executives just got fired, senior IT leadership consistently fails here, and the person who accepts this role inherits the chaos their predecessors left behind. His instinct to decline is not cowardice — it is pattern recognition.
- **Steve's framing of IT is revealing.** "It should be like using the toilet" is not an insult — it is a precise articulation of what the business needs: invisible reliability. The failure mode Steve fears is not slowness or inefficiency but catastrophic visible disruption. This framing will drive every priority in the novel.
- **The first crisis arrives before the ink is dry.** Payroll failing on Bill's first day as VP establishes the tempo of the book: reactive, urgent, high-stakes. The system was already unstable before Bill touched it; he inherits both the role and the emergency.
- **Military backgrounds shape the dynamic.** Both Bill (Marine sergeant) and Steve (Army major) operate with a shared vocabulary of duty and hierarchy. When Steve says "can I count on you?", Bill hears an order he cannot refuse — not manipulation, but a cultural register he was trained to respond to.
