# Chapter 18

## What Happens

Steve convenes an IT leadership off-site in the corporate boardroom. Bill, Chris, Wes, Patty, and Erik are all present. The mood is tense — Patty barely acknowledges Bill after the resignation; Wes greets him with ostentatious sarcasm.

Steve opens by acknowledging his own responsibility for the string of failures — not just Phoenix, but the audit issues, the invoicing and inventory collapses, the SOX-404 problems. He references Erik's framing of the CEO-CIO relationship as a dysfunctional marriage, and publicly apologizes to Bill by name: *"He told me things I didn't want to hear, and I shut him down. In hindsight, he was completely right, and I was completely wrong."*

Steve then does something none of them expected: he makes himself vulnerable. He invokes Lencioni's *Five Dysfunctions of a Team* — which argues that trust requires vulnerability — and shares his background. He grew up in rural poverty in Texas, worked his way through college at a copper mine, served nine years in the Army. Right before discharge, his commanding officer told him that without exception, none of the people who served under him would want to work with him again, and that he'd win an Asshole of the Decade Award by a wide margin. He says that moment was one of the most crushing of his life — and that after three decades of deliberately rebuilding himself as a team builder, he's made the same mistake again.

He asks the room whether they're willing to build a team that can genuinely trust one another. Chris is the first to say yes. Wes and Patty nod. Everyone looks at Bill.

---

## The Team Reset

Chapter 18 has no new operational content. It is entirely the leadership reset that makes the next phase of the novel possible.

Steve's arc from Chapter 1 to Chapter 18 is a study in the conditions under which a CEO changes. The change required: personal consequences that connected directly to his own decisions (missed earnings, board pressure, outsourcing threat), a messenger outside the organizational hierarchy who could hold his feet to the fire without career risk (Erik), and a moment of public accountability in front of the people he had failed. Bill's resignation supplied the direct evidence that his management style was producing the precise opposite of what he wanted.

Erik's role throughout the novel is worth noting here. He is not Bill's mentor only — he is apparently also coaching Steve. He has access to both sides of the dysfunctional marriage. The framing he offers ("two sides of a dysfunctional marriage who both feel held hostage by the other") places responsibility on both parties, which Steve could accept in a way he couldn't accept blame from Bill directly.

## Management Observations

- **Steve's public vulnerability is load-bearing, not decorative.** The team has watched Steve override expert judgment, dismiss warnings, micromanage crises, and produce compounding failures. An apology alone is insufficient to reset that dynamic. What Steve does instead — expose the specific personal failure that he's been avoiding repeating for thirty years — creates a different kind of credibility. He's not claiming to be reformed; he's naming the pattern, acknowledging the recurrence, and asking to work on it together. That's a different ask.
- **The "Asshole of the Decade" story lands because Steve tells it on himself, not on someone else.** Vulnerability in leadership is only useful if it concerns the person speaking. Steve could have apologized and then attributed the team's dysfunction to others. Instead, he describes a personal failure, its impact, and his belief that he repeated it. The room responds — Wes's eye-roll at Chris is about defensiveness, not disbelief. Even the skeptics are paying attention.
- **The Lencioni citation is structural, not decorative.** Steve is telling the group explicitly what kind of work they're about to do: building trust through vulnerability, in the sequence that Lencioni describes. This signals that Steve has done the reading and is committing to a specific method, not just expressing good intentions. Whether he follows through is the question the next chapters will answer.
